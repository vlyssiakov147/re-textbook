# Chapter 5: Project Team Assembly

A development project is not built by a developer — it is built by a team. The developer's role is to originate the opportunity, assemble the capital, secure the approvals, and manage the process from start to finish. Everything in between — designing the building, managing the construction, obtaining the legal clearances, navigating the financing, and executing the entitlements — is done by specialized professionals who the developer selects, contracts, and coordinates.

The quality of the team is one of the most significant determinants of project outcomes. A mediocre site with an excellent team will consistently outperform an excellent site with a mediocre team. The right architect produces a design that wins approvals faster and leases up at better rents. The right construction manager keeps costs within budget and the schedule on track. The right planning consultant reads the political landscape and structures an application that builds rather than erodes municipal support. Team assembly is a strategic decision, not an administrative one.

This chapter covers who is on the development team, what each member does, how they are selected, and how their work is contracted. It is calibrated to a Construction Management delivery model, which is the approach your firm uses.

---

## 5.1 Why Team Assembly Matters

The consequences of a poor team selection decision compound over the life of a project. A construction manager who wins the job on a low fee but lacks the site supervision capacity to manage a complex high-rise will fall behind schedule in months 6 through 12, generating cost overruns that dwarf the initial fee savings. An architect who is talented but inexperienced in the specific municipality will produce a design that triggers avoidable planning objections and extends the entitlement timeline by 12 months. A planning consultant with weak relationships at a politically contentious municipality will fail to read the signals that an experienced consultant would catch early enough to adjust the strategy.

These are not hypothetical risks — they are the kinds of failures that experienced developers have lived through and that drive their emphasis on team quality over cost minimization at the selection stage.

**The Team Evolves Through the Project Lifecycle**

The project team is not assembled all at once. Different professionals are brought on at different stages depending on when their work is needed:

- **Pre-acquisition:** Planning consultant, architect (feasibility level), legal counsel, cost consultant
- **Post-acquisition, pre-application:** Full design team, traffic consultant, environmental consultant, geotechnical engineer, civil engineer
- **Entitlement period:** Planning consultant leads, full technical study team active
- **Pre-construction:** Construction manager, structural engineer deeper engagement, M&E engineers, interior designer, specifications consultant
- **Construction period:** Construction manager active, project monitor engaged, lender relationship active
- **Lease-up / closeout:** Leasing team, property manager, legal counsel for closing

Understanding this staging is important because it affects when consultant costs are incurred and how they flow through the project budget. A consultant brought on too early is paying fees before their work can be usefully applied. One brought on too late creates a bottleneck that holds up downstream work.

On condo projects, the sales and marketing team — presentation centre, broker, model suite, and digital marketing — is engaged well before construction completion, typically during the pre-sales period that precedes construction financing. That team and the pre-sales process are covered in depth in Chapter 10.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Your most direct team interactions are with the Construction Manager — from whom you collect monthly invoices, certified progress reports, and contractor declarations that form the core of the draw package — and with the Project Monitor, who reviews your draw submissions and whose report to the lender gates the advance of funds. Both relationships are operational and recurring, and the quality of those interactions has a direct effect on how smoothly monthly draws are processed. Understanding what each party needs from you — and why — is covered in depth in Section 5.3 and 5.6.

## 5.2 The Developer's Internal Team

Before any external consultant is hired, the developer must have the internal capacity to manage them. The internal development team is the hub through which all external consultants are coordinated, and whose judgment ultimately determines whether the project succeeds.

**Principal / Managing Partner**

The senior decision-maker on the project. Sets strategy, manages investor relationships, leads negotiations on land acquisition and financing, and makes the key go/no-go decisions throughout the project. On smaller development firms this person may also carry the VP of Development role.

**VP of Development / Director of Development**

Leads the day-to-day management of the entitlement and pre-construction process. Manages the planning consultant and architect relationship, attends planning meetings and community consultations, drives the design development process, and interfaces with the municipality. Responsible for the entitlement timeline and budget.

**Project Manager**

Manages the construction period execution — the construction schedule, budget, change orders, draw process, and interface with the construction manager. The project manager is the primary internal owner of the monthly draw cycle and is the construction manager's main point of contact within the developer organization.

**Senior Analyst / Analyst**

Supports the financial modelling, draw package preparation, investor reporting, budget variance tracking, and general financial administration of the project. The Senior Analyst role sits at the intersection of the project team and the financial team — close enough to the construction and procurement activity to understand what is driving costs, and responsible for communicating that information accurately to lenders and investors.

**Accounting / Finance**

Manages project banking, processes payments from the project account, tracks HST, manages capital calls, and maintains the LP capital accounts. On smaller development firms the Senior Analyst role overlaps significantly with project accounting.

> **Owner's Representative — When External Project Management is Used**
> On institutional joint ventures, or on projects where the developer's internal team is thinly staffed relative to the project's complexity, some developers engage an **Owner's Representative** — a third-party project management firm retained by the developer to manage the external consultant team on their behalf. This is distinct from the Project Monitor (who reports to the lender and focuses on cost verification) and from the CM (who manages construction execution). An owner's rep firm acts as an extension of the developer's internal team: attending design meetings, tracking entitlement milestones, managing consultant deliverables, and coordinating the pre-construction process. In Canada, firms such as Turner & Townsend, Colliers Project Leaders, and Parseghian Consulting play this role on larger or more complex projects. When an owner's rep is engaged, the Senior Analyst's reporting relationships shift slightly — the owner's rep may sit between the analyst and some external consultants, and may have their own reporting requirements that the analyst feeds into.

> **Common Problem — Understaffed Internal Teams**
> Development firms that grow quickly sometimes expand their project pipeline faster than their internal team capacity. An internal project manager managing four simultaneous projects cannot give adequate attention to any of them. Draw packages get delayed. Change orders go unreviewed. Budget variances are not caught early. The cost of understaffing the internal team is always higher than the cost of hiring the right people — it just manifests more slowly and less visibly.

## 5.3 The Construction Manager — Your Most Important External Relationship During Construction

On a Construction Management (CM) delivery model, the Construction Manager is the developer's primary field partner throughout the construction period. Understanding what the CM does, how they are paid, and how their performance affects the draw process is directly relevant to your daily work.

**What is Construction Management?**

In a Construction Management model, the CM does not take a fixed-price lump sum risk on the project. Instead, they manage the construction process on behalf of the developer for a fee — typically expressed as a percentage of total construction cost (5.5–7% is a reasonable current range for complex high-rise residential in the GTA, though fees fluctuate with market conditions and project complexity). The CM's fee covers their project management, site supervision, scheduling, procurement coordination, quality management, and reporting services.

Under this model, all trade contracts are entered into directly by the developer (or by the CM as agent for the developer), and all trade costs flow through to the developer at actual cost — hence "open book." The CM's financial exposure is limited to their fee; if trade costs exceed the budget, that exposure falls on the developer and their contingency, not the CM.

**Why Developers Use CM vs. Fixed Price**

The CM model is the dominant delivery approach for high-rise residential development in the GTA for several reasons. It gives the developer full transparency into actual costs — there is no markup hidden in a lump sum price. It allows the design to continue evolving during the construction period without the cost and friction of change order negotiations that would occur under a fixed-price contract. And it gives the developer direct control over trade selection and procurement, which allows them to leverage existing relationships and optimize for both cost and quality.

The tradeoff is that the developer carries the cost risk. A fixed-price GC takes on the risk of trade cost escalation; a CM does not. This is why cost monitoring — the draw package, budget variance reporting, and contingency management that you work on — is so critical under a CM model. The developer has no financial backstop other than their own contingency and budget discipline.

> **Key Concept — The CM Fee and What it Covers**
> The CM fee is typically calculated as a percentage of the total construction cost, excluding the CM fee itself. On the 9700 Yonge Street project, the construction cost estimate excludes the CM fee entirely — it is noted as "EXCLUDED" at Division 0 and carried separately in the proforma. On the 1156 The Queensway project, the CM fee is included as a line item in the project budget at approximately 5.5% of hard construction cost. Understanding whether the CM fee is inside or outside the hard cost estimate matters for LTC calculations, draw budget reconciliation, and investor reporting.

**General Conditions — What They Are and What They Include**

The CM fee covers the CM's management services, but it does not cover the direct costs of operating the construction site itself. Those costs — called **General Conditions** — are funded separately as direct project costs and appear as their own line item in the construction budget. A typical General Conditions budget for a 30-month GTA high-rise project includes:

- **Superintendent(s):** Full-time site supervision staff — often the single largest General Conditions cost. A senior superintendent on a complex high-rise might represent $350,000–$450,000 per year in salary and benefits.
- **Site office and trailer:** Lease or purchase of the construction site trailer, furnishings, computers, and office equipment.
- **Temporary utilities:** Electricity, water, heat, and telecommunications to the site during construction.
- **Hoisting and craning:** Tower crane rentals, hoisting coordinators, and material lifts — a significant cost on high-rise projects where vertical logistics are continuous.
- **Site security:** Fencing, hoarding, security personnel, and camera systems.
- **Safety program:** Safety officer salary, PPE, first aid stations, and mandatory safety training costs.
- **Mobilization and demobilization:** Setup and teardown of the site infrastructure at the start and end of the project.
- **Temporary protection:** Shoring of adjacent properties, protection of existing utilities, and environmental controls.

On a mid-size GTA high-rise (approximately 200–250 units, 30-month construction period), a General Conditions budget of $8M–$13M is typical, though this varies significantly by project complexity, site conditions, and market pricing. General Conditions are separate from and in addition to the CM fee and should be tracked as their own cost category in the project budget.

**Bonding Requirements**

On CMHC-insured projects and on larger conventional construction loans, the lender may require the CM or major trade contractors to provide performance bonds and labour and material (L&M) payment bonds as a condition of financing. A **performance bond** is a surety instrument under which a bonding company guarantees that it will step in to complete the contracted work if the contractor defaults. A **labour and material payment bond** (L&M bond) protects subcontractors and suppliers from non-payment by the contracting party.

Bond premiums are typically 1–2% of the bonded contract value and appear as a line item in the construction budget. The bonding company's willingness to bond a contractor — and the bond limit they will extend — is itself a meaningful signal of the contractor's financial strength and is a legitimate prequalification criterion. A CM or trade contractor that cannot obtain bonding at the required level should be treated as a risk flag, not merely a negotiating point.

**The CM's Monthly Responsibilities — What You Receive from Them**

Each draw cycle, the CM provides the development team with the documentation that forms the backbone of the draw package:

- **Monthly progress certificate** — The CM's formal certification of the value of work completed to date across all trade contracts and the CM's own fee. This is the primary document that the project monitor reviews to verify the draw request.
- **Schedule of values** — A breakdown of the total construction contract by trade and line item, showing the original budget, work completed to date, current period work, and balance to complete. This is the construction-side equivalent of the overall project cost report.
- **Change order log** — A running record of all approved and pending change orders, showing their impact on the approved construction budget.
- **Construction schedule update** — Updated project schedule showing progress against the baseline, any schedule variances, and the projected substantial completion date.
- **Statutory declarations** — Formal legal declarations from the CM confirming that all subcontractors and suppliers have been paid for the previous period and that there are no outstanding liens. Required before each draw can be advanced under the Ontario Construction Act.
- **Lien waivers** — Signed documents from major subcontractors confirming they have been paid and waiving their lien rights for the amounts paid.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Collecting and organizing these documents from the CM is a core part of assembling the monthly draw package. In practice, this means following up with the CM's project coordinator in the days before the draw submission deadline, reviewing the progress certificate against the budget to identify any unexpected variances, flagging change orders that haven't been formally approved, and ensuring the statutory declarations cover the correct period and signatories. The quality and completeness of what the CM provides directly determines how much additional work you need to do to prepare the package — a well-organized CM makes your job substantially easier.

> **Key Concept — The Statutory Holdback**
> Under the Ontario Construction Act, owners are required to retain 10% of each draw advance as a **statutory holdback**. This holdback is not released to the CM or trades until the expiry of the lien period following a declaration of **substantial performance**. Substantial performance under the Construction Act is reached when the project is at least 97% complete — or when the remaining incomplete or deficient work costs less than the lesser of $500,000 or 3% of the contract price, whichever is lower.
>
> In practical terms, this means the lender structures each advance to fund 90% of the certified draw value, with the remaining 10% held in a separate holdback account. The funded amount per draw will always be less than the CM's certified amount, and tracking the holdback balance — both the amount accumulated and the conditions for its release — is part of accurate budget and LTC management. When preparing draw reconciliations for lenders and investors, you must be precise about whether a reported figure reflects the gross certified amount or the net funded amount after holdback. Conflating the two is a common source of budget reporting errors.

**Evaluating CM Performance**

The developer's internal team — primarily the Project Manager — monitors the CM's performance on an ongoing basis. The metrics that matter are: schedule adherence (is the project tracking to the baseline?), cost control (is the CM managing subcontractor costs within budget?), change order discipline (is the CM identifying and pricing change orders accurately and promptly?), and reporting quality (are the monthly documents accurate, complete, and on time?).

A CM who consistently submits late or incomplete draw documentation, who is slow to price change orders, or whose schedule reports consistently understate delays is creating downstream problems for the developer — delayed draws, lender friction, and investor reporting gaps that ultimately reflect on the development team.

## 5.4 The Architect and Design Consultants

The architect is the lead design professional on the project and the coordinator of the full design team. Their work begins at the feasibility stage — sometimes even before site acquisition — and continues through to construction completion and occupancy.

**The Architect's Scope**

The architect's scope of work is typically broken into stages that correspond to the project lifecycle:

1. **Feasibility / Concept Design** — Initial massing studies, program testing, and site layout to support the acquisition decision and early planning strategy.
2. **Schematic Design** — Development of the design concept to a level sufficient for the planning application package — floor plans, elevations, sections, and renderings.
3. **Design Development** — Refining the design in response to planning comments and advancing it toward construction-level detail.
4. **Construction Documents** — Full working drawings and specifications from which the building will be constructed. These are the documents that form the basis of the trade contracts.
5. **Construction Administration** — During construction, the architect reviews shop drawings, responds to requests for information (RFIs), conducts site reviews, and issues certificates of payment that align with the CM's progress certifications.

The architect also coordinates the structural, mechanical, and electrical engineers — who are typically sub-consultants to the architect rather than directly contracted to the developer — and the interior designer, landscape architect, and other specialists.

**The Architect's Contract — RAIC Document Six**

In Canada, the standard form architect-client agreement is **RAIC Document Six**, published by the Royal Architectural Institute of Canada. RAIC Document Six is the Canadian equivalent of the AIA B101 contract used in U.S. practice — it defines the architect's scope of services, compensation structure, deliverables, and professional obligations. Developers working in Ontario should ensure that their architect agreements are based on or negotiated from RAIC Document Six, as it reflects the Canadian regulatory and professional context. Architects practising in Ontario must be licensed members of the **Ontario Association of Architects (OAA)**.

**The Architect's Fee**

Architectural fees on large residential projects are typically negotiated as a percentage of construction cost or as a lump sum based on an estimated scope. It is important to distinguish between the **architect's own fee** and the **total design team fee** — the latter includes the fees of sub-consultants (structural, mechanical, electrical engineers, and others) coordinated through the architect and is materially higher.

On a 200-unit purpose-built rental project, the architect's own fee might range from $1,500,000 to $2,500,000 depending on project complexity and the firm's market positioning. When sub-consultant fees are included, the total design team fee through the architect can range from $3,500,000 to $6,000,000 or more. When building the project budget, confirm with the architect whether their quoted fee includes or excludes sub-consultant fees — the distinction matters significantly for budget accuracy.

> **Common Problem — Scope Creep in Design Contracts**
> Architectural contracts specify a defined number of design iterations, submission rounds, and site review visits. When the planning process requires more resubmissions than anticipated, or when the client requests significant design changes after construction documents are complete, the additional work is legitimately out of scope and subject to additional fees. Developers who do not track design contract scope carefully can find themselves with significant unbudgeted architectural fees on complex or contentious projects.

**Sub-Consultants on the Design Team**

The following sub-consultants are typically part of the design team on a major residential project in Ontario:

| Consultant | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Structural Engineer | Foundation design, concrete structure, shoring and dewatering specifications |
| Mechanical Engineer | HVAC, plumbing, fire protection design and specifications |
| Electrical Engineer | Power distribution, lighting, fire alarm, communications design |
| Geotechnical Engineer | Soil investigation, foundation recommendations, dewatering design |
| Civil / Servicing Engineer | Site servicing, grading, stormwater management |
| Landscape Architect | Landscape design, public realm, amenity areas |
| Interior Designer | Suite finishes, common area design, material specifications |
| Code Consultant | Ontario Building Code compliance review; typically engaged when the project involves complex occupancy or code interpretations that exceed standard architectural practice |
| Specifications Consultant | Technical specification writing for trade contracts |
| Acoustic / Noise Consultant | Noise and vibration impact assessment required for planning applications and OBC compliance; particularly important for projects near rail corridors, arterials, or existing residential |
| Wind Study Consultant | Pedestrian-level wind analysis required by municipalities on tall buildings; typically a condition of planning approval on projects above approximately 20 storeys |
| Accessibility Consultant | AODA and OBC accessibility compliance review; increasingly standard on larger residential and mixed-use projects |
| Heritage Consultant | Required on or adjacent to listed or designated heritage properties; prepares the Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) submitted with the planning application |

Engineers practising in Ontario must be licensed with **Professional Engineers Ontario (PEO)**.

## 5.5 The Legal Team

The developer's legal counsel is engaged at virtually every stage of the development process. Legal work on a large development project covers a wide range of specialized practice areas, and developers typically work with law firms that have dedicated real estate development groups.

**Land Acquisition**

Real estate legal counsel manages the purchase agreement negotiation, title review, due diligence coordination, and closing of the land acquisition. They review the vendor's representations and warranties, advise on deal structure, and manage the transfer of title. On many Ontario development projects, the registered owner of the land is a **bare trustee** or **nominee corporation** — a holding entity that holds legal title on behalf of the LP as beneficial owner. This structure is standard Ontario tax and liability planning, and the legal team establishes and manages it.

**Corporate and Partnership Structure**

The legal team structures the LP/GP partnership, drafts the limited partnership agreement, and prepares the subscription documents through which LP investors commit their capital. The LP agreement — which governs the waterfall, capital call rights, LP protective provisions, and GP obligations — is one of the most important legal documents in the project and must be carefully drafted to reflect the agreed commercial terms. The capital structure and waterfall mechanics are covered in depth in Chapter 6.

**Financing**

Legal counsel negotiates and reviews the construction loan agreement, intercreditor agreements (where mezzanine debt is present), and any CMHC-related documentation. They manage the conditions precedent checklist, coordinate title insurance, and manage the closing of the financing.

**Entitlements**

Planning lawyers advise on the planning application strategy, draft Community Benefits Charge (CBC) agreements or legacy Section 37 agreements (see note below), review conditions of approval, and manage OLT proceedings if the application is appealed.

> **Key Concept — Section 37 vs. Community Benefits Charge (CBC)**
> Under the pre-2020 planning framework, municipalities could require developers to provide community benefits — Section 37 Density Bonusing — in exchange for permissions to exceed base zoning. Section 37 contributions were individually negotiated between the developer and the municipality and took the form of cash contributions, affordable housing units, public art, or community facilities.
>
> As of 2020, the **Community Benefits Charge (CBC)** framework under the Planning Act replaced Section 37 Density Bonusing for most new residential development applications. Unlike Section 37, which was negotiated project by project, the CBC is formula-based and capped at 4% of the land value at the time of the application. In practice, both instruments remain in use — the CBC applies to most new applications, while Section 37 agreements remain in effect for applications filed under the prior framework. The distinction matters for budget-setting because CBC is more predictable (formula-based) while legacy Section 37 obligations on existing approvals may be larger and are individually defined in the approval conditions. Your planning consultant and legal counsel should confirm which regime applies on any specific project.

**Construction Contracts**

The CM agreement and major trade contracts are reviewed and negotiated by legal counsel with construction law expertise. The CM agreement in particular — which defines the CM's scope, fee, performance obligations, and termination rights — is a critical document that must be carefully structured to protect the developer's interests.

**Condominium Registration**

On condominium projects, the legal team manages the preparation and registration of the **Condominium Declaration and Description** under the Condominium Act, 1998 — the legal instrument that converts the building into a registered condominium corporation and vests title to individual units in purchasers. This is a significant legal deliverable that requires coordination between the legal team, the architect, the surveyor, and the municipality. The full condominium registration process is covered in Chapter 12.

**External Accountants and Tax Advisors**

The developer's external accounting firm is a distinct engagement from the internal finance function. External accountants prepare the LP's annual audited financial statements, file the partnership's tax returns, and advise on HST structuring — including the **HST new housing rebate** applicable to condo purchasers and the **self-supply rule** applicable to purpose-built rental projects. Under the self-supply rule, a developer building a PBR project is deemed to have sold and repurchased the building at fair market value upon first rental, triggering an HST remittance obligation — this is a material cash flow item that the accounting team must plan for and the analyst must ensure is reflected in the project budget and cash flow model. The external accountants are a working relationship for the Senior Analyst: year-end audit preparation typically involves the analyst compiling information packages, responding to auditor queries, and reconciling project accounts.

## 5.6 The Financial and Lending Team — Your Primary External Interface

From a Senior Analyst perspective, the financial and lending team members are the most directly relevant external relationships. Several in particular affect your day-to-day work.

**The Construction Lender**

The construction lender — typically a Canadian chartered bank like BMO, TD, RBC, or Scotia, or a CMHC-approved lender — provides the senior debt that funds the majority of construction costs. The lender's primary interface with the developer during the construction period is through the draw process. Each month, the lender reviews the draw package, the project monitor report, and confirms that all conditions for the advance are met before releasing funds.

The lender also monitors the project through covenant compliance — the developer must maintain certain financial ratios and project metrics (LTC, cost to complete coverage, insurance) throughout the construction period. Breaches of loan covenants can trigger default provisions, so tracking covenant compliance is an ongoing obligation.

**Lender's Legal Counsel**

The construction lender has its own legal counsel — separate from and in addition to the developer's legal counsel — who reviews the draw package, confirms title searches, verifies lien search results, and issues a legal opinion confirming that conditions are satisfied before each advance is released. Lender's counsel fees are paid by the developer as a condition of the loan and appear as a line item in the soft cost budget. On a typical GTA high-rise construction loan, lender's counsel fees for each draw advance (covering a title continuation and lien search) are approximately $1,500–$3,000, in addition to ongoing review fees for contract amendments and material changes.

From an operational perspective, lender's counsel is another party the analyst coordinates with at each draw cycle — they request the title continuation and lien search, confirm receipt of the project monitor report, and issue their confirmation to the lender that legal conditions are satisfied. Delays in receiving lender's counsel confirmation are a common source of draw timing friction.

**The Lender's Conditions Checklist**

Separate from the draw package itself, the construction lender maintains a **conditions checklist** — a standing list of conditions that must be satisfied before each advance can be released. This checklist is established at loan closing and remains in effect throughout the construction period. Typical conditions include:

- Current certificates of insurance (builder's risk, CGL) naming the lender as additional insured
- Confirmation that no construction liens are registered against the property
- Confirmation that the CM agreement is in good standing and no material defaults have occurred
- Updated cost-to-complete certification from the project monitor
- Confirmation of covenant compliance (LTC, cost to complete coverage ratio)
- Receipt and approval of the project monitor's monthly report
- No material adverse change in the project or the borrower's financial position

The conditions checklist and the draw package review are parallel processes, not sequential ones. A draw package can be complete and the project monitor report can recommend approval, but the draw will not advance until every condition on the checklist is satisfied. Tracking which conditions are outstanding — and following up with the appropriate parties (insurance broker, legal counsel, CM) to close them — is part of the analyst's draw cycle responsibilities.

**The Project Monitor**

The project monitor is an independent quantity surveyor appointed by the lender — on your projects, Finnegan Marshall serves this role — to provide monthly independent certification of project progress and cost. The project monitor is the lender's eyes and ears on the project; their report is what gives the lender confidence to advance funds each month.

The project monitor's monthly report typically covers:

- Verification that work claimed in the draw package has actually been completed on site
- Review of the CM's progress certificate and schedule of values for reasonableness
- Assessment of the cost to complete against the remaining loan availability and contingency
- Comment on any budget variances, change orders, or schedule deviations
- A recommendation to the lender to approve, partially approve, or hold the draw pending additional information

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Your relationship with the project monitor is one of the most operationally important external relationships you manage. In practice, it works like this: you submit the draw package to the project monitor, they review it and come back with questions or requests for additional documentation, you respond and provide the requested items, and they issue their report to the lender recommending approval. The faster and more completely you respond to project monitor queries, the faster the draw is approved and funds are advanced. Developing a working relationship with the project monitor team — understanding what they look for, anticipating their questions before they ask them, organizing the draw package so it is easy for them to review — is one of the most practical skills you can develop at this stage of your career. The Finnegan Marshall team you work with on 9700 Yonge and 1156 The Queensway are experienced at this process — they have a clear methodology and clear expectations, and meeting those expectations consistently is what keeps draws moving smoothly.

**The Cost Consultant / Quantity Surveyor (Developer-Side)**

Separate from the lender's project monitor, developers sometimes engage their own independent quantity surveyor to prepare and update the construction cost estimate throughout the project. This is the role Finnegan Marshall played in preparing the 9700 Yonge Street construction cost estimate — providing the developer with an independent, detailed cost picture that informs the budget, the financing structure, and the lender's underwriting.

**The Mezzanine Lender**

On projects where senior debt alone is insufficient to meet the developer's equity minimization targets, a **mezzanine lender** may participate in the capital stack. Mezzanine debt sits between the senior construction loan and the LP equity — it is typically more expensive (interest rates of 10–15% or higher are common), ranks behind the senior lender in repayment priority, and is governed by an **intercreditor agreement** that defines the respective rights and remedies of the senior and mezzanine lenders relative to each other and to the borrower.

From an analyst perspective, a mezzanine lender adds a second layer of draw reporting requirements, covenant compliance tracking, and approval conditions that must be coordinated alongside the senior lender's process. The mezzanine lender typically has their own draw conditions checklist, may require their own project monitor report or cost certification, and must approve certain material project decisions (major change orders, contractor defaults) independently of the senior lender. Managing two lender relationships simultaneously is materially more complex than managing one, and the analyst must ensure that both lenders receive consistent information and that the reporting timelines are aligned.

**The CMHC Correspondent and MLI Select**

On CMHC-insured deals, the lender typically has a designated CMHC correspondent — a team member who manages the CMHC application process, tracks conditions, and coordinates between the developer, the lender, and CMHC. The dominant CMHC insured lending product for purpose-built rental in the current market is **MLI Select**, which offers construction-to-permanent financing at favorable loan-to-cost ratios (up to 95% in some qualifying cases) and extended amortization periods (up to 50 years) in exchange for meeting minimum scoring thresholds across three criteria: affordability (a proportion of units at below-market rents), accessibility (units meeting accessibility design standards), and energy efficiency (building energy performance targets). The CMHC correspondent manages the MLI Select scoring process, tracks conditions throughout the construction period, and coordinates the transition from construction to permanent loan at occupancy. Understanding the MLI Select timeline and conditions is the correspondent's primary responsibility, but the developer team must be responsive to CMHC's information requests — delays in providing required documentation can hold up the permanent loan conversion and affect the project's exit financing.

**The Lender's Appraiser**

The construction lender requires an independent appraisal as a condition of financing — this appraisal establishes the as-stabilized value of the completed project, which governs the LTAV (Loan to As-stabilized Value) covenant and forms part of the lender's underwriting. In Canada, lenders require appraisals from appraisers holding the **AACI designation (Accredited Appraiser Canadian Institute)**, granted by the Appraisal Institute of Canada. The AACI is the Canadian equivalent of the MAI designation (Member, Appraisal Institute) used in U.S. practice. The appraisal is prepared at the lender's instruction and cost (though often reimbursed by the developer under the loan conditions) and is updated periodically throughout the construction period if market conditions change materially.

## 5.7 The Planning and Entitlement Team

The planning and entitlement team is responsible for navigating the approvals process described in Chapter 4. At the senior analyst level your exposure to this team is limited, but understanding who is on it and what they do provides important context for how the pre-construction period works.

**Planning Consultant**

The planning consultant — typically a registered professional planner (RPP) at a firm specializing in development approvals — leads the entitlement strategy and manages the municipal planning process. They prepare or supervise the preparation of the Planning Justification Report, manage the pre-consultation process, attend planning committee meetings, interface with municipal planning staff, and advise on community engagement strategy.

The planning consultant's value is a combination of technical planning knowledge and municipal relationship capital. An experienced consultant who has worked extensively in a specific municipality understands the planning department's priorities, knows the staff, and can read political signals that a less experienced firm would miss.

**Urban Designer**

Works closely with the architect to develop the Urban Design Brief and to ensure the project's massing, character, and public realm interface are positioned to win planning support. On complex or high-profile projects, the urban designer may be a separate engagement from the architect; on others, the architect performs this function.

**Transportation Consultant**

Prepares the Traffic Impact Study and, where required, the Transportation Demand Management plan. Interfaces with the municipal transportation engineering department during the review process. On projects near transit nodes or on arterial roads, the transportation study is typically a major item of scrutiny.

**Heritage Planner**

On projects involving listed or designated heritage properties — or on sites adjacent to such properties — a **Heritage Planner** prepares the Heritage Impact Assessment (HIA) required by the municipality. The HIA documents the heritage attributes of the affected property, assesses the impact of the proposed development on those attributes, and proposes conservation or mitigation measures. In Toronto and many other GTA municipalities, a satisfactory HIA is a mandatory submission requirement for any planning application that triggers heritage considerations. Heritage-related planning conditions can impose design constraints (retention of facades, specific material requirements) that affect both the architect's scope and the construction budget.

**Environmental and Geotechnical Consultants**

As covered in Chapter 2, these consultants are engaged during due diligence and carry their work forward into the planning and construction periods. The geotechnical consultant's dewatering and shoring designs are key inputs into the construction documentation.

**Property Manager — Early Engagement**

On purpose-built rental projects, the property manager is increasingly engaged during the design development stage — well before construction begins — rather than only at lease-up. A property manager brought in during design can review suite layouts for operational efficiency, flag common area configurations that generate disproportionate maintenance burdens, advise on amenity programming relative to the target tenant profile, and input into building systems specifications from an operational cost perspective. Their early input prevents design decisions that look attractive on paper but create operational cost bleed over the life of the building.

For PBR projects in the GTA, property management firms active in the market include Greenwin, MetCap Living, Osgoode Properties, and Minto Apartment REIT. On projects developed for institutional joint venture partners, the JV partner may specify their preferred property manager as a condition of the partnership. The property management agreement, fee structure, and pre-opening responsibilities are typically finalized during the pre-construction period and are a soft cost item in the project budget.

## 5.8 Procurement — How Consultants and Contractors are Selected

Selecting the right team members through a rigorous procurement process is how developers manage team quality risk. The approach varies depending on the type of engagement, the relationship history between the developer and the firm, and the strategic importance of the role.

**Sole Source Selection**

For relationships where the developer has an established track record with a specific firm — a trusted planning consultant, an architect who has successfully designed multiple projects for the firm, a construction manager who has built their last three projects — the developer may engage that firm directly without a competitive process. This is called sole source selection.

Sole source selection is appropriate when the relationship value — established trust, shared project history, proven performance — outweighs the potential cost savings of a competitive process. It is most defensible for highly relationship-dependent roles like planning consultants and architects, and less defensible for commodity-like services where fee variation is significant and performance is easier to benchmark.

**Request for Proposal (RFP)**

For new relationships or for roles where the developer wants to test the market, a formal Request for Proposal process is used. The RFP is issued to a shortlist of qualified firms — typically 3-5 — and requests a written proposal covering the firm's understanding of the project, their proposed approach and team, their relevant experience, their proposed fee, and their availability.

A well-structured RFP evaluation considers several dimensions beyond fee: relevant project experience, proposed team quality, approach and methodology, reference checks, and cultural fit with the developer organization. Selecting purely on the basis of lowest fee is a reliable way to assemble a mediocre team.

> **Key Concept — The CM RFP Process**
> The Construction Manager RFP is one of the most consequential procurement decisions on a development project. The CM will manage hundreds of millions of dollars of construction work and will be the developer's primary field partner for 2-4 years. A typical CM RFP for a major high-rise project asks for: the proposed project team (superintendent, project manager, site coordinator), the CM's experience on comparable projects, their proposed preconstruction services scope, their fee proposal (percentage of construction cost), their general conditions budget proposal, their proposed insurance coverage, and their approach to schedule management, cost control, and reporting.

**Delivery Models — CM, Fixed-Price GC, and Design-Build**

This chapter is calibrated to the Construction Management model, which is the approach your firm uses for high-rise residential development. For context, two other delivery models are commonly encountered in the broader Ontario market:

A **Fixed-Price General Contractor (GC / Stipulated Price)** takes a lump-sum contract covering all construction work, bearing the risk of trade cost overruns. The developer receives cost certainty on the contract price but loses transparency into actual trade costs, and design changes after contract execution are subject to costly change order negotiations. This model is more appropriate when the design is substantially complete before procurement — a condition rarely achieved on complex high-rise projects.

**Design-Build** is a third model under which a single entity provides both design and construction services to the developer's performance specifications. The design-build contractor subcontracts the design work and delivers the completed building at a fixed price. Design-build offers a single point of accountability and typically the fastest delivery timeline, but the developer sacrifices meaningful design control and the ability to evolve the program during design development. Design-build is common in Ontario's industrial, institutional, and light commercial sectors but is rarely used for high-rise residential development where design quality, planning approval sensitivity, and phased design evolution make the CM model significantly more practical.

**Prequalification**

For construction trade contracts — the subtrades managed by the CM — a prequalification process is often used to establish a qualified bidder list before the formal tender. Prequalification screens trades on the basis of financial capacity, bonding capacity, relevant experience, safety record, and reference checks. Only prequalified firms are invited to bid.

**Competitive Tender**

Trade contracts under the CM model are typically procured through competitive tender — multiple qualified firms submit priced bids on a defined scope of work, and the contract is awarded to the lowest compliant bidder. The CM manages this process, reviews the bids, and makes a recommendation to the developer. On significant trade packages (concrete, mechanical, electrical), the developer may want to review the bid results and the CM's recommendation before the contract is awarded.

**Worked Example — Evaluating a CM Proposal:** Your firm is reviewing two CM proposals for a 350-unit rental tower. Firm A proposes a CM fee of 5.0% on a projected construction cost of $130,000,000 — a fee of $6,500,000 — with a general conditions budget of $11,200,000 and a proposed superintendent with 18 years of high-rise residential experience. Firm B proposes a CM fee of 4.5% — a fee of $5,850,000, saving $650,000 — with a general conditions budget of $10,400,000, but their proposed superintendent has 7 years of experience, primarily on mid-rise wood frame construction. The $650,000 fee saving on Firm B represents 0.5% of the construction cost. A single schedule delay of 3 months caused by inadequate site supervision would cost approximately $1,875,000 in additional construction loan interest alone — nearly three times the fee saving. Experience and team quality, not fee minimization, should drive the selection.

## 5.9 Contracts and Scope of Work

Every team member relationship is governed by a contract. Understanding the key elements of the contracts that frame your working relationships — particularly the CM agreement — helps you understand what obligations each party has and what recourse exists when performance falls short.

**The CM Agreement**

The Construction Management Agreement defines the legal relationship between the developer and the CM. Key provisions include:

- **Scope of services** — What the CM is responsible for, including preconstruction services (estimating, scheduling, procurement planning, subcontractor prequalification) and construction services (site management, trade coordination, quality management, reporting).
- **Fee** — The CM's compensation, typically expressed as a percentage of total construction cost. Whether the fee applies to the CM's own general conditions costs is an important point of negotiation — some agreements include general conditions in the fee basis, others exclude them.
- **General conditions** — The costs of running the site office — superintendent salaries, site trailer, temporary utilities, safety equipment, hoisting — which are funded separately from the CM fee as direct project costs.
- **Performance obligations** — Schedule adherence, reporting requirements, change order management procedures, and quality management obligations.
- **Change order authority** — The dollar threshold below which the CM can approve changes without developer authorization, and the process for approvals above that threshold.
- **Termination** — Grounds on which the developer can terminate the CM (for cause and for convenience) and the process and consequences of termination.

**CCDC 5A and CCDC 5B — The Two CM Contract Forms**

The Canadian Construction Documents Committee (CCDC) publishes two standard Construction Management contract forms that differ fundamentally in how risk is allocated between the developer and the CM.

**CCDC 5A — CM as Agent**

CCDC 5A is the standard form for Construction Management where the CM acts as the developer's agent. Under this form, all trade contracts are between the developer and the trade contractors directly — the CM manages the trades on the developer's behalf but does not hold the contracts. The developer has full contractual privity with every trade and bears all trade cost risk. This is the most common CM contract form used on GTA high-rise residential projects, and the open-book cost transparency it provides is one of its primary advantages. You will see CCDC 5A referenced in construction documentation, draw reports, and lender conditions on most projects your firm works on.

**CCDC 5B — CM as Constructor**

CCDC 5B is used when the CM takes on a more contractor-like role — holding the trade contracts directly rather than as the developer's agent. Under this form, the trade contracts are between the CM and the subtrades, not between the developer and the subtrades. The developer contracts only with the CM, who in turn contracts with and manages the trades.

The key differences from 5A are:

| Item | CCDC 5A (CM as Agent) | CCDC 5B (CM as Constructor) |
|------|----------------------|-----------------------------|
| Trade contracts held by | Developer | CM |
| Developer privity with trades | Direct | Indirect — through CM |
| CM cost risk | None — fees only | Limited — CM may carry some cost risk on trade packages |
| Lien exposure | Developer directly exposed | CM is the contracting party — developer has one step of practical separation |
| Transparency | Full open-book | Open-book but CM has more control over trade pricing |
| Common use case | Large complex projects where developer wants full control | Projects where developer wants a single point of responsibility |

**When CCDC 5B is Used**

CCDC 5B is chosen when the developer wants a single point of contractual responsibility for the construction work — one party to hold accountable for trade performance rather than managing direct relationships with dozens of trade contractors. It is also sometimes used when the developer has less internal construction management capacity and wants the CM to carry more of the day-to-day trade contract administration burden.

The lien exposure difference is particularly relevant in Ontario. Under CCDC 5A, trade contractors and suppliers have direct lien rights against the owner's interest in the property. Under CCDC 5B, the CM is the contracting party with the trades, which provides the developer one additional layer of practical separation from direct trade lien claims — however, this does not eliminate lien risk. Under the Ontario Construction Act, lien remedies can still reach the owner's interest in the property regardless of the contractual structure, and the statutory holdback obligation applies to the owner in both cases. The practical difference is in the contractual relationships and the parties' direct obligations to each other — not in the ultimate availability of the lien remedy.

> **Common Problem — Assuming 5A and 5B are Interchangeable**
> The choice between CCDC 5A and 5B is a strategic decision that affects the entire risk and accountability structure of the construction period. Switching from 5A to 5B mid-project, or selecting the wrong form for the project context, creates ambiguity about who holds the trade contracts and who is responsible for trade performance. This matters acutely in a dispute scenario — if a trade contractor stops work or submits a lien claim, who they have a contract with determines who they can pursue and through what mechanism. The CM agreement form should be selected deliberately, with legal counsel involved, before the project reaches the procurement stage.

**CCDC 2 — Stipulated Price Contract**

Alongside CCDC 5A and 5B, the other CCDC contract form developers regularly encounter is **CCDC 2 — Stipulated Price Contract**, which is the standard form for a fixed-price general contractor relationship in Canada. Under CCDC 2, the GC agrees to complete the defined scope of work for a set price, bearing the risk of cost overruns on the trade packages they manage. CCDC 2 is the Canadian equivalent of the AIA A101 contract used in U.S. practice and is the contract form used when a developer chooses fixed-price GC delivery rather than the CM model. Even if your firm does not use CCDC 2 on its own projects, you will encounter references to it in industry documents, lender precedents, and conversations with trade contractors.

> **Key Concept — Why the Contract Scope Matters for Draw Administration**
> The CM's reporting obligations under the CM agreement — what documents they must produce, how frequently, and to what standard — are what you rely on every draw cycle. If the CM agreement does not specifically require monthly statutory declarations, or does not define the format and timing of the progress certificate, the CM has no contractual obligation to provide them on your timeline. In practice, experienced development teams negotiate detailed reporting requirements into the CM agreement precisely because the draw cycle depends on them. When you review the CM agreement early in a project, pay attention to the section covering reporting — those provisions define what you can ask for and when.

**Project Insurance Program**

Every development project requires a coordinated insurance program. Understanding the key insurance coverages is important because insurance certificates are part of the draw package, and insurance compliance is a standing lender condition. The main coverages on a GTA high-rise project are:

- **Builder's Risk Insurance (Course of Construction):** Covers damage to the structure under construction from fire, weather, theft, vandalism, and other perils. Typically carried by the developer (or the CM on their behalf), naming the lender as additional insured. Premium is based on total construction value and appears as a line item in the soft cost budget.
- **Commercial General Liability (CGL):** Carried by the CM and all major trade contractors. Covers bodily injury and property damage claims arising from construction activities. Minimum coverage limits are specified in the CM agreement and in each trade contract. Certificates of insurance from the CM and major trades are collected as part of the pre-construction compliance package and updated annually.
- **Errors and Omissions (E&O) / Professional Liability:** Carried by design professionals (architect, engineers, planning consultant). Protects the developer in the event of a design error or professional negligence claim. Minimum coverage limits are negotiated in the consultant agreements.
- **Directors and Officers (D&O) / General Partner Liability:** Carried by the GP entity, protecting the principals from claims by LP investors arising from management decisions. Required by most LP subscription agreements.
- **Owner Controlled Insurance Program (OCIP):** On larger projects, some developers implement an OCIP — a single coordinated liability policy covering the developer, CM, and all trades under one program. An OCIP simplifies certificate collection and can reduce overall insurance cost but requires significant upfront premium outlay and program administration.

**Consultant Agreements**

Consultant agreements — with the architect, engineers, and planning consultants — define scope, fee, schedule, and deliverables. Key provisions relevant to project administration include:

- **Deliverables and milestones** — What the consultant must produce and when. Tied to fee payment schedules.
- **Reimbursable expenses** — Travel, printing, permit fees, and other out-of-pocket costs that are reimbursed on top of the base fee.
- **Additional services** — Scope changes that are legitimately outside the original agreement and subject to additional fees. Understanding what is in scope vs. additional services prevents disputes.
- **Professional liability (E&O) insurance** — The consultant's errors and omissions insurance coverage, which the developer relies on in the event of a design error that causes loss.

## 5.11 Regulatory Licensing — HCRA and Tarion

Before a developer can legally build and sell new homes in Ontario, the firm and its principals must hold the appropriate licences issued by the Home Construction Regulatory Authority (HCRA). This is not a planning approval or a construction permit — it is a business licence that governs who is legally permitted to operate as a builder or vendor of new homes in the province. It belongs in a chapter on team assembly because it is a foundational credential that the development firm must have in place before any project involving new home sales can proceed.

**The Home Construction Regulatory Authority (HCRA)**

The HCRA is the regulatory body established under the New Home Construction Licensing Act, 2017 (NHCLA) to license and regulate builders and vendors of new homes in Ontario. It replaced the previous self-regulatory structure — under which Tarion performed both warranty administration and builder licensing functions — and gave the province direct oversight over the new home construction industry. HCRA and Tarion were formally separated in 2021 as distinct organizations with distinct mandates: HCRA licenses and regulates builders and vendors; Tarion administers the statutory new home warranty program.

Two distinct licences are required to develop and sell new condominiums or freehold homes in Ontario:

**Builder Licence** — Required to construct new homes. The builder licence is held by the entity that physically constructs the building — in most cases the development company or a related entity. The licence requires the applicant to demonstrate financial solvency, provide details of the proposed officers and directors, and confirm that key personnel meet the HCRA's experience requirements.

**Vendor Licence** — Required to sell new homes to purchasers. The vendor licence is held by the entity that enters into agreements of purchase and sale with condo buyers. On many development projects, the builder and vendor are the same entity or related entities, but both licences must be held.

> **Key Concept — Licence Conditions and Principal Disclosure**
> HCRA licences are not issued to anonymous companies — they require disclosure of the principals (officers and directors) of the applicant entity. Each principal is individually assessed for suitability, including their history with prior development projects, any prior regulatory findings, and their financial background. A principal with a history of project failures, unresolved Tarion claims, or regulatory violations at a prior development firm may affect the new firm's ability to obtain or renew its licence. This is why the composition of the development firm's principals is not just a governance matter — it has direct regulatory implications.

**Licence Renewal and Ongoing Compliance**

HCRA licences must be renewed annually. Renewal requires submission of updated financial information, disclosure of any changes in principals, and confirmation of ongoing compliance with the NHCLA. The HCRA also conducts audits and investigations, and has the power to impose conditions on licences, suspend licences, or revoke them for non-compliance.

> **Common Problem — Letting Licences Lapse**
> A lapsed builder or vendor licence is not a minor administrative issue — it means the development firm is legally prohibited from constructing or selling new homes until the licence is reinstated. On an active project with purchase agreements outstanding, a lapsed vendor licence creates significant legal exposure with purchasers and potential Tarion complications. Licence renewal dates should be tracked as a standing item in the firm's compliance calendar.

**Tarion — New Home Warranty Enrolment**

Tarion administers the statutory new home warranty program in Ontario under the Ontario New Home Warranties Plan Act. Every new home sold in Ontario — including every condominium unit — must be enrolled with Tarion before an agreement of purchase and sale can be executed with a purchaser.

Tarion enrolment involves registering the project with Tarion, paying per-unit enrolment fees, and ensuring the development meets Tarion's construction standards throughout the build. In exchange, purchasers receive statutory warranty coverage: one year on defects in work and materials, two years on electrical, plumbing, heating, and building envelope systems, and seven years on major structural defects.

Tarion also administers deposit protection for condo purchasers — deposits paid on new condo purchases are protected up to defined limits in the event the builder fails to complete the project or return deposits.

It is important to note that Tarion enrolment requirements apply specifically to homes sold to individual purchasers. **Purpose-built rental projects that are not sold to the public** — and are instead retained by the developer as a rental asset or sold in a single institutional transaction — are generally not subject to Tarion enrolment requirements, since there are no individual home purchases to warranty. Developers operating in both the condo and PBR space should confirm with legal counsel whether and to what extent HCRA licensing and Tarion enrolment requirements apply on a given project.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> HCRA licensing and Tarion enrolment sit outside the financial administration work you do day-to-day — they are managed by the firm's principal and legal counsel. However, understanding that these licences exist and what they cover helps you understand the firm's regulatory context. If you are ever asked to compile financial information for an HCRA renewal submission or to track Tarion enrolment fees in the project budget, knowing what those items are and why they exist makes you more effective in that task. Tarion enrolment fees are a real project cost that should appear as a line item in the soft cost budget. The fee is calculated per unit based on a schedule that is tiered by purchase price — the total project cost depends on unit mix and pricing, and should be calculated using the current Tarion fee schedule rather than estimated by analogy to other projects. On a 200-unit condo project, fees can be substantial and should be confirmed with legal counsel during budget preparation.

The full treatment of the Tarion warranty process, condominium registration under the Condominium Act, the HCRA's role at occupancy, and the interim occupancy period is covered in Chapter 12 — Certificate of Occupancy & Closeout.

350-unit purpose-built rental tower. Projected construction cost: $130,000,000. Projected construction period: 30 months. Construction loan balance at peak draw: $110,000,000 at 7.5% per annum.

**Proposal Comparison**

| Reference Projects | 3 comparable towers built | 1 comparable tower, 3 mid-rise |
| Schedule Methodology | Detailed CPM schedule provided | General phasing diagram only |
| Reporting Package | Detailed monthly template included | Standard industry package |

**Fee Difference Analysis**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| CM fee saving (Firm B vs A) | $650,000 |
| General conditions saving (Firm B vs A) | $800,000 |
| Total cost saving — Firm B | $1,450,000 |
| As % of total construction cost | 1.1% |

**Risk-Adjusted Cost of Choosing Firm B**

The $1,450,000 saving must be weighed against the risk of underperformance from a less experienced team. Consider three scenarios:

| Risk Scenario | Probability | Cost Impact | Expected Value |
|---------------|------------|------------|----------------|
| No performance issue | 40% | $0 | $0 |
| 3-month schedule delay (interest + acceleration) | 35% | $2,800,000 | $980,000 |
| 6-month delay + cost overruns requiring additional contingency draw | 25% | $5,500,000 | $1,375,000 |
| **Expected cost of underperformance risk** | | | **$2,355,000** |

The expected cost of the underperformance risk ($2,355,000) exceeds the total saving from choosing Firm B ($1,450,000) by $905,000. On a risk-adjusted basis, Firm A is the better choice despite the higher fee.

Note also that this analysis captures only the direct financial costs of a delay scenario. A CM-driven schedule slip carries an additional cost not captured in the interest calculation: lender confidence. A project that misses milestone dates triggers increased lender scrutiny, may require the developer to explain variances to investors, and can affect the developer's ability to access future financings with the same lender on favorable terms. These relationship costs are real but difficult to quantify — they reinforce the conclusion that the fee saving from Firm B is not worth the risk.

**Recommendation**

Select Firm A. The 18-year superintendent and proven track record on comparable high-rise projects reduces the probability of the schedule and cost scenarios that create the most significant financial exposure. The fee premium of $1,450,000 over Firm B represents 1.1% of construction cost — a reasonable price for experienced, proven site leadership on a $130M project with a $110M construction loan where a 3-month delay costs nearly $2M in interest alone.

If budget pressure requires fee negotiation, approach Firm A to reduce the general conditions budget by $300,000-$500,000 (consolidating some site staffing) rather than reducing the CM fee percentage, which directly affects their margin and may reduce the quality of the management team they deploy.

## AE-5.8 — Running a Consultant RFP: Planning Consultant Selection

This exercise walks through the RFP process for selecting a planning consultant on a new mixed-use intensification project in Vaughan.

**Project Context**

New OPA/ZBA application for a 22-storey mixed-use rental tower in Vaughan. Site is in a High-Rise Mixed Use designation. Application complexity is moderate — OPA may not be required, ZBA is certain. Community sensitivity is moderate — the site is near established residential. Timeline pressure is high — the developer wants to submit within 6 months.

**RFP Shortlist**

Three planning consultant firms are shortlisted based on their known presence in Vaughan development approvals:

| Criterion | Firm X | Firm Y | Firm Z |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|
| Vaughan-specific experience | Extensive — 8 approvals in last 3 years | Moderate — 3 approvals, 2 pending | Limited — 1 approval, primarily Toronto practice |
| Proposed lead planner | Principal, 20 yrs experience | Associate, 8 yrs experience | Principal, 15 yrs (Toronto-focused) |
| Proposed fee | $185,000 | $145,000 | $210,000 |
| Timeline to submission | 5 months | 6 months | 7 months |
| Approach to community engagement | Proactive — early stakeholder meetings proposed | Reactive — respond to formal process only | Proactive — detailed community strategy |
| References from Vaughan projects | Strong — 3 recent client references | Limited — 1 reference | None for Vaughan |

**Evaluation Scoring (weighted)**

| Criterion | Weight | Firm X | Firm Y | Firm Z |
|-----------|--------|--------|--------|--------|
| Local experience & relationships | 35% | 9/10 | 6/10 | 4/10 |
| Proposed team quality | 25% | 9/10 | 6/10 | 8/10 |
| Fee | 20% | 7/10 | 9/10 | 5/10 |
| Timeline | 15% | 9/10 | 7/10 | 5/10 |
| Community engagement approach | 5% | 8/10 | 5/10 | 8/10 |
| **Weighted Score** | | **8.40** | **6.65** | **5.40** |

**Recommendation**

Select Firm X at $185,000. The $40,000 premium over Firm Y is justified by Firm X's significantly stronger Vaughan-specific experience and relationships, the higher seniority of the proposed lead planner, and the faster proposed timeline. A 1-month acceleration in the submission timeline alone — meeting the developer's 5-month target vs. Firm Y's 6-month target — reduces carrying costs by approximately $197,500 on a $31.5M land value at 7.5%. The cost of the better consultant is recovered in the first month of time saved.

Once selected, the next step is fee negotiation. It is standard practice to negotiate the winning consultant's fee downward from their proposal — typically seeking a 5–10% reduction while leaving the scope and team unchanged. Key points to address in the planning consultant agreement include: whether the fee is structured as a retainer or milestone-based (milestone-based is preferable as it aligns payment with deliverables), what specifically constitutes "additional services" that trigger extra fees beyond the base scope (resubmissions beyond an agreed number, OLT proceedings, additional public meetings), and who owns the work product if the developer terminates the engagement.

## Chapter Summary

The development team is the developer's primary tool for converting a site and a capital stack into a completed building. Getting team selection right — choosing the construction manager with the right experience, the planning consultant with the right municipal relationships, the architect with the right design sensibility for the product type — has more impact on project outcomes than almost any other decision in the development process. For a Senior Analyst whose daily work involves the construction manager and the project monitor, understanding what those parties are obligated to provide, why they provide it, and what the consequences are when they fall short gives you the context to do your own work more effectively and to identify problems before they escalate into project issues.

Team assembly is not glamorous work — it does not involve creative design decisions or bold financial structuring — but it is foundational to everything that follows. The statutory holdback, the lender's conditions checklist, the bonding requirements, the insurance program, the CCDC contract forms — these are the mechanics that govern how money flows through the project and how risk is allocated among the parties. Understanding them is part of understanding how a development project actually works.

And before any of it begins on a condo project, the regulatory foundation must be in place: the HCRA licences that authorize the firm to build and sell, and the Tarion enrolments that protect the buyers who will eventually purchase what the team builds. On a purpose-built rental project, the licensing obligations differ — confirm with legal counsel — but the principle is the same: the team cannot execute until the regulatory platform is established.
