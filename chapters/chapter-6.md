## 6.1 The Role of Design in Real Estate Development

Design is not decoration. In real estate development, design is a financial instrument. Every decision made on paper during the design phase — floor plate size, ceiling heights, unit mix, corridor width, cladding material, mechanical room placement — becomes a cost or a revenue driver once construction begins. A developer who treats design purely as an aesthetic exercise is a developer who loses control of their budget.

The design process sits at the intersection of three competing pressures that every development team must constantly balance:

**Regulatory compliance** — the building must satisfy zoning bylaws, the Ontario Building Code, accessibility standards, fire codes, and any site-specific conditions of approval from the entitlement process. These are non-negotiable constraints that set the outer boundaries of what can be built.

**Market positioning** — the building must satisfy the expectations of its end users, whether those are condo purchasers, rental tenants, retail operators, or office occupants. Design quality directly affects achievable rents and sale prices, absorption rates, and long-term asset value. Under-designing relative to the market leaves money on the table. Over-designing relative to what the market will pay destroys feasibility.

**Construction cost** — every design decision has a cost implication. The relationship between design and cost is not linear. A small change in specification — moving from a standard window to a curtainwall system, for example — can increase cost by a factor of three or four for that element. The design team and the cost consultant must work together continuously to ensure design ambition stays within the cost envelope established in the feasibility study.

The developer's job is to hold these three pressures in productive tension throughout the entire design process, from the first sketch to the final set of construction documents. When one pressure dominates at the expense of the others, the project suffers — either through regulatory rejection, market underperformance, or budget overrun.

### The Design Process as a Funnel

Think of the design process as a funnel. At the top, the aperture is wide — almost anything is possible in concept. As the project moves through successive design stages, decisions get made, options get eliminated, and the funnel narrows. By the time a full set of construction documents is issued, virtually every decision has been locked in and the cost of changing anything increases dramatically.

This funnel dynamic has a critical implication: **the earlier a problem is identified in the design process, the cheaper it is to fix.** A change made during schematic design costs a fraction of what the same change costs during design development, which in turn costs a fraction of what it costs during construction documents, which costs a tiny fraction of what it costs in the field during construction.

This is why experienced developers invest heavily in design review at early stages. Spending $50,000 on an intensive schematic design review process can prevent a $2,000,000 change order six months later.

## 6.2 The Design Team — Who Does What

A development project of any meaningful scale involves a large, multi-disciplinary design team. Understanding who each consultant is, what their scope covers, and how they interact with each other is essential for anyone managing the financial side of a project.

### The Architect — Team Lead

The architect is the lead design professional on almost every project. Their scope covers the overall building design, spatial planning, building envelope, interior layouts, and coordination of all other design consultants. The architect produces the drawings that all other consultants respond to, and is responsible for ensuring the final design package is coherent and code-compliant.

In Ontario, the architect of record must be licensed by the Ontario Association of Architects (OAA). The OAA licence is what the building department requires for an architect of record to seal and stamp permit drawings submitted to the municipality. A foreign firm — including U.S. practices — cannot seal Ontario permit drawings without either obtaining OAA licensure or entering a formal collaboration agreement with an OAA-licensed firm. For complex projects, the developer may engage a separate design architect for creative vision and an architect of record for technical execution — a common arrangement on signature projects where a high-profile firm is engaged for design but lacks the local code expertise to carry the project through permit.

The architect-owner contract in Canada is typically structured on the **Royal Architectural Institute of Canada (RAIC) standard form agreement** — specifically the RAIC Client and Architect Agreement — or on the OAA's equivalent standard form. These are the Canadian equivalents of the AIA contract documents referenced in U.S. textbooks. Developers should be aware that, like the AIA documents in the U.S., the RAIC standard form was written from the architect's perspective; developers frequently negotiate modifications.

The architect's fee is typically structured as a percentage of **hard construction cost** — commonly 4% to 7% for a mid-rise residential project, with the percentage decreasing as project size increases. Applying this percentage to total project cost (which includes land, municipal fees, financing, and other soft costs) would significantly overstate the fee; the basis is construction cost only. On a typical GTA mid-rise, architectural fees in absolute dollar terms commonly range from $800,000 to over $2,000,000 depending on project scale and complexity. Fees are earned across design stages, with payment milestones tied to stage completions.

### Structural Engineer

The structural engineer designs the building's structure — the foundation system, floor plates, columns, shear walls, and any specialized structural elements. On a concrete high-rise, the structural engineer's decisions about floor plate thickness, column spacing, and core location have enormous implications for rentable area, construction cost, and construction schedule.

The structural engineer works closely with the geotechnical engineer (who characterizes the soil and recommends foundation type) and the architect (whose floor layouts must work within the structural grid). Structural fees are typically 1% to 2% of hard construction cost.

### Mechanical, Electrical & Plumbing (MEP) Engineers

Mechanical, electrical, and plumbing are often engaged as a single multi-discipline firm on residential projects, or as separate consultants on larger or more complex projects. Their scope includes:

- **Mechanical** — HVAC systems, ventilation, geothermal systems where applicable, fire suppression
- **Electrical** — power distribution, lighting, emergency systems, EV charging infrastructure
- **Plumbing** — domestic water, sanitary drainage, gas

MEP systems are among the most expensive components of a building. As noted in the 9700 Yonge Street cost estimate, mechanical alone (plumbing, geothermal, ventilation) runs $67,000–$68,000 per unit on a purpose-built rental project — one of the largest single line items in the hard cost budget. MEP decisions made during design directly set that cost.

### Other Key Consultants

**Civil Engineer** — site grading, servicing connections (water, sewer, storm), road works, and site drainage. Critical for projects with complex servicing requirements or those requiring municipal infrastructure upgrades.

**Landscape Architect** — outdoor amenity areas, green roofs, public realm improvements, and any privately owned publicly accessible spaces (POPS) required as a condition of approval.

**Interior Designer** — suite finishes, lobby and amenity design, material specifications. On condo projects, interior design has a direct impact on purchaser perception and achievable price per square foot. On rental projects, it drives tenant attraction and retention.

**Sustainability / Green Building Consultant** — energy modelling, LEED certification, Toronto Green Standard (TGS) compliance. Increasingly a required member of the team rather than an optional one, particularly in Toronto where TGS compliance is a condition of Site Plan Approval.

**Building Envelope Consultant** — reviews the cladding, window, and waterproofing design for thermal performance, water penetration resistance, and durability compliance. This role is common on mid-rise and high-rise projects and is increasingly required by construction lenders and insurers as a condition of financing. The envelope consultant's engagement typically runs from design development through construction administration.

**Acoustics Consultant** — particularly important for mixed-use projects where residential units sit above retail or mechanical equipment, and for projects near transit corridors or highways.

**Code Consultant** — on complex projects, a dedicated building code consultant reviews the design for compliance and manages the alternative solution process where the design departs from prescriptive code requirements.

**Traffic Engineer** — required for projects above a certain size to model traffic impacts, design access and egress, and satisfy municipal transportation department requirements.

**Wind / Pedestrian Wind Consultant** — required by the City of Toronto for high-rise projects under the Tall Building Design Guidelines. A pedestrian wind analysis assesses the impact of the proposed building on wind conditions at grade and on adjacent properties. Poor wind performance at grade can require modifications to the tower form or ground-level treatment as a condition of Site Plan Approval.

**Elevator Consultant** — designs the vertical transportation strategy, specifies elevator count and capacity, and manages elevator procurement. Elevator count and placement is a significant driver of both construction cost and long-term operating cost.

**Soils, Environmental & Hydrological Consultant** — Phase I and Phase II environmental site assessments, geotechnical investigation, and hydrogeological reporting. This role is typically engaged during site due diligence (covered in the site acquisition chapter) but remains active through design and construction where contamination or challenging soil conditions require ongoing management. This consultant's fees appear in the soft cost budget from early in the project lifecycle.

## 6.3 The Architect's Scope — From Concept to Construction Documents

The architect's contract defines their scope across five broadly recognized phases, though terminology varies between firms. Understanding what each phase produces and what decisions get locked in at each stage is fundamental to managing design cost and schedule.

### Pre-Design / Project Definition

Before design begins, the architect works with the developer to define the project program — the list of spaces, their sizes, their relationships to each other, and their performance requirements. For a residential tower, the program includes the unit mix (number of studios, one-bedrooms, two-bedrooms, three-bedrooms), suite sizes, common area allocations, amenity program, parking count, and loading configuration.

The program is the single most important document produced in the pre-design phase. A poorly defined program produces a design that does not serve its market. A program that is revised mid-design produces costly redesign work billed as additional fees.

### Schematic Design (SD)

Schematic design translates the program into preliminary design concepts. The architect produces floor plan studies, massing models, and preliminary building sections sufficient to confirm that the program can be achieved on the site within the applicable zoning envelope.

The key deliverables at SD stage are a confirmed floor plate layout, a preliminary unit mix, a massing model, and a preliminary building section showing floor-to-floor heights. At this stage, decisions about floor plate efficiency — the ratio of rentable or saleable area to gross building area — are established. A floor plate that is 5% less efficient than it could be represents a permanent loss of revenue across every floor of the building.

SD is also when the developer engages the cost consultant to produce a Class D cost estimate — a rough order of magnitude based on area and system assumptions rather than detailed drawings. This estimate tells the developer whether the design concept is in the right cost range before significant design investment is made.

### Design Development (DD)

Design development takes the approved schematic design and develops it to a level sufficient to confirm building systems, resolve major design issues, and produce a reliable cost estimate. At DD stage, the structural system is confirmed, MEP systems are defined, the building envelope approach is established, and the interior layouts are refined to a level where suite efficiency can be accurately measured.

The Class C cost estimate produced at DD stage is a meaningful cost control document. If the DD cost estimate shows the project trending above the feasibility study budget, design development is the last practical moment to make significant design changes without incurring major redesign costs.

### Construction Documents (CD)

Construction documents are the complete set of drawings and specifications from which the building will be tendered and built. A full CD set for a residential tower includes architectural drawings, structural drawings, mechanical drawings, electrical drawings, plumbing drawings, civil drawings, landscape drawings, and specifications for every material and system in the building.

The CD package includes both drawings and specifications. Drawings convey spatial and dimensional information; specifications define the quality, performance standard, and installation requirements for every material in the building. In Canada, specifications are organized under the **CSC MasterFormat** — the Canadian edition of the Construction Specifications Institute's MasterFormat standard used in the U.S. MasterFormat divides construction scope into numbered divisions: Division 1 (General Requirements), Division 3 (Concrete), Division 4 (Masonry), Division 5 (Metals), Division 7 (Thermal & Moisture Protection), and so on through Division 16 (Electrical). When you open a quantity surveyor's construction cost estimate — such as the Finnegan Marshall reports used throughout this course — the budget is organized by these same divisions. Understanding that structure is what allows you to read a QS report intelligently rather than treating it as an undifferentiated list of numbers.

Developers tend to focus their design reviews on drawings at the expense of specifications, which is a mistake. The quality, durability, and warranty compliance of nearly every material in the building is governed by the spec, not the drawings. A developer who approves drawings without reviewing specifications is approving only half the document package that will govern what gets built.

The CD stage represents the majority of the architect's fee — typically 40% to 50% of total architectural services. It is the most time-intensive phase and the one most sensitive to scope changes. A change in suite layout at CD stage requires coordinated revisions across architectural, structural, mechanical, electrical, and plumbing drawings simultaneously — a multiplier effect on both cost and schedule.

The CD stage is also reviewed in milestone increments rather than as a single deliverable. On most Ontario projects, the developer reviews drawings and specifications at 50% complete and 95% complete (some teams use 25%/50%/75%/95% intervals on larger projects). These milestone reviews are the developer's primary tool for catching scope drift and cost-driving decisions within the CD stage before the permit set is finalized. Reviewing only the completed CD set — after all decisions are locked — eliminates the opportunity to course-correct at reasonable cost.

### Construction Administration (CA)

Construction administration is the architect's ongoing involvement during the construction phase. CA services include reviewing shop drawings and product submittals, responding to Requests for Information (RFIs) from the contractor, reviewing and certifying change orders, conducting site reviews, and issuing the Substantial Performance certificate.

An important distinction governs the architect's CA role: the architect **monitors** the work but does not **supervise** it. Monitoring means periodic site reviews, reviewing submittals, and responding to RFIs. Supervision — continuous on-site direction of the contractor's means, methods, and workforce — is the contractor's responsibility, not the architect's. This distinction defines the boundary of the architect's professional liability: if a field deficiency is discovered, the question of who bears responsibility turns in part on whether it was something the architect should have caught through periodic monitoring or something that only continuous supervision would have detected.

CA is often underscoped and undervalued by developers seeking to minimize soft costs. A well-resourced CA process catches field problems early, reduces RFI volume, and accelerates the change order resolution process. An underresourced CA process results in a contractor who substitutes materials without authorization, a design that drifts from intent during construction, and disputes at project closeout.

## 6.4 Design Stages — Schematic, Design Development & Construction Documents

The progression from schematic design through construction documents follows a deliberate logic: each stage resolves a specific category of uncertainty before passing the project to the next stage. Understanding what questions each stage is designed to answer helps a development team manage the process rather than be managed by it.

### Stage Gate Reviews

Best practice in development management is to treat each design stage transition as a formal stage gate — a structured review at which the developer explicitly approves the work before authorizing the next stage. This sounds obvious but is frequently skipped in practice, particularly under schedule pressure.

A stage gate review for schematic design should answer four questions:

1. Does the design achieve the approved program?
2. Does the massing comply with the approved zoning envelope?
3. Is the preliminary cost estimate within the feasibility study budget?
4. Are there any design decisions made at this stage that will limit options in subsequent stages?

Only when all four questions are answered satisfactorily should design development be authorized. A developer who approves schematic design without a satisfactory answer to question 3 is authorizing design development spend on a project that may not pencil.

### Floor Plate Efficiency

One of the most consequential design metrics established during schematic design is floor plate efficiency — the ratio of net sellable or rentable area to gross floor area. On a residential project, typical floor plate efficiency runs 75% to 85%. The gap between 75% and 85% on a 200-unit project represents a material difference in revenue.

Floor plate efficiency is primarily driven by core size and corridor configuration. A central core (elevators, stairs, mechanical shafts grouped at the building's centre) generally produces higher efficiency than a side core configuration, but creates internal corridor conditions that affect unit layout quality. The architect's job is to optimize this tradeoff — maximizing efficiency without producing units that are difficult to sell or rent because of poor natural light, awkward layouts, or long internal corridors.

### The Importance of Sections

Developers who focus exclusively on floor plans often miss critical design information that only shows up in building sections. Floor-to-floor height — the vertical distance from one finished floor to the next — has direct implications for construction cost, mechanical system design, and marketability.

A residential floor-to-floor of 3.0 metres is functional but modest. 3.2 metres starts to feel more generous. A two-storey retail base with a 6.0 metre floor-to-floor produces a very different retail environment than a 4.5 metre floor-to-floor. These decisions, made at schematic design, lock in a cost per floor that multiplies across the full height of the building.

### The Cost Estimate Classification System

Cost estimates in Ontario are produced by quantity surveyors (QS) in accordance with the **Canadian Institute of Quantity Surveyors (CIQS)** methodology — referenced explicitly in the Finnegan Marshall estimates used throughout this course. The Class D / Class C / Class B / Class A classification terminology used in this chapter reflects that CIQS framework. U.S. textbooks and some American practitioners may reference the AACE (Association for the Advancement of Cost Engineering) classification system instead, which uses a different numbering convention (Class 5 through Class 1, roughly corresponding to D through A in the Canadian system). The underlying concept — that estimate reliability increases as design detail increases — is the same; the terminology is not.

## 6.5 Zoning Compliance & Design Constraints

The design must operate within the envelope established by the zoning bylaw and any conditions attached to the site's approval. Understanding how zoning constraints shape design is essential for evaluating whether a design is optimizing the site or leaving value on the table.

### The Ontario Planning Hierarchy

Design constraints on an Ontario project flow through a layered hierarchy: the Provincial Policy Statement (PPS) sets provincial land use direction; the Official Plan (OP) establishes city-wide land use policy; Secondary Plans add area-specific policy for defined neighbourhoods; and the site-specific zoning by-law sets the legally binding development parameters. Conditions of approval from the Site Plan Approval process are then layered on top. U.S. textbooks use different terminology for equivalent instruments — the "general plan" in U.S. practice corresponds roughly to the Official Plan; the "specific plan" corresponds roughly to the Secondary Plan. The mechanisms differ in important ways, but the structural hierarchy is similar.

### Key Zoning Parameters That Drive Design

**Floor Space Index (FSI)** — the ratio of total building floor area to site area. An FSI of 4.0 on a 1,000 m² site permits 4,000 m² of gross floor area. FSI is the primary determinant of building density and the first number a developer calculates when evaluating a site. The equivalent U.S. term is Floor Area Ratio (FAR); the concepts are identical. FSI is Toronto's preferred terminology and is used throughout this chapter; FAR appears in many 905-area zoning by-laws and in most U.S. textbooks — they refer to the same measure.

**Building Height** — expressed in metres, storeys, or both. Height limits may be absolute, or they may be expressed as a view corridor or angular plane requirement that allows a taller building to step back as it rises.

**Setbacks** — the minimum distance between the building face and the property line. Front, rear, and side setbacks collectively define the buildable footprint. On a tight urban site, setbacks can constrain the floor plate significantly.

**Stepbacks** — upper-floor setbacks that reduce massing at height. Common on mid-rise and high-rise projects where the municipality requires building massing to transition to the scale of adjacent streets or neighbourhoods.

**Podium Height** — on projects with a base-and-tower configuration, the zoning or design guidelines may specify a maximum podium height — the height of the lower building mass — to maintain street wall continuity.

**Amenity Space Requirements** — many municipalities require a minimum quantum of indoor and outdoor amenity space as a function of unit count. Toronto's zoning by-law requires a minimum of 4.0 m² of indoor amenity space and 4.0 m² of outdoor amenity space per unit as a general baseline; however, this requirement may be subject to site-specific conditions or Major Transit Station Area (MTSA) policy overlays that modify the standard. Always verify the applicable requirement against the current zoning instrument and any conditions of approval for the specific site.

### Design Guidelines vs. Zoning

In addition to the zoning bylaw, many Toronto and 905 municipality projects are subject to design guidelines — documents that set expectations for architectural character, massing, materiality, and the public realm. Design guidelines are typically not law but they shape what the municipality will and will not support through the approval process.

Toronto's Tall Building Design Guidelines, Mid-Rise Guidelines, and various Secondary Plan design policies all set expectations that the design team must respond to. A design that ignores the applicable guidelines will face pushback at Site Plan Approval, adding time and cost to the approval process.

## 6.6 Building Code & Accessibility Requirements

The Ontario Building Code (OBC) sets minimum standards for structural performance, fire safety, means of egress, plumbing, mechanical systems, energy efficiency, and accessibility. Compliance with the OBC is not optional — a building permit will not be issued for a non-compliant design, and a building that is constructed in non-compliance with the OBC creates significant liability for the developer.

An important distinction from U.S. practice: the OBC is a uniform **provincial** standard that applies across all Ontario municipalities. Municipalities cannot modify or reduce OBC requirements. They can, however, require performance standards *above* the OBC through instruments like the Toronto Green Standard — but they cannot opt out of or locally amend the provincial code. This differs from the U.S. model, where building codes are typically state-adopted and subject to local amendment. The practical implication is that the OBC's provisions apply consistently whether a project is in Toronto, Richmond Hill, or Mississauga — what changes across municipalities are the additional requirements layered on top, not the base code floor.

### Key Code Considerations for Residential Development

**Fire separations and exit requirements** — the OBC specifies the fire resistance rating required for floor assemblies, suite demising walls, corridor walls, and exit stairwells based on building height and occupancy. These requirements drive structural system selection and wall construction specifications.

**Means of egress** — the number and configuration of exit stairwells is governed by travel distance requirements (the maximum distance a building occupant must travel to reach an exit) and occupant load. On a typical residential floor plate, travel distance requirements effectively cap the distance from the farthest suite to the nearest stair.

**Accessibility** — Ontario's accessibility requirements for new construction are among the most comprehensive in Canada, governed by both the OBC and the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA). Requirements include minimum suite dimensions for accessible units, accessible washroom configurations, elevator requirements, accessible parking ratios, and accessible routes through all common areas.

**Energy efficiency** — OBC Division B Part 12 sets minimum energy performance standards for new construction, with requirements for building envelope thermal performance, window-to-wall ratio limits, and mechanical system efficiency. These requirements have been progressively tightened and interact with Toronto's additional requirements under the Toronto Green Standard.

### Alternative Solutions

The OBC permits design departures from prescriptive requirements through the alternative solution process — provided the designer can demonstrate that the proposed alternative achieves the same level of performance as the prescriptive requirement. Alternative solutions are common on complex or architecturally ambitious projects and require the engagement of the Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ) early in the design process. They add time and cost but can enable design outcomes that would otherwise be impossible.

## 6.7 Sustainability, Green Building & LEED in Ontario

Sustainability in building design has moved from a differentiator to a baseline expectation in most Ontario markets. Three frameworks are most relevant to Ontario development: the Toronto Green Standard, LEED certification, and the Passive House standard.

### Toronto Green Standard (TGS)

The Toronto Green Standard is a mandatory performance standard for new development in the City of Toronto, applied as a condition of Site Plan Approval. The TGS is organized into four tiers:

- **Tier 1** — minimum mandatory requirements for all new development. These are effectively conditions of Site Plan Approval and are not optional.
- **Tiers 2, 3, and 4** — increasingly ambitious voluntary performance levels tied to development charge refund incentives.

TGS Version 4 (the current version) focuses on four outcome areas: climate change mitigation (operational carbon), climate change resilience (passive survivability), human health, and ecology. Key requirements include near-zero carbon performance for new residential buildings above a certain height, EV charging infrastructure, bird-friendly glazing, and urban heat island reduction measures.

The financial implication of TGS compliance is meaningful. Achieving Tier 1 adds a measurable cost to the building envelope and mechanical systems compared to a code-minimum building. Achieving higher tiers adds progressively more, though this can be partially offset by the DC refund incentive program.

### LEED

LEED (Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design) is a voluntary third-party certification program. In Canada, LEED is administered by the **Canada Green Building Council (CaGBC)**, which is a separate organization from the U.S. Green Building Council (USGBC) referenced throughout U.S. textbooks. The Canadian program uses the same framework and certification levels (Certified, Silver, Gold, Platinum) but applies Canada-specific reference standards and compliance pathways. A project certified under LEED Canada is not simply following the U.S. LEED system — the CaGBC administers a distinct program adapted for the Canadian regulatory and climate context.

LEED certification is pursued on Ontario development projects in two primary scenarios: where the developer's equity partners or institutional investors require it as a condition of investment; and where the target tenant pool — particularly office and institutional tenants — will pay a premium for certified space or require it as a condition of lease.

For residential condos and rentals, LEED certification is less common than TGS compliance because the incremental certification cost and timeline are difficult to justify without a direct revenue or financing premium. The *Energy Star* certification program, which is prominent in U.S. commercial and residential development practice and frequently cited in U.S. textbooks, has limited uptake in Ontario multi-residential development. TGS compliance is the functional baseline standard in Toronto; Energy Star is not the primary sustainability reference point in this market.

### Passive House

Passive House (Passivhaus) is a rigorous performance standard focused on minimizing heating and cooling energy demand through building envelope quality rather than mechanical system efficiency. Passive House buildings are characterized by very high levels of insulation, triple-glazed windows, minimal thermal bridging, airtight construction, and heat recovery ventilation.

Passive House is gaining traction in Ontario's purpose-built rental sector — and the financial case for it has strengthened significantly through CMHC's **MLI Select** financing program. MLI Select provides tiered financing incentives for purpose-built rental projects that achieve higher energy performance: at the highest performance tier, projects can access 50-year amortization (compared to the standard 25-year maximum), meaningfully lower mortgage loan insurance premiums, and higher loan-to-cost ratios. The difference in debt service between a 25-year and 50-year amortization on a large rental project can be several hundred thousand dollars per year — a direct and permanent improvement to cash-on-cash returns. This means the decision about energy performance standard is not purely a construction cost question; it is also a financing structure question. A project that spends $1.5–2.5 million more on envelope and mechanical to achieve Passive House certification may recover that premium entirely through more favourable CMHC financing terms, in addition to lower long-term operating costs.

The incremental construction cost of Passive House is real but declining as the Ontario supply chain for high-performance building products matures.

## 6.8 Value Engineering — Balancing Cost and Design

Value engineering (VE) is one of the most misunderstood and misapplied terms in development. In its proper form, value engineering is a systematic process of analyzing the function of a building system or component and finding ways to deliver that function at lower cost without compromising performance. In practice, it is often used as a euphemism for cutting specifications when the budget is over.

The distinction matters because these are fundamentally different exercises with very different outcomes.

### True Value Engineering vs. Spec Cutting

**True value engineering** asks: what is this element trying to achieve, and is there a better way to achieve it? A VE exercise on a lobby might conclude that the same spatial quality can be achieved with a different stone species, a different lighting strategy, or a reconfigured layout that reduces square footage without reducing impact. The outcome is equivalent performance at lower cost.

**Spec cutting** asks: what can we take out or downgrade to reduce cost? Spec cutting produces lower cost but also lower performance — shorter material lifespans, higher maintenance costs, reduced marketability, and sometimes code compliance issues if the cuts go too far.

Experienced development teams treat VE as a design exercise rather than a budget exercise. The cost consultant identifies where the design is trending above budget. The architect proposes alternatives that maintain design intent. The developer evaluates the alternatives against their market positioning strategy. The result is a design that is optimized for value rather than simply reduced in cost.

### When VE Happens — and Why Timing Matters

VE is most effective when conducted during design development — after the structural system and building systems are confirmed but before the full construction document set is produced. At this stage, changes to systems and specifications can be implemented across the drawings at relatively low cost.

VE conducted during or after construction documents is significantly more expensive because changes ripple across multiple drawing disciplines simultaneously. VE conducted during construction — the most common scenario when a contractor's tender comes in over budget — is the most expensive of all because it may require not just drawing revisions but also demolition of work already completed.

This is why the Class C estimate at design development is so important. A DD estimate that shows the project trending 8% over budget triggers a VE exercise while changes are still relatively cheap to implement. A tender that comes in 8% over budget triggers a much more expensive and disruptive process.

### Common VE Opportunities in Residential Development

- **Building envelope** — cladding material is often the single highest-impact VE item. Moving from a unitized curtainwall to a stick-frame window-and-spandrel system, or from a brick veneer to a fibre cement panel, can reduce envelope cost by 20% to 40% while maintaining code performance.
- **Suite finishes** — flooring species, countertop material, appliance specifications, and fixture quality are all adjustable based on market positioning. The key is calibrating specifications to achievable sale price or rent rather than applying a blanket upgrade or downgrade.
- **Mechanical systems** — heat recovery ventilator (HRV) specifications, fan coil configurations, and domestic hot water system design are all areas where the mechanical engineer can often find cost without compromising performance.
- **Parking structure** — post-tensioned concrete slab vs. conventional reinforced concrete is a common structural VE item. Reducing the number of below-grade levels — eliminating one level of underground parking — is often the highest-impact single VE decision available, but requires confirming that the parking reduction is supportable under zoning.

## 6.9 Design Reviews, Approvals & Coordination

Managing the design process requires more than hiring the right consultants. It requires active coordination, clear communication protocols, and a structured review process that keeps all parties aligned.

### Internal Design Reviews

The developer's project manager (or development manager) should conduct formal internal design reviews at each stage gate. These reviews should involve not just the developer's internal team but also the cost consultant, who can translate design decisions into cost implications in real time.

A well-run schematic design review typically covers: program compliance check, massing compliance with the approved zoning envelope, floor plate efficiency calculation, preliminary unit mix revenue model, cost estimate review, and identification of any design decisions that will drive cost or revenue materially.

Internal design reviews should also include input from the project's leasing, sales, and property management representatives — either as direct team members or as external reviewers engaged specifically for this purpose. These parties bring end-user perspective that the design and cost teams do not: a leasing agent knows whether the proposed unit mix reflects what the target rental market will absorb; a property manager knows whether the corridor configuration will create maintenance challenges; a condo sales team knows whether the lobby design will support the price-per-square-foot assumption in the revenue model. Design that is technically sound and cost-controlled but disconnected from how the end product will actually be leased or sold creates problems at a stage when they are far more expensive to fix. Involving these parties during design — not after it — is one of the most consistent practices of experienced development teams.

### Consultant Coordination

One of the most common sources of design error and project delay is poor coordination between design consultants. The architect produces the coordinating drawings, but structural, mechanical, and electrical consultants all produce their own drawings — and conflicts between these drawing sets (a structural beam in the same space as a mechanical duct, for example) are common and costly to resolve in the field.

Building Information Modelling (BIM) has significantly reduced coordination conflicts on projects that use it well. A federated BIM model allows all consultants to work in a shared three-dimensional environment, with automated clash detection identifying conflicts before they reach the field.

Even on projects that do not use full BIM, a formal coordination review — where all drawing disciplines are overlaid and checked for conflicts — should be conducted before the CD set is issued for permit and tender.

### Phased Permitting — Early Works Permits

On larger Ontario projects, developers commonly obtain an **Early Works Permit** — sometimes called a shoring and excavation permit — before the full building permit is issued. This allows foundation excavation, shoring installation, and below-grade construction to begin while the full permit set is still under municipal review, compressing the overall pre-construction schedule by weeks or months. The full building permit for the above-grade structure must still be obtained before work proceeds above grade; the early works permit is not a shortcut on design completeness — it requires a fully engineered shoring and excavation design package. From a Senior Analyst's perspective, the practical consequence is that construction draw requests for shoring and excavation can begin appearing in the draw schedule before the full building permit is in hand. Understanding why this is contractually and regulatorily permissible is part of reading the draw package accurately.

### Municipal Design Review

Site Plan Approval (SPA) involves a structured design review by the municipality's urban design and planning staff. This is distinct from zoning review — SPA is about design quality, not just compliance. Municipal urban design staff will comment on massing, materiality, the public realm interface, landscaping, and the relationship of the building to adjacent streets and properties.

Managing the SPA design review process requires understanding what the municipality is prioritizing, responding to comments efficiently, and knowing which comments are mandatory and which are aspirational. A skilled planning and urban design consultant is invaluable in navigating this process.

## AE-6.4 — Reading Design Stage Deliverables

**Scenario**

You receive the following documents from the architect for internal review:

- Dimensioned floor plans at 1:100 scale showing a typical residential floor with 8 units per floor
- A building section showing floor-to-floor heights of 3.1 metres throughout
- A massing model showing a 22-storey tower above a 4-storey podium
- A preliminary unit mix table: 20 studios (42 m²), 60 one-bedrooms (58 m²), 80 two-bedrooms (82 m²), 20 three-bedrooms (105 m²)
- A memo from the architect noting that structural system and MEP systems are "to be determined pending structural engineer engagement"
- A preliminary cost estimate from the cost consultant at $285/sf blended above-grade

**Questions**

1. What design stage are these documents most consistent with?
2. What category of cost estimate corresponds to these deliverables?
3. The cost consultant's estimate is $285/sf. The feasibility study assumed $310/sf. What does this tell you, and what should happen next?
4. The architect's memo notes that structural and MEP systems are TBD. Is this appropriate for this design stage?
5. Calculate the approximate gross floor area of the typical residential floors (floors 5–22), assuming 8 units per floor with an average unit size of 73 m² and a floor plate efficiency of 80%.

---

## AE-6.4 — Solution

**Question 1 — Design Stage**

These documents are consistent with **Schematic Design (SD)**. The indicators are: dimensioned but not fully detailed floor plans, a massing model rather than a fully developed building section, and structural/MEP systems that have not yet been confirmed. At Design Development, structural systems would be confirmed and MEP systems defined.

**Question 2 — Cost Estimate Class**

A cost estimate produced at schematic design is a **Class D estimate** — an order-of-magnitude figure based on area and system assumptions. It is reliable enough to confirm the project is in the right cost range but not reliable enough for detailed budget management.

**Question 3 — Cost Estimate Below Feasibility Budget**

The $285/sf estimate is $25/sf below the $310/sf feasibility assumption — approximately 8% under. The appropriate response is **not to treat this as found money**. The correct action is to flag it for investigation before design development begins:

- Is the SD estimate based on assumptions consistent with the design intent, or has the cost consultant assumed a simpler building than is planned?
- Does the estimate include the full scope of soft costs, or only hard construction?
- Is the estimate current (January 2026 dollars) or based on older benchmarks?

An SD estimate that comes in significantly under the feasibility budget is a reason for careful scrutiny, not celebration. The gap often closes — or reverses — as the design is developed and priced more accurately.

**Question 4 — Structural and MEP TBD**

Yes, this is appropriate for schematic design. The structural system and MEP systems are confirmed at Design Development, not Schematic Design. If these were still TBD at DD stage, that would be a problem. At SD, the architect's job is to establish the spatial concept; the engineers respond to that concept at DD.

**Question 5 — Gross Floor Area Calculation**

- Floors 5–22 = 18 residential floors
- 8 units per floor x average unit size 73 m² = 584 m² of net area per floor
- Floor plate efficiency 80% = gross floor area per floor = 584 / 0.80 = **730 m² per floor**
- Total residential gross floor area = 730 x 18 = **13,140 m²**

Note: the podium floors (1–4) and any mechanical/amenity floors are excluded from this calculation and would be calculated separately.

## AE-6.8 — Evaluating a Value Engineering Proposal

**Scenario**

The design development cost estimate has come in at $338/sf against a feasibility budget of $310/sf — a 9% overrun of approximately $4.2M on a 150,000 sf building. The project manager has asked the design team to identify $4.5M in value engineering opportunities.

The architect and cost consultant have submitted the following three VE proposals for your review:

**VE-01 — Cladding System Change**
Replace the specified unitized curtainwall system on the tower facade with a stick-frame window and spandrel panel system. Estimated saving: $2,800,000. The architect notes that the visual appearance from street level will be "substantially similar" but concedes that the window-to-wall ratio will decrease from 55% to 45% and the thermal performance of the envelope will be reduced.

**VE-02 — Suite Finish Downgrade**
Reduce flooring specification from engineered hardwood to luxury vinyl plank throughout all suites. Reduce countertop specification from quartz to laminate in studio and one-bedroom units only. Estimated saving: $920,000.

**VE-03 — Eliminate One Level of Underground Parking**
Reduce underground parking from two levels (120 spaces) to one level (60 spaces). Estimated saving: $3,600,000. The cost consultant notes that below-grade construction is the most expensive component per square foot in the building. The planning consultant advises that the zoning bylaw requires a minimum of 0.5 spaces per unit and the project has 180 units, requiring 90 spaces minimum.

**Exercise**

Evaluate each VE proposal on the following criteria: financial impact, design/quality impact, and feasibility/risk. Identify which proposals you would recommend accepting, modifying, or rejecting, and state your reasoning.

---

## AE-6.8 — Solution

**VE-01 — Cladding System Change: ACCEPT WITH CAUTION**

The $2,800,000 saving is substantial and addresses the majority of the cost gap. However, the quality implications require scrutiny before acceptance:

- A reduction in window-to-wall ratio from 55% to 45% affects natural light in suites — a marketability consideration for a condo project and a tenant satisfaction consideration for a rental project
- Reduced thermal performance of the envelope may affect Toronto Green Standard compliance, which needs to be confirmed before accepting
- The architect's characterization of "substantially similar" visual appearance should be tested against the market positioning of the project

Recommendation: Accept subject to confirmation that TGS Tier 1 compliance is maintained and that the marketing team has reviewed the window-to-wall ratio reduction against comparable projects in the market.

**VE-02 — Suite Finish Downgrade: ACCEPT PARTIALLY**

The luxury vinyl plank substitution for engineered hardwood is a legitimate VE item — LVP has improved significantly in quality and is accepted in both the rental and condo markets. The estimated saving is meaningful.

The countertop downgrade from quartz to laminate in studios and one-bedrooms requires more careful evaluation. In the current GTA condo market, laminate countertops are below market standard and may negatively affect absorption or require a price reduction that exceeds the $920,000 saving.

Recommendation: Accept the flooring change. Reject the countertop change in the condo product. If this is a rental project, the countertop change may be acceptable depending on the rental market tier being targeted.

**VE-03 — Eliminate One Level of Underground Parking: REJECT**

This proposal cannot proceed as described. The planning consultant has confirmed that the zoning bylaw requires a minimum of 90 parking spaces for 180 units. Reducing to one level of 60 spaces would put the project 30 spaces below the zoning minimum — a non-compliant condition that would prevent the building permit from being issued.

Even if a zoning amendment or minor variance were available to reduce the parking requirement, pursuing that approval would add 6 to 12 months to the project schedule. The carrying cost of that delay would significantly erode the $3,600,000 saving.

Recommendation: Reject. Explore whether the structural design of the two-level parking structure can be optimized as an alternative cost reduction — for example, reviewing the slab thickness design with the structural engineer.

**Overall VE Result**

Accepting VE-01 (with conditions) and the flooring component of VE-02 generates approximately $3,500,000 to $3,700,000 in savings — closing most of the $4,500,000 gap. The remaining shortfall should be addressed through a targeted review of mechanical system specifications and general conditions scope rather than further finish downgrades.

## AE-6.9 — Managing a Design Review Conflict

**Scenario**

The project is in design development. The cost consultant (Finnegan Marshall) has issued the Class C estimate at $338/sf, as described in AE-6.8. In response, the project manager asks the architect to proceed with the cladding VE described in VE-01.

The architect responds with the following email:

*"We must advise that the proposed cladding change will compromise the architectural integrity of the building and will likely result in rejection of the design at Site Plan Approval. We are not prepared to proceed with this change without a written direction from the developer acknowledging these risks."*

The cost consultant responds separately:

*"The curtainwall system as specified is over-engineered for this project type and market. Similar projects in the submarket have used stick-frame systems without any issue at SPA. We recommend proceeding."*

**Exercise**

1. Who is right — the architect or the cost consultant?
2. What process should the developer follow to resolve this conflict?
3. What written documentation should be produced before proceeding?

---

## AE-6.9 — Solution

**Question 1 — Who is Right?**

Both parties are partially right, and the developer should resist the temptation to simply side with whoever is most persuasive. The architect is right that the cladding change carries SPA risk — this is a legitimate concern that must be assessed, not dismissed. The cost consultant is right that stick-frame cladding systems have been approved at SPA in comparable projects — this is also true.

The actual answer depends on facts neither party has definitively established: whether the specific municipality's urban design guidelines for this site require the curtainwall aesthetic, and whether comparable projects that used stick-frame systems are genuinely comparable in terms of location, height, and design guidelines context.

**Question 2 — Process to Resolve**

The developer should take the following steps:

First, direct the planning and urban design consultant — not the architect or the cost consultant — to assess the SPA risk of the cladding change. The planning consultant has the relationship with the municipal urban design staff and can determine whether this change is likely to generate objection.

Second, if the planning consultant's assessment is that the change is approvable, provide written direction to the architect to proceed and acknowledge the architect's stated concerns on the record. The architect has fulfilled their professional obligation by raising the concern; the developer can exercise their authority to proceed.

Third, if the planning consultant's assessment is that the change creates material SPA risk, explore alternative envelope VE options with the architect before making a decision.

**Question 3 — Written Documentation**

Before proceeding, the following documentation should exist on the project file:

- The architect's written objection (already exists per the scenario)
- The planning consultant's written assessment of SPA risk
- A written direction from the developer to the architect, acknowledging the raised concerns and authorizing the change
- A notation in the project risk register recording the decision and the basis for it

This documentation protects the developer if the SPA process later generates comments on the cladding, and ensures the decision was made deliberately rather than by default.

## Chapter Summary

Design is a financial discipline as much as a creative one. Every decision made during the design process — from floor plate configuration to cladding specification to floor-to-floor height — has cost and revenue implications that flow directly into the project's feasibility and into the monthly draw packages and budget variance reports that track the project's financial health.

The design process moves through a funnel from wide-open schematic concept to locked construction documents. The most important principle is that problems are cheapest to fix early. Stage gate reviews, Class D and Class C cost estimates, proactive value engineering during design development, and interim CD milestone reviews are the tools that keep design and cost aligned before the project reaches the field.

Ontario's regulatory environment — the Ontario Building Code as a uniform provincial standard, the Toronto Green Standard, and the Site Plan Approval design review process — sets meaningful constraints on design that are specific to this market and distinct from the U.S. frameworks described in many standard development textbooks. The consultant team required on a GTA project is also more extensive than U.S. textbook descriptions typically suggest: building envelope consultants, wind consultants, and dedicated sustainability consultants are now standard roles on any mid-rise or high-rise project, and their fees appear in the soft cost budget from the early stages of design. Understanding these constraints and the composition of the consultant team is part of understanding why projects in the GTA cost what they cost and take as long as they take.
