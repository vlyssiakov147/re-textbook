# Chapter 1: Market Analysis & Feasibility

Before a single dollar is committed or a permit is filed, every successful real estate development begins with one foundational question: *does this project make sense?* Market analysis and feasibility studies are the tools developers use to answer that question rigorously — with data, comparables, and financial modeling — rather than gut feel. Getting this phase right is the single most important thing a developer can do. No amount of great execution recovers a project built on a flawed market thesis.

---

## 1.1 What is Market Analysis?

Market analysis is the systematic process of evaluating the economic environment in which a proposed development will compete. At its core, it answers one question: is there a genuine, quantifiable need for what you are proposing to build, in this location, at this point in time? It is not a marketing exercise. It is not an attempt to build the case for a project you have already decided to pursue. Done properly, market analysis is an objective interrogation of the market — and it should be capable of killing a project just as readily as it confirms one.

In practice, developers conducting market analysis are assembling a picture of four interconnected dynamics: the existing supply of competing product, the current and projected demand from end users, the pricing that the market will support, and the timing sensitivity of the opportunity. Each of these informs the others, and none can be evaluated in isolation.

A useful concept when beginning to evaluate any site is **situs** — a term from Graaskamp's Situs Theory referring to the full set of locational relationships that connect a site to its surrounding environment. Situs captures not just what is physically adjacent to the site but the broader question of whether the site fits its location, whether the location fits the intended tenant or end user, and whether the tenant fits the financing structure that the project requires. These three-way relationships — site to location, location to user, user to financing — must all align for a development to succeed. Two identical buildings in different locations will perform very differently because their situs is different, which is why location-specific market analysis cannot be replaced by general market benchmarks.

Market analysis typically occurs at two distinct stages. The first is a high-level screening — a quick read of a market or submarket to determine whether it warrants deeper investigation. This might take a few days and rely heavily on secondary data sources such as CBRE, JLL, or Altus Group reports, municipal planning documents, and census data. The second is a formal market study, which is more rigorous, often commissioned from a third-party consultant, and forms part of the package presented to lenders and equity partners.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> At the Senior Analyst level, market analysis and feasibility is work you are more likely to support and review than lead independently. Your principal contribution at this stage is data assembly and quality control — pulling vacancy and absorption figures from brokerage reports, building and validating comp tables, tracking the development pipeline in a submarket, and stress-testing the revenue assumptions that feed into a pro forma. The strategic interpretation — whether the market thesis is sound, whether the land price is justified, whether the project should proceed — is typically driven by a VP or Principal who relies on your analysis being accurate and well-organized. Notably, the feasibility and financing work covered in this chapter connects directly to the construction draw process and investor reporting you manage day-to-day — because the assumptions made here are the ones you will be reconciling against actual costs and funding requests throughout the construction period.

The most important habit to develop early is the distinction between what a market looks like today versus what it will look like when your project delivers. A residential tower that takes 18 months to get entitlements, 24 months to build, and 12 months to lease up will not be competing in today's market — it will be competing in the market that exists 4 to 5 years from now. Every assumption in your analysis needs to be tested against that future state, not the present one.

## 1.2 Supply & Demand Fundamentals

Real estate markets are driven by the relationship between supply and demand, but what makes them uniquely challenging to analyze is that supply is extremely slow to respond. When demand for apartments rises, developers cannot simply increase supply overnight. Sites need to be identified, assembled, and purchased. Entitlements take months or years. Construction takes additional years. By the time new supply actually reaches the market, the demand conditions that justified it may have shifted entirely. This lag is the fundamental source of cyclicality in real estate development.

> **Key Concept — Vacancy Rate**
> The vacancy rate is the percentage of available space in a market that is currently unoccupied and available for lease or sale. For residential, a healthy market typically sits below 5% vacancy. For office and retail, thresholds vary more widely by city and submarket, but vacancy above 10-12% generally signals an oversupplied market. Vacancy rate alone is not sufficient — trend direction matters as much as the absolute number. A market at 7% vacancy that was at 12% two years ago tells a very different story than one at 7% that was at 3%.

On the supply side, developers must track not just current vacancy but the full development pipeline — every project that is currently under construction, in permit review, or recently approved that will add new inventory to the market. This is where many feasibility analyses go wrong. A developer sees low vacancy and strong rents today, commits to a project, and then discovers 18 months later that six competing towers are delivering simultaneously. The pipeline was always visible; it just was not tracked carefully enough.

Pipeline data is available through brokerage firms, municipal permit databases, and data providers like Altus, CoStar, or RealPage. In Canada specifically, CMHC publishes detailed residential construction and starts data by census metropolitan area that is widely used as a primary pipeline source for residential analysis.

On the demand side, the drivers vary significantly by asset class. For **residential** development, the primary demand indicators are population growth, household formation rates, net migration, employment growth, and income levels relative to housing costs. For **commercial office**, demand is tied to white-collar employment. For **retail**, demand analysis focuses on trade area demographics, consumer spending capacity, traffic counts, and competitive positioning. For **mixed-use** development, the analysis is necessarily more complex because each component has its own supply and demand dynamics, and the interaction between them must be evaluated as well.

> **Common Problem — Mistaking a Strong Rental Market for a Strong Development Market**
> These are not the same thing. A submarket can have very low vacancy and strong rents — which looks great on the demand side — but if land costs are high, construction costs have escalated, and cap rates have compressed, the project still may not pencil. Market analysis tells you whether there is demand. Feasibility analysis tells you whether you can profitably meet that demand. Always run both before drawing conclusions.

**Worked Example — Reading a Residential Market:** Your firm is evaluating a site in a mid-sized urban submarket for a 150-unit purpose-built rental building. You pull the latest CMHC data and find the submarket vacancy rate is 2.1%, down from 3.4% two years ago. Average asking rents for two-bedroom units in comparable buildings are $2,400/month, up 11% year-over-year. The pipeline shows one competing 200-unit project under construction delivering in 14 months, and another 120-unit project in early permitting. Net migration to the city was +8,400 people last year, and a major tech company recently announced it is opening a regional office bringing an estimated 1,200 jobs. On the demand side, the indicators are strong. On the supply side, you need to model what the market looks like in 3 years when your building delivers — not today. The question becomes: will demand growth outpace the additional supply coming online?

## 1.3 Comparable Property Analysis

Comparable property analysis — universally called "running comps" — is the process of identifying and analyzing existing properties that are similar enough to your proposed development that their operating performance provides a reliable benchmark for what your project can achieve. It is one of the most hands-on parts of market analysis, and it is something analysts do extensively.

The goal is not simply to find the average rent or price in the market. The goal is to understand the full competitive landscape your project will enter — what existing properties are offering, what tenants or buyers are paying, where there are gaps or opportunities, and where you need to differentiate to justify your projected rents or prices.

**How to Build a Comp Table:** A residential rental comp table should capture the following for each comparable property: project name and address, year built or delivered, total number of units, unit mix (studio/1BR/2BR/3BR), average rent per unit type, average rent per square foot, current occupancy rate, any concessions being offered (free rent, free parking, etc.), amenity package, and parking ratio.

> **Key Concept — Comp Quality vs. Comp Proximity**
> The temptation when building a comp set is to only use properties within a tight geographic radius. But the more important criterion is product quality and target demographic. A luxury rental tower 2 km away is a more relevant comp for a proposed luxury building than a mid-market rental across the street. Always lead with product comparability, then consider location.

**Common Problem — Cherry-Picking Comps:** This is one of the most common and consequential errors in development feasibility. A developer who selects only the highest-performing properties in the market to benchmark their project will produce revenue projections that are unrealistically optimistic. The credible approach is to present the full comp set — high, mid, and low performers — and then make a clear, defensible argument for where your project will position within that range and why.

**Common Problem — Using Stale Comps:** A comp from three years ago in a market that has moved significantly is worse than no comp at all — it actively misleads your analysis. Always prioritize comps from the last 12-18 months.

**Worked Example — Positioning a Mixed-Use Condo Project:** Your firm is planning a mixed-use condominium tower with ground-floor retail in an urban infill location. You build a comp set of 8 recently completed or actively selling condo projects within a 3 km radius. The comp set shows average pricing ranging from $850/sf to $1,350/sf. Your site has strong transit access and is in a neighbourhood undergoing visible gentrification, but the building will be a mid-market product, not luxury. You position your project at $1,050–$1,100/sf — above the market average but below the luxury tier — and build your revenue model on that range.

> **See Applied Exercise — Section AE-1.3**

> **Watch Out — Asking Price vs. Achieved Price**
> In condominium markets especially, there is often a gap between asking prices and actual achieved sale prices. Always try to verify actual achieved prices through land registry data, brokerage contacts, or data services rather than relying solely on marketing materials.

## 1.4 Absorption Rate & Lease-Up Modeling

Absorption rate is the speed at which available units or space in a market are leased or sold over a defined period, typically expressed on a monthly or annual basis. Understanding absorption is critical because it directly determines your revenue ramp-up assumptions — how quickly your project generates income after it delivers.

There are two absorption concepts to keep distinct. **Market absorption** refers to the rate at which the entire market is absorbing available supply — this is the macro context. **Project absorption** refers to the rate at which your specific project is expected to lease up or sell out — this is what goes into your financial model. Project absorption is always a subset of market absorption.

The distinction between gross and net absorption also matters. **Gross absorption** counts all new leases executed in a period, regardless of whether any space was simultaneously vacated. **Net absorption** subtracts space that became vacant during the same period. Net absorption is the more meaningful measure of genuine demand growth.

> **Key Concept — Lease-Up Period and Carrying Cost**
> The lease-up period is the time from your building opening to reaching stabilized occupancy — typically defined as 93-95% for rental residential. Every month of lease-up is a month where you are paying interest on your construction loan, covering operating costs, and generating below-stabilized revenue. Always model a base case, an optimistic case, and a conservative case for lease-up timing.

**Common Problem — Assuming Full Market Absorption:** A developer looks at market data showing 400 units absorbed per month across the submarket and reasons that their 300-unit project will therefore lease up quickly. This ignores the fact that market absorption is shared across all competing projects. Always model your project as a share of market absorption, not the entirety of it.

**Worked Example — Lease-Up Modeling for a Rental Building:** Your 180-unit purpose-built rental building is expected to deliver in Q3. The submarket is absorbing approximately 120 new rental units per month across all competing projects. You estimate your project will capture roughly 25% of that monthly absorption given your size and competitive position, implying absorption of 30 units per month. At that rate, your lease-up to 95% occupancy (171 units) would take approximately 5.7 months — call it 6 months for modeling purposes.

## 1.5 Highest and Best Use Analysis

Highest and Best Use (HBU) analysis is the process of determining which legally permissible, physically possible, financially feasible, and maximally productive use of a site would generate the greatest land value. The four criteria are sequential and hierarchical — a use must first be physically possible, then legally permissible, then financially feasible, and finally maximally productive. Situs theory introduced in Section 1.1 runs directly through HBU analysis: the maximally productive use is always evaluated in the context of what the location actually supports, who the end users are, and whether the financing structure that use requires is achievable.

> **Key Concept — As Vacant vs. As Improved**
> HBU analysis is often conducted in two phases: as if the site were vacant (what would you build here from scratch?) and as currently improved (given the existing building, what is the best use?). These can yield different answers. A site with an older commercial building might have an HBU as vacant of a residential tower, but the cost of demolition and the remaining value of the existing income stream might mean the HBU as improved is to continue operating the commercial use for several more years before redeveloping.

**Why HBU Matters for Developers:** When a developer pays for a site, they are paying for its development potential. The land value implied by the purchase price should be justifiable by the HBU of the site. If a developer proposes a use that does not reflect the HBU, they are leaving value on the table and their financial returns will likely underperform.

**Common Problem — Zoning Assumptions in HBU:** Developers sometimes identify a site, fall in love with a development program, and then build their HBU analysis around that program rather than conducting an objective assessment. HBU analysis should be based on what is currently permitted as a baseline, with rezoning upside modeled separately and conservatively.

**Worked Example — Mixed-Use HBU Analysis:** Your firm is reviewing a 0.8-acre infill site in a transit-oriented node currently zoned for commercial use at a 2.0 FSR. The site has a small older retail strip that generates modest income. Three uses are evaluated: (1) Continue commercial use with minor renovations; (2) Purpose-built rental residential at 4.5 FSR via rezoning; (3) Mixed-use with ground-floor retail and rental residential above. After running a residual land value analysis for each scenario, the mixed-use scenario produces the highest justified land value.

## 1.6 The Feasibility Study

### What Feasibility Actually Means

The best definition of feasibility in the real estate development context comes from renowned educator James A. Graaskamp, who articulated it in his foundational 1972 paper *A Rational Approach to Feasibility Analysis*: a real estate project is feasible when the analyst determines that there is a reasonable likelihood of satisfying explicit objectives when a selected course of action is tested for fit to a context of specific constraints and limited resources.

Each element of this definition is deliberate and important. First, feasibility never means certainty — it means reasonable likelihood. A favourable feasibility result does not guarantee a project succeeds. Second, feasibility requires satisfying explicit objectives — and those objectives belong not just to the developer but to every participant: the lender, the equity investors, the end users, and the public sector. A project that works financially for the developer but falls below the return threshold required by the equity investor is not actually feasible. Third, it is a specific course of action being tested — not a general idea. Timing and execution matter. A project that might be feasible in 2027 may not be feasible today. Fourth, it is tested against specific constraints — physical, legal, financial, and ethical. A project that clears all financial hurdles but requires misrepresentation to the municipality or unrealistic assumptions to attract investors is not feasible in any meaningful sense.

This is significantly broader than the simplified view of feasibility as "value exceeds cost." Cost coverage is a necessary condition but not sufficient on its own.

### The Staged Nature of Feasibility Analysis

Feasibility analysis does not happen all at once. It is an iterative process that unfolds in stages, with each stage costing more, committing more, and requiring more rigorous data than the one before it. Understanding this staging is essential because it explains how developers make go/no-go decisions efficiently — spending money progressively as confidence builds rather than committing to a full formal study on every site they consider.

**Stage 1 — The Back-of-the-Envelope Pro Forma**

Stage 1 feasibility is the first filter every development idea must pass before any meaningful time or money is spent on it. It is a simple, rapid comparison of estimated value against estimated cost — typically done in under an hour using rough benchmarks. The format is straightforward: estimate what the completed project will be worth (by applying a cap rate to estimated NOI for a rental project, or a price per square foot for a condo), and compare that against a rough estimate of what it will cost to build. If value comfortably exceeds cost, the idea warrants further investigation. If not, it does not — and you move on without having spent significant resources.

Most development ideas die at this stage, which is exactly the point. Running a quick back-of-the-envelope test before commissioning a formal market study or hiring a consultant is how experienced developers protect their time and capital.

**A simple back-of-the-envelope structure for a rental project:**

| Item | Example |
|------|---------|
| Estimated stabilized monthly rent | $2,400/unit |
| Estimated units | 200 units |
| Gross annual revenue | $5,760,000 |
| Less vacancy (5%) and operating expenses (~40%) | ($3,168,000) |
| Estimated NOI | $2,592,000 |
| Capitalized at 4.5% cap rate | **Value = $57,600,000** |
| Land cost | $15,000,000 |
| Construction cost estimate (~$350/sf x 140,000 sf) | $49,000,000 |
| Soft costs, fees, financing (~20% of hard) | $9,800,000 |
| **Estimated total cost** | **$73,800,000** |
| **Preliminary verdict** | Value ($57.6M) < Cost ($73.8M) — does not pencil at current land price |

This rough test tells you immediately that either the land price needs to come down, rents need to be higher, costs need to be lower, or some combination of all three before this project is worth investigating further.

**Stage 2 — Idea Refinement**

If the back-of-the-envelope test is promising, the developer moves into idea refinement — tying the concept to a specific site, engaging early consultants, conducting initial due diligence on site constraints, and refining the cost and revenue assumptions. Financial feasibility at Stage 2 is more detailed than Stage 1 but still preliminary. The developer is also beginning to assess whether lenders and investors are likely to be interested, which is part of the multi-participant feasibility test described below.

**Stage 3 — The Formal Feasibility Study**

The formal feasibility study is prepared when the developer has sufficient confidence in the concept and site to justify the cost of a comprehensive analysis. This is the document presented to construction lenders, equity investors, and often the municipality. A complete formal feasibility study includes: an executive summary; site and area maps; photographs and renderings; a formal market study with absorption projections; detailed revenue and operating cost projections; preliminary architectural drawings; documented cost estimates confirmed by a quantity surveyor; a development schedule; financing terms and sources; and background on the key project participants. The analytical core includes sensitivity analysis that moves the project from merely feasible to optimally structured, a risk assessment, and confirmation that the project meets each participant's objectives.

### Feasibility Must Work for All Participants

A project is not truly feasible unless it works for each party whose participation is required to make it happen. Consider a project where the developer's returns look excellent — but where the construction lender's loan-to-cost ratio exceeds their policy limit, the equity investors' preferred return threshold is not met in the base case, and the municipality's community benefit requirements have not been satisfied. That project is not feasible, despite the developer's numbers working.

In practice, this means the developer must model and confirm each participant's position explicitly: the lender's LTC, debt service coverage, and cost-to-complete coverage; the equity investor's preferred return, IRR hurdle, and equity multiple; the end user's affordability; and the public sector's planning objectives. If any participant's position is not viable, the project requires restructuring before it is truly feasible.

### The Six Core Components

1. **Market Analysis** — The full supply and demand assessment, comp analysis, and absorption projections.
2. **Site Analysis** — Physical characteristics, ownership structure, title encumbrances, environmental conditions, and development constraints.
3. **Development Program** — Building type, height, total gross floor area, unit mix, amenity package, and parking ratio.
4. **Cost Estimate** — A preliminary budget covering hard and soft costs, validated by a quantity surveyor before being relied upon in final underwriting.
5. **Revenue Projections** — Projected rents or sale prices supported by the comp analysis, stabilized occupancy assumptions, lease-up timeline, and operating expense estimates.
6. **Return Analysis** — The financial metrics that tell you whether the project is worth pursuing: yield on cost, development spread, IRR, equity multiple, and profit margin.

> **Common Problem — Garbage In, Garbage Out**
> A feasibility study is only as reliable as its inputs. The two inputs that cause the most problems are revenue assumptions that are too optimistic and cost assumptions that are too low. Construction costs in many Canadian markets increased 30-40% between 2019 and 2023. Using pre-escalation cost benchmarks in a current feasibility study is a serious error that has caused real losses for developers who did not update their underwriting.

**Sensitivity Analysis:** Once you have built your base case feasibility model, the most valuable thing you can do is run sensitivity analysis — systematically varying your key assumptions to understand how much they affect your returns. At minimum, stress-test rents or sale prices, construction costs, and absorption timeline. The standard is not to find a project that is feasible in the base case but one that remains viable across a realistic range of scenarios.

## 1.7 NOI, Cap Rates, and DCF — The Financial Engine of Feasibility

Understanding how a project's financial value is calculated is the core analytical tool of feasibility. This section explains how Net Operating Income is built, how it translates to value through cap rates, and how Discounted Cash Flow analysis and IRR extend that thinking across the life of a project.

### Net Operating Income

Net Operating Income (NOI) is the income a property generates from its operations after operating expenses but before debt service, capital expenditures, and income taxes. It is the industry-standard measure of a property's productivity and the foundation of almost every valuation and feasibility calculation.

**Potential Gross Income (PGI)** is the total rent the property would generate if it were 100% occupied at full asking rent with no concessions — every unit rented at the full asking rate.

**Effective Gross Revenue (EGR)** is PGI adjusted for real-world conditions. From PGI, you subtract vacancy loss (typically 5-7% for a stabilized rental residential building), collection loss (rent not collected from tenants who occupy but do not pay), and rent concessions (free rent periods offered to attract tenants). You then add miscellaneous income — parking revenue, storage fees, laundry, rooftop antenna leases, and similar ancillary sources.

**Operating Expenses** are the periodic costs required to keep the property operating: property taxes (often the largest single line item), property management fees (typically 3-5% of revenue), insurance, utilities for common areas, repairs and maintenance, cleaning, landscaping, and building staff. Debt service, capital expenditures, and depreciation are excluded from operating expenses — they are kept below the NOI line because they reflect financing and ownership decisions, not the property's operational productivity.

**NOI = Effective Gross Revenue − Operating Expenses**

A simplified NOI structure for a 180-unit rental building:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Potential Gross Income (180 units x $2,400/mo x 12) | $5,184,000 |
| Less vacancy (5.5%) | ($285,120) |
| Less collection loss (0.5%) | ($25,920) |
| Plus parking and misc income | $180,000 |
| **Effective Gross Revenue** | **$5,052,960** |
| Less property taxes | ($540,000) |
| Less management fee (4%) | ($202,118) |
| Less insurance, maintenance, utilities, staff | ($480,000) |
| **Net Operating Income** | **$3,830,842** |

### Cap Rates and Value

The capitalization rate (cap rate) is the relationship between a property's NOI and its market value: **Value = NOI ÷ Cap Rate**.

If the above building has a stabilized NOI of $3,830,842 and the prevailing market cap rate is 4.25%, the implied value is $3,830,842 ÷ 0.0425 = **$90,137,000**.

Cap rates are not arbitrary — they reflect the risk-free rate of return (proxied by Government of Canada bond yields), a risk premium for the asset class and submarket, and expected income growth. Lower cap rates mean higher valuations: investors pay more per dollar of NOI when the asset is perceived as lower risk or in high demand. GTA purpose-built rental cap rates have been trading in the 3.75–4.75% range in recent years, reflecting strong investor demand in supply-constrained markets.

For a developer, the relevant metric is **yield on cost**: Stabilized NOI ÷ Total Project Cost. If total project cost is $82,000,000 and stabilized NOI is $3,830,842, yield on cost is 4.67%. The **development spread** — yield on cost minus market cap rate — is 4.67% minus 4.25% = 42 basis points. Experienced developers typically target 100-200+ bps to justify the time, risk, and capital commitment of development versus simply acquiring an existing stabilized asset.

### Discounted Cash Flow and IRR

The cap rate approach values a property based on a single year's NOI. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) analysis extends this by projecting the full cash flow stream over an expected holding period — typically 5 to 10 years — and discounting each year's cash flow back to a present value using a required rate of return.

The logic is that a dollar received 5 years from now is worth less than a dollar today, because the dollar today could be invested and generate returns in the interim. DCF applies a discount rate — the investor's required return — to systematically reduce the value of each future cash flow to its present equivalent. The sum of all those discounted cash flows, minus the initial investment, is the Net Present Value (NPV). A positive NPV means the project is expected to generate returns above the required threshold.

The Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the discount rate at which the NPV of all cash flows equals zero. It is the actual return the investment is expected to generate. Investors compare the project IRR against their hurdle rate — if the expected IRR exceeds the hurdle, the project is worth pursuing.

In practice, DCF for a rental development project works as follows: year-by-year NOI is projected with modest annual rent growth (typically 2-3%), operating expenses escalate separately, and in the final year the property is sold at a capitalized value based on the terminal year NOI and an exit cap rate. The exit cap rate is typically set slightly higher than the entry cap rate to reflect the asset aging over the holding period. All projected cash flows are discounted at the required return and compared to total development cost.

IRR is the primary return metric used in LP/GP structures — it is what your equity investors are measuring their returns against, and it is what waterfall and promote provisions are structured around. Understanding how NOI growth, cap rate assumptions, and exit timing affect IRR is foundational to understanding why projects are structured the way they are.

## 1.8 Construction Loan Interest — Bank Prime and CORRA

The feasibility study must accurately model financing costs, and in Canada that means understanding how construction loan interest is priced and how it behaves over the life of the project. Construction loans in Canada are priced off one of two benchmarks: Bank of Canada Prime Rate or CORRA.

### Bank of Canada Prime Rate

The Prime Rate is the lending rate that Canada's chartered banks charge their most creditworthy customers. Each bank sets its own Prime Rate, but in practice all major banks move in lockstep with the Bank of Canada's overnight policy rate — currently 2.25% as of early 2026, resulting in a Prime Rate of 4.45%. Construction loans priced off Prime are expressed as Prime plus a spread — for example, Prime + 1.50% currently produces an all-in rate of approximately 5.95%. Prime-based pricing is straightforward to track because it changes only when the Bank of Canada moves its policy rate at scheduled announcement dates.

### CORRA

CORRA (Canadian Overnight Repo Rate Average) is Canada's risk-free benchmark rate, administered by the Bank of Canada. It measures the cost of overnight general collateral repo transactions secured by Government of Canada treasury bills and bonds. CORRA replaced CDOR (the Canadian Dollar Offered Rate — Canada's equivalent of LIBOR) as the standard benchmark for Canadian commercial loans. As of November 2023, all new commercial loan agreements in Canada must reference CORRA, Term CORRA, or Prime in place of the now-retired CDOR.

For loan pricing purposes, lenders use **Term CORRA** — a forward-looking version of CORRA based on CORRA futures markets. Term CORRA is available in two tenors: **1-Month** (the market's expectation of average overnight CORRA over the next 30 days) and **3-Month** (the market's expectation over the next 90 days). Both are set at the beginning of the interest period, meaning the borrower knows their rate before the period begins.

On a CORRA-based construction loan the all-in rate is: **Term CORRA (1M or 3M) + Lender Spread.** Lender spreads on well-structured GTA high-rise construction loans typically range from 150 to 300 basis points depending on project risk, LTC, and the borrower relationship.

### 1-Month vs. 3-Month Term CORRA

The choice between 1-month and 3-month Term CORRA affects how quickly rate changes flow through to the loan and therefore how your interest accrual behaves during the construction period.

**1-Month Term CORRA** resets every 30 days. In a declining rate environment, 1-month resets are advantageous — each new period reflects the lower rate quickly, reducing total interest cost. In a rising rate environment, 1-month resets are a disadvantage for the same reason — increases flow through fast.

**3-Month Term CORRA** resets every 90 days. In a rising rate environment, 3-month pricing locks in the current rate for a full quarter before the next increase flows through — providing 90 days of protection. In a falling rate environment, 3-month pricing means you continue paying the higher rate for up to 90 days after the market has moved lower.

| Rate Environment | More Favourable Choice | Reason |
|-----------------|----------------------|--------|
| Rates expected to fall | 1-Month Term CORRA | Faster reset captures rate decreases sooner |
| Rates expected to rise | 3-Month Term CORRA | Locks in current lower rate for 90 days |
| Rates stable or uncertain | 1-Month Term CORRA | More flexibility; reassess every 30 days |

The 3-month tenor also tends to trade 5 to 20 basis points above 1-month in a normal yield curve environment because markets price in a small premium for the longer commitment period. On a $100M construction facility at a spread of 2.00%, a 25 basis point difference in the benchmark rate equals $250,000 in annualized interest cost — meaningful, not transformational, but worth understanding when reviewing loan documents and modeling the interest reserve.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Monthly draw packages include an interest accrual line calculated by the lender against the drawn balance and the applicable Term CORRA rate for that period. When you reconcile the interest line in the draw report against the project's interest reserve, you are working directly with this calculation. Knowing whether your loan is on 1-month or 3-month CORRA — and understanding how the rate resets — helps you anticipate whether the interest reserve is tracking on budget or whether a rate movement in the prior period will show up as a variance in the current draw.

## 1.9 Key Metrics Reference

| Metric | Definition |
|--------|-----------|
| Vacancy Rate | Unoccupied available space as a % of total inventory. Trend direction matters as much as the absolute number. |
| Net Absorption | New space leased minus space vacated in a period. The most accurate measure of true demand growth. |
| Absorption Rate | Units or SF leased/sold per month. Drives lease-up timeline and revenue ramp assumptions. |
| Potential Gross Income | Total revenue if the property were 100% occupied at full asking rent with no concessions. |
| Effective Gross Revenue | PGI adjusted for vacancy, collection loss, and concessions, plus miscellaneous income. |
| Net Operating Income (NOI) | Effective Gross Revenue minus operating expenses. Excludes debt service and capital expenditures. |
| Cap Rate | NOI divided by asset value (or value = NOI ÷ cap rate). Reflects risk, location, and income growth expectations. |
| Yield on Cost | Stabilized NOI divided by total project cost. Must exceed the prevailing cap rate to justify development risk. |
| Development Spread | Yield on cost minus market cap rate. Typically needs to be 100-200+ bps to be attractive. |
| DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) | Multi-year valuation method that discounts projected annual cash flows and terminal sale proceeds to present value. |
| IRR (Internal Rate of Return) | The discount rate at which NPV of all project cash flows equals zero. Primary return metric for equity investors. |
| NPV (Net Present Value) | Sum of discounted future cash flows minus initial investment. Positive NPV means returns exceed the required threshold. |
| Stabilized Occupancy | The long-term occupancy a project sustains once fully leased. Typically 93-95% for residential rental. |
| Residual Land Value | Land value implied by working backward from projected revenues minus all costs and required profit. |
| FSR / FAR | Floor Space Ratio / Floor Area Ratio. Total buildable area divided by site area. Determines permitted density. |
| Bank Prime Rate | The lending rate set by Canadian chartered banks, moving in lockstep with the Bank of Canada overnight policy rate. |
| Term CORRA | Forward-looking risk-free benchmark rate for Canadian commercial loans. Available in 1-month and 3-month tenors. |
| Lender Spread | Basis points added above the benchmark rate (Prime or CORRA) to reflect credit risk, project risk, and lender margin. |

## AE-1.2 — Evaluating Demand vs. Supply

**Step 1 — Establish Your Delivery Timeline**

Work backward from a realistic construction schedule. Site acquisition and closing takes 3 months, entitlements and permits take 9 months, construction takes 22 months. Delivery is approximately 34 months from today — 3 years. That is the market you are underwriting.

**Step 2 — Quantify Demand Growth Over the Period**

Net migration: 8,400 people/year ÷ 2.1 persons per household = 4,000 new renter households/year x 3 years = 12,000. Employment demand: 1,200 new jobs x 60% net new renters = 720. Total estimated demand: **~12,700 new renter households**.

**Step 3 — Quantify Supply Additions**

Project A: 200 units (confirmed), Project B: 120 units (probable), plus a 20% unknown pipeline buffer = **384 units of competing supply** before your project delivers. Your project adds 150 units for a total supply addition of ~534 units over 3 years.

**Step 4 — Compare**

| Metric | Figure |
|--------|--------|
| New Renter Households (3yr) | ~12,700 |
| New Rental Units Added (3yr) | ~534 |
| Demand-to-Supply Ratio | ~24:1 |

The ratio confirms substantial market capacity. However — a strong demand-to-supply ratio does not automatically mean the project is viable. You still need to confirm that the rents the market supports are sufficient to justify land cost, construction cost, and financing cost at an acceptable return.

## AE-1.3 — Positioning a Mixed-Use Condo Project: Full Cost Stack

**Project Assumptions:** 180-unit mixed-use condo tower, ground-floor retail (5,000 sf), urban infill site. Land value $18,000,000. Total gross construction area approximately 140,000 sf.

**Full Cost Stack Summary**

| Category | Amount |
|----------|--------|
| Land (all-in) | $19,583,000 |
| Municipal Fees | $14,120,000 |
| Construction (all divisions + CM) | $60,590,000 |
| Design Consultants | $3,150,000 |
| Legal & Administration | $2,600,000 |
| Marketing & Leasing | $677,000 |
| Financing Costs | $9,730,000 |
| Operating (Lease-Up) | $1,200,000 |
| Contingency | $3,700,000 |
| **Total Project Cost** | **~$115,350,000** |

Municipal fees alone represent approximately **12.2% of total project cost**.

**Revenue and Profit Analysis**

| Scenario | Net Revenue | Profit | Margin |
|----------|-------------|--------|--------|

**Conclusion:** At $1,075/sf the project produces a 22% margin — viable but at the lower end of institutional requirements. At $950/sf the margin drops to 8%, at which point the project does not proceed without renegotiating the land price. The $18M land value is only defensible if $1,075/sf pricing holds.

## AE-1.5 — Mixed-Use HBU Analysis

**Residual Land Value = Net Revenue − (Hard Costs + Soft Costs + Financing) − Required Profit**

| Scenario | Residual Land Value |
|----------|-------------------|
| Continue Commercial Use (NOI $180K capitalized at 6.0%) | ~$2,400,000 |
| Purpose-Built Rental at 4.5 FSR (NOI $2,642,400 capitalized at 4.25%) | ~$9,060,000 |
| Mixed-Use Residential + Ground Floor Retail (blended 4.35% cap) | ~$9,627,000 |

**Conclusion:** Mixed-use produces the highest residual land value. The premium over rental-only is modest ($567,000), reflecting the real-world dynamic that retail adds value but not dramatically at this density. The far more significant finding is that continuing commercial use destroys approximately $7,200,000 in potential land value relative to either development scenario — a number that dwarfs any renovation cost savings.

## Chapter Summary

Market analysis and feasibility are not bureaucratic steps in the development process — they are the intellectual foundation on which every project decision is built. The core analytical sequence is: screen the idea quickly with a back-of-the-envelope test, confirm demand fundamentals through supply and demand analysis and comparable properties, model absorption conservatively, test the site's HBU objectively, and then build a formal feasibility study that satisfies not just the developer's return requirements but those of every participant whose commitment is needed. The financial tools — NOI, cap rates, DCF, and IRR — translate market observations into numbers that lenders and investors can evaluate. And the financing structure that underlies the project — including how construction loan interest is priced off Bank Prime or Term CORRA — is itself a material input into feasibility that must be understood and modeled accurately. A developer who commands all of these tools and applies them with intellectual honesty will consistently make better decisions than one who commands only parts of the picture.
