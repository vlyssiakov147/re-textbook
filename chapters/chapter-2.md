# Chapter 2: Site Selection & Due Diligence

A development project lives or dies by the quality of the site it is built on. Location is the most cited factor in real estate value, but site selection is far more than choosing a desirable neighbourhood. It is a rigorous, multi-disciplinary investigation that determines whether a site is physically buildable, legally clear, environmentally safe, and financially viable before a developer commits to acquiring it. Due diligence is the process of uncovering everything the seller either does not know or would prefer you not to find out.

Developers who skip or rush due diligence are not being efficient — they are transferring risk onto themselves. Every problem discovered after closing is a problem the developer owns entirely. Every problem discovered during due diligence is a problem that can be priced, negotiated, or walked away from.

---

## 2.1 What is Site Selection?

Site selection is the process of identifying, evaluating, and ultimately choosing the land on which a development project will be built. It is rarely a single decision — it is an iterative filtering process that begins broadly and narrows through successive rounds of analysis.

The starting point is always the development program: what does the developer intend to build? A 500-unit purpose-built rental tower has very different site requirements than a 40-unit boutique condo, a grocery-anchored retail centre, or an industrial logistics facility. The program determines the minimum site size, the required density permissions, the infrastructure capacity needed, and the target location characteristics. Site selection works backward from the program to find sites that can accommodate it.

Developers typically evaluate many sites for every one they acquire. A disciplined acquisition team might review 50 opportunities, conduct preliminary analysis on 15, complete detailed due diligence on 5, and close on 1. The filtering happens across three broad stages.

**Stage 1 — Macro Screening**

The first filter is market and location. Is this submarket one where the developer wants to build? Is there demonstrated demand for the intended product type? Are land values and construction costs at levels where the project can pencil? This stage uses secondary data — brokerage reports, municipal planning documents, demographic data, and internal market knowledge — and takes days rather than weeks.

**Stage 2 — Site-Specific Screening**

The second filter evaluates specific sites within the target area against the development program. Key questions at this stage include: Is the site large enough? What does current zoning permit, and is there a realistic path to any rezoning required? Are there obvious physical constraints — irregular shape, steep topography, visible environmental issues? Who owns it, and is there any indication they might sell? This stage is still largely desktop research but may include a site walkthrough.

**Stage 3 — Full Due Diligence**

The third filter is a comprehensive, multi-disciplinary investigation of the shortlisted site. This is what most people mean when they say "due diligence" — the formal process of commissioning third-party reports, reviewing title, confirming zoning, assessing environmental conditions, evaluating geotechnical and servicing capacity, and tying all findings back into a revised feasibility model. This stage takes weeks to months and costs money. It is typically conducted under a conditional purchase agreement that gives the developer the right to walk away if findings are unsatisfactory.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Site selection and due diligence is primarily driven by senior development staff — VPs, Directors, and Principals who have the market relationships and strategic judgment to identify and pursue opportunities. At your stage, the most common point of entry is in the Stage 3 due diligence process, where you may be asked to track the status of reports, review and summarize consultant findings, update the feasibility model as new information comes in, and flag items that require senior attention. Understanding what each report is looking for and why it matters is what makes you useful in that process.

## 2.2 Evaluating a Site — The Physical Assessment

Before commissioning any formal reports, an experienced developer walks the site and conducts a physical assessment with their own eyes. This is not a substitute for professional reports — it is a preliminary filter that can identify obvious deal-killers quickly and inform the scope of the formal investigation.

**Size and Shape**

The site must be large enough to accommodate the development program with appropriate setbacks, parking, landscaping, and amenity space. Irregular shapes — narrow lots, L-shaped parcels, sites with significant notches or easement intrusions — constrain building footprints and can significantly reduce developable area relative to the nominal lot size. A site that appears adequate on paper may be functionally undersized once constraints are mapped.

**Topography**

Significant grade changes across a site affect excavation costs, structural design, and drainage. A steeply sloping site may require extensive cut-and-fill earthworks or retaining walls. For below-grade parking in particular, topography determines whether excavation is straightforward or whether complex and expensive shoring and dewatering systems are required. The 9700 Yonge Street project, for example, required H-pile and lagging shoring across the entire perimeter and a sophisticated dewatering system due to groundwater found at shallow depth — both significant cost items driven by site-specific physical conditions.

**Access and Frontage**

How does the site connect to the public road network? A site with a single narrow access point creates constraints for construction logistics, emergency vehicle access, and long-term building operations. Corner sites with multiple frontages are generally more valuable and more flexible. Rear-lot sites with laneway access present different challenges for loading, garbage collection, and emergency access.

**Adjacent Uses and Surroundings**

What surrounds the site? Industrial uses to the north may create noise or odour issues that affect residential livability and require mitigation measures. A neighbouring property with a right of way across your site could restrict what you build and where. Power lines, telecommunications infrastructure, and underground utilities crossing or adjacent to the site all require investigation. A cemetery, ravine, or heritage property abutting the site may create setback requirements or view protection policies that reduce buildable envelope.

**Existing Structures**

If the site has existing buildings, they will need to be demolished or incorporated. Demolition cost and timing must be accounted for in the budget. If the existing building is tenanted, there may be lease obligations, tenant relocation requirements, or rental housing replacement obligations under municipal policy that extend the pre-construction timeline and add cost.

> **Key Concept — Condominium Conversion vs. Redevelopment**
> In many Canadian municipalities, if a site contains existing rental housing, the developer may be required to replace all rental units demolished on a one-for-one basis at similar rents as a condition of redevelopment approval. This obligation can fundamentally change the economics of a project and must be identified early in the site selection process, not after a purchase offer has been submitted.

**Worked Example — Physical Site Assessment Red Flags:** Your firm is evaluating a 1.2-acre urban infill site currently occupied by an older two-storey commercial building. The site is L-shaped, with the longer leg fronting the main street and a shorter leg extending behind an adjacent property. On a site walkthrough, you observe that the shorter leg is subject to a hydro easement running across it, which will restrict building within that area. The site also slopes approximately 3 metres from front to back. The adjacent property to the east is an operating auto repair shop. You note potential soil contamination risk from the auto repair use, a constrained building footprint due to the easement, and meaningful excavation costs due to the grade change and potential groundwater issues given the topography. All three items get flagged for follow-up in the formal due diligence scope.

## 2.3 Title, Ownership & Legal Due Diligence

Before a developer can build anything, they need to own the land — or at least have a secure legal interest in it. Title due diligence is the process of confirming that the seller actually has the right to sell what they are purporting to sell, and identifying any encumbrances, restrictions, or third-party interests that will travel with the land after closing.

**Title Search**

A title search is conducted by the developer's legal counsel using the provincial land registry. It reveals the chain of ownership of the property, any registered mortgages or liens against it, easements and rights of way that have been registered on title, restrictive covenants that limit how the land can be used, and any outstanding writs or legal proceedings that could affect title.

> **Key Concept — Easements and Rights of Way**
> An easement is a registered right that gives a third party the ability to use a portion of the property for a specific purpose. Utility easements are the most common — they give Hydro, Bell, or the municipality the right to access and maintain underground infrastructure running beneath the property. An easement does not prevent development entirely, but it restricts what can be built within the easement zone and who controls access to it. A developer who builds over an easement without consent from the easement holder may be required to demolish the encroaching structure at their own cost.

**Ownership Structure**

Who actually owns the property? In residential or small commercial transactions, ownership is usually straightforward. In larger development land transactions, the vendor may be a corporation, a partnership, a numbered company, or a trust — and determining who has the authority to sell, whether there are partners or beneficiaries whose consent is required, and whether there are any corporate encumbrances or tax obligations that could cloud the transaction requires careful legal review.

**Vendor Representation and Warranties**

Purchase agreements for development land typically include representations and warranties from the vendor — statements about the condition of the property, the absence of known environmental issues, the validity of any existing approvals, and the accuracy of information disclosed. Understanding what the vendor is and is not warranting is important because it determines what risk remains with the developer after closing. Vendors of development land often attempt to sell on an "as is, where is" basis with minimal representations, which shifts the burden of discovery entirely onto the purchaser's due diligence process.

**Title Insurance**

Title insurance is a policy that protects the purchaser against losses arising from defects in title that were not discovered during the title search — including survey errors, fraud, and encumbrances that were not registered at the time of the search. It is a standard closing requirement in most development land transactions and is typically required by the construction lender as well.

**Survey**

An as-built survey confirms the exact boundaries of the property, the location of any existing structures relative to those boundaries, encroachments onto or from adjacent properties, and the registered location of any easements or rights of way. Survey discrepancies — where the actual boundary differs from the registered boundary — are more common than most people expect, particularly on older urban lots that have been subdivided or assembled over time.

> **Common Problem — Assuming the Site Area**
> Developers sometimes rely on the municipal property assessment or a prior listing's stated lot area without commissioning their own survey. This is a meaningful risk. A site that is listed as 1.5 acres but surveys at 1.35 acres can support materially less density, which affects the entire financial model. Always verify actual site area through a current survey before finalizing the acquisition and development program.

**Worked Example — Title Encumbrance Discovery:** Your firm is in due diligence on a downtown mixed-use site. The title search reveals a drainage easement registered in 1962 running diagonally across the rear third of the property, granting the City of Toronto the right to access and maintain a storm sewer running beneath the site. The easement is 6 metres wide and follows the alignment of the pipe. Your architect confirms that the proposed tower footprint would encroach into the easement zone. The options are: (1) redesign the building footprint to avoid the easement, which reduces the tower floor plate by approximately 8%; (2) apply to the City to relocate the easement, which is possible but adds 12-18 months and significant cost; or (3) negotiate a price reduction with the vendor that reflects the reduced development potential. The finding does not kill the deal but materially affects the program and price.

## 2.4 Environmental Due Diligence (Phase 1 & 2 ESAs)

Environmental due diligence is one of the most consequential components of site due diligence for urban development. Contaminated soil or groundwater on a development site can add millions of dollars in remediation cost, delay a project by years, and in extreme cases make a site undevelopable entirely. The standard framework for environmental assessment in Canada follows a two-phase process.

**Phase 1 Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)**

A Phase 1 ESA is a desktop and site reconnaissance study — it does not involve any soil or groundwater sampling. Its purpose is to identify Recognized Environmental Conditions (RECs) — evidence or indicators that hazardous substances may have been released on or near the site that could affect the property.

The Phase 1 process involves reviewing historical records (aerial photographs, fire insurance maps, historical directories, building permits) to understand what uses have occupied the site over its history, interviewing people familiar with the site, reviewing regulatory databases for any reported spills or remediation orders, and conducting a site walkthrough to observe current conditions and any visible indicators of contamination.

The output is a report that lists any RECs found and makes a recommendation on whether a Phase 2 investigation is warranted. A Phase 1 with no RECs is a relatively clean result — though it is not a guarantee of clean soil, only a statement that no indicators of contamination were found through desktop and visual review.

> **Key Concept — What Triggers Contamination Risk**
> The single most reliable predictor of contamination risk is historical land use. Certain uses are consistently associated with hazardous substances: gas stations and fuel storage (petroleum hydrocarbons and BTEX compounds), dry cleaners (chlorinated solvents), auto repair shops (oils, solvents, heavy metals), industrial manufacturing, rail yards, and former landfill sites. In dense urban areas where land has changed hands and uses many times over decades, finding some historical contaminating use in the chain is extremely common — which is why Phase 1 ESAs are standard practice rather than optional.

**Phase 2 Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)**

If the Phase 1 identifies RECs, the next step is a Phase 2 ESA, which involves physical investigation of the soil and groundwater. Boreholes and monitoring wells are drilled at strategic locations across the site, soil and groundwater samples are collected and sent to a laboratory, and the results are compared against Ontario's applicable site condition standards to determine whether contamination is present and whether it exceeds acceptable limits for the intended use.

The Phase 2 report will characterize the nature and extent of any contamination found, identify which contaminants are present and at what concentrations, and recommend whether remediation is required and if so, what approach is appropriate.

**Remediation Approaches**

If contamination is found that exceeds acceptable standards, there are several remediation approaches depending on the type and extent of contamination:

1. **Excavation and disposal** — contaminated soil is physically removed and transported to a licensed disposal facility. This is the most common approach for localized contamination. Cost depends heavily on the volume of material and its classification under provincial regulations — from a few hundred thousand dollars for a small localized exceedance to several million for a heavily contaminated urban site.
2. **In-situ treatment** — contaminants are treated in place using chemical injection, bioremediation, or other techniques without excavating soil. Appropriate for certain contaminant types and can be less disruptive, but takes longer.
3. **Risk assessment** — in some cases, contamination that exceeds generic site condition standards can be managed through a site-specific risk assessment that demonstrates the contamination does not pose unacceptable risk given the intended use and exposure pathways. This approach avoids or reduces remediation but adds time and regulatory complexity.

> **Common Problem — Underestimating Remediation Cost and Timeline**
> Remediation budgets are notoriously difficult to estimate before the full extent of contamination is known. A Phase 2 may identify contamination in one area of the site, but excavation frequently reveals that the contaminated zone extends further than the borings suggested. Developers who budget for remediation based on Phase 2 findings alone often find actual costs are 30-100% higher once excavation begins. Always apply a meaningful contingency to remediation estimates and ensure the purchase agreement allows for price adjustments if remediation costs exceed a defined threshold.

The 9700 Yonge Street project that you work with provides a real example: exceedance of PHC (Petroleum Hydrocarbon Compounds) and VOC (Volatile Organic Compounds) was found on site at 1 to 3 metres depth on the east side of the site. This triggered a targeted remediation scope that had to be planned into the construction sequence and budgeted accordingly.

**Worked Example — Phase 2 ESA Finding:** A Phase 1 ESA for a proposed mixed-use residential site identifies two RECs: a former dry cleaner that occupied the ground floor of a commercial building on the site from 1967 to 1989, and an underground fuel storage tank associated with a heating system that was decommissioned in 1995. A Phase 2 ESA is commissioned. Soil sampling near the former dry cleaner location identifies chlorinated solvents (PCE and TCE) at concentrations exceeding Ontario's residential site condition standards to a depth of 4 metres. Sampling near the tank shows residual petroleum hydrocarbons but at concentrations below the applicable threshold. The Phase 2 recommends excavation and disposal of approximately 850 cubic metres of contaminated soil at an estimated cost of $680,000, with a note that the estimate carries a +/- 40% uncertainty range.

## 2.5 Geotechnical & Servicing Assessment

**Geotechnical Investigation**

A geotechnical investigation examines the soil and bedrock conditions beneath a site to determine the appropriate foundation system for the proposed development and to quantify the cost and complexity of below-grade construction. This is particularly important for high-rise development in urban markets, where deep excavation for multi-level underground parking is standard.

The investigation involves drilling boreholes across the site, collecting soil and rock samples at various depths, conducting in-situ testing, and measuring groundwater levels. The output is a geotechnical report that describes the soil profile, recommends an appropriate foundation system, provides design parameters for shoring and excavation, and assesses groundwater conditions and dewatering requirements.

**Foundation Systems**

The most significant output of the geotechnical report for cost purposes is the foundation system recommendation:

1. **Conventional spread footings or mat foundation** — appropriate where competent bearing soils are found at shallow depth. The least expensive option.
2. **Driven piles** — steel or concrete piles driven to bearing capacity at depth. Used where near-surface soils are inadequate.
3. **Drilled cast-in-place (CIP) piles or caissons** — bored holes filled with reinforced concrete. Common in urban high-rise construction.
4. **Raft slab on ground improvement** — a thick reinforced concrete mat combined with soil densification techniques. Used on challenging sites. The 9700 Yonge Street project used a raft slab system ranging from 700mm to 1,400mm in thickness, combined with CFA piles and tiedown anchors to manage hydrostatic pressure — a complex and expensive solution driven by the specific geotechnical conditions on that site.

**Shoring and Dewatering**

For any project with below-grade construction, the geotechnical report will also address shoring — the temporary support system that holds back adjacent soil and structures during excavation — and dewatering — the management of groundwater encountered during excavation.

Shoring systems range from simple soldier pile and lagging walls to complex tieback systems and secant pile walls depending on excavation depth, adjacent structures, and soil conditions. Dewatering can range from simple sump pumps for minor groundwater to sophisticated well-point systems for sites with high groundwater tables. Both are significant cost variables that are site-specific and cannot be assumed from benchmarks alone.

> **Key Concept — Groundwater and its Impact on Cost**
> High groundwater is one of the most common sources of unexpected cost escalation on urban development projects. When groundwater is encountered at shallow depth, it must be managed continuously throughout the excavation and construction period, and the below-grade structure must be designed to resist the hydrostatic pressure permanently. The 9700 Yonge Street project found groundwater at 0.58 to 5.0 metres depth with an estimated steady-state dewatering requirement of 280,700 litres per day — a substantial ongoing cost during construction.

**Servicing Assessment**

A servicing assessment determines whether the municipal infrastructure surrounding the site — water, sanitary sewer, storm sewer, and dry utilities — has sufficient capacity to support the proposed development, and what connection costs and off-site works may be required.

For large developments, the City may require the developer to fund upgrades to trunk infrastructure — water mains, sanitary sewers, or stormwater management facilities — that extend well beyond the project boundary. These costs can be substantial and are not always captured in the development charges calculation. They need to be identified and budgeted during due diligence, not after permits are issued.

> **Common Problem — Assuming Servicing Capacity Exists**
> Developers sometimes assume that because the site is in an urban area with existing infrastructure nearby, connecting services will be straightforward and inexpensive. This is not always true. Urban infill sites in areas with aging infrastructure may require significant main replacements or upgrades. High-density developments in areas not originally planned for that intensity may require trunk sewer or water main upgrades that the municipality will require the developer to fund as a condition of development approval. Always commission a servicing assessment from a civil engineer early in the due diligence process.

## 2.6 Zoning & Planning Confirmation

Understanding what a site is permitted to do — and what approvals will be required to do what you intend — is a critical component of due diligence. Zoning and planning confirmation connects the physical site to the regulatory framework that governs what can be built on it.

**Current Zoning**

Every parcel of land in an Ontario municipality is assigned a zoning designation under the municipality's zoning bylaw. The zoning designation specifies: the permitted uses (residential, commercial, industrial, mixed-use, etc.), the maximum density (expressed as floor space ratio or maximum gross floor area), the maximum building height, required setbacks from property lines, minimum parking requirements, and other development standards.

The developer must confirm that the current zoning either permits the intended development as-of-right, or that a realistic path exists to obtain the necessary rezoning. An as-of-right development — one that conforms to existing zoning — avoids the time and cost of a rezoning application and the risk of approval being denied or conditioned.

**Rezoning Applications**

When the intended development requires density, height, or uses beyond what current zoning permits, the developer must apply to the municipality for a zoning bylaw amendment. In Ontario, this process involves submitting a planning application package that includes a planning justification report, traffic study, shadow study, urban design guidelines, and various other technical reports depending on the municipality and the scale of the application.

The rezoning process in major Ontario municipalities — particularly Toronto — can take anywhere from 18 months to 4 or more years, depending on complexity, political context, and whether the application is appealed to the Ontario Land Tribunal. This timeline uncertainty is one of the most significant risks in development, and it must be factored into the financial model through conservative timeline assumptions and appropriate contingency.

> **Common Problem — Buying on Rezoning Upside That Does Not Materialize**
> One of the most common and costly errors in development is paying a land price that is justified only by a rezoning that the developer has not yet received — and then failing to obtain it, or receiving approval only after a multi-year delay that erodes the project economics. The risk is not just time — during a rezoning process, market conditions can shift, interest rates can change, construction costs can escalate, and the project that made sense when the land was acquired may no longer pencil once approval is finally in hand. Always model the base case on current as-of-right permissions, with the rezoning upside modeled separately under a clearly labeled optimistic scenario.

**Official Plan Conformity**

In Ontario, a municipality's Official Plan is the highest-level land use planning document, providing the long-term vision for how land in the city will develop. Zoning bylaws must conform to the Official Plan. A rezoning application that is consistent with the Official Plan is considerably more defensible and more likely to succeed than one that requires an Official Plan Amendment as well — which is a separate, higher-order approval process.

**Site Plan Approval**

Even for as-of-right developments, most municipalities require site plan approval before a building permit is issued. Site plan approval is the process through which the municipality reviews and approves the detailed design of the building and site — landscaping, grading, drainage, loading, garbage collection, exterior design, and the technical interface between the building and the public realm. Site plan approval is generally less contentious than rezoning but still takes time — typically 6-18 months depending on the municipality and the complexity of the project.

## 2.7 Financial Due Diligence — Tying it Back to Feasibility

The findings of the physical, legal, environmental, geotechnical, and planning due diligence investigations do not exist in isolation — they must be translated into financial terms and incorporated into the feasibility model. This is where due diligence and financial analysis converge.

**The Revised Feasibility Model**

As due diligence findings come in, the feasibility model must be updated to reflect them. The discovery of contamination adds a remediation budget line and extends the pre-construction timeline. A geotechnical finding that requires a more expensive foundation system increases the hard cost estimate. A title encumbrance that reduces the buildable floor plate reduces the revenue projection. A rezoning requirement that adds 24 months to the entitlement timeline increases carrying costs and defers the revenue stream.

Each finding must be quantified — at least in order-of-magnitude terms — and the model updated before the developer makes the final acquisition decision. The due diligence period exists precisely for this purpose: to refine the financial assumptions from the initial feasibility study into a more accurate picture of the actual project.

**The Go/No-Go Decision**

At the end of the due diligence period, the developer must make a binary decision: proceed with the acquisition on the negotiated terms, renegotiate the purchase price or terms to reflect the findings, or exercise the right to terminate the agreement and recover the deposit.

This decision should be driven by a clear analytical framework: does the project still meet the required return thresholds after incorporating all due diligence findings into the feasibility model? If yes, proceed. If not, either renegotiate to adjust the land price downward to restore returns, or walk away.

> **Key Concept — The Price Chip**
> "Chipping" the price is the practice of using due diligence findings as leverage to renegotiate the purchase price downward. A developer who discovers $800,000 of unexpected remediation cost during Phase 2 investigation has a legitimate basis to request a corresponding reduction in the purchase price. Whether the vendor agrees depends on negotiation dynamics, the competitiveness of the market, and how motivated they are to close — but the finding provides a documented, defensible basis for the request that a vendor cannot easily dismiss.

**Worked Example — Go/No-Go Decision:** Your firm has been in due diligence for 45 days on a mixed-use infill site under a conditional agreement at $14,500,000. The initial feasibility study at that price projected a 21% profit margin. Due diligence has produced three findings: (1) the Phase 2 ESA identified contamination requiring estimated remediation of $650,000 with a 40% uncertainty range; (2) the geotechnical report recommended a more expensive raft slab foundation rather than the conventional spread footings assumed in the initial estimate, adding approximately $1,200,000 to the hard cost budget; and (3) the title search revealed an easement that reduces the net buildable floor plate by 6%, which reduces projected revenue by approximately $3,100,000. The combined impact is a reduction in profit margin from 21% to approximately 12% — below the 18% threshold the firm requires. The development team now has a choice: chip the price or walk away.

## 2.8 Key Metrics & Terms Reference

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| REC | Recognized Environmental Condition — a finding in a Phase 1 ESA indicating potential contamination requiring further investigation. |
| Phase 1 ESA | Desktop and site reconnaissance study to identify RECs. No soil sampling involved. |
| Phase 2 ESA | Physical soil and groundwater investigation triggered by Phase 1 RECs. Involves drilling, sampling, and lab analysis. |
| Remediation | The process of cleaning up contaminated soil or groundwater to meet applicable standards. |
| Site Condition Standards | Ontario's regulatory benchmarks (under O. Reg. 153/04) for acceptable concentrations of contaminants in soil and groundwater, varying by intended land use. |
| Easement | A registered right giving a third party use of a portion of the property for a specific purpose. Travels with the land on sale. |
| Right of Way | Similar to an easement — a right to cross or use a strip of land, often for utilities or access. |
| Geotechnical Report | Engineering study of subsurface soil and rock conditions informing foundation design and excavation approach. |
| Shoring | Temporary structural support system holding back soil and adjacent structures during excavation. |
| Dewatering | Management and removal of groundwater encountered during excavation. |
| As-of-Right | Development that conforms to existing zoning without requiring a rezoning application. |
| Zoning Bylaw Amendment | Application to the municipality to change the zoning designation of a property to permit a different use or greater density. |
| Official Plan Amendment | Higher-order planning application to change the municipality's long-term land use designation. Required when rezoning conflicts with the Official Plan. |
| Site Plan Approval | Municipal review of detailed building and site design, required before building permit issuance even for as-of-right projects. |
| Title Insurance | Insurance policy protecting against losses from title defects not discovered during the title search. |
| Price Chip | The practice of using due diligence findings to negotiate a reduction in the agreed purchase price. |
| Conditional Agreement | Purchase agreement that gives the buyer the right to terminate and recover their deposit if due diligence findings are unsatisfactory within a defined period. |

## Applied Exercises

### AE-2.2 — Physical Site Assessment: Translating Constraints into Cost

This exercise carries forward the worked example from Section 2.2. You have identified three physical constraints on the L-shaped infill site: a hydro easement across the shorter leg, a 3-metre grade change from front to back, and a neighbouring auto repair shop to the east suggesting potential soil contamination risk.

**Step 1 — Quantify the Easement Impact on Buildable Area**

The site is L-shaped with a total area of 1.2 acres (52,272 sf). The shorter leg where the easement runs represents approximately 18% of total site area — roughly 9,409 sf. The hydro easement is 5 metres wide running across the full width of the shorter leg, removing approximately 3,800 sf from the buildable footprint.

Assuming an FSR of 4.5 on a site that was 52,272 sf, the theoretical maximum GFA would be 235,224 sf. With the easement constraint reducing the effective site area, the practical buildable envelope is reduced to approximately 228,900 sf — a reduction of about 2.7%. On a project pricing at $1,000/sf condo, this represents approximately $6,300,000 in lost revenue potential.

**Step 2 — Quantify the Grade Change Impact on Excavation Cost**

A 3-metre grade change across a site creates differential excavation requirements. For a 2-level underground parking structure with a target underside of slab elevation, the front of the building may require 6 metres of excavation while the rear requires only 3 metres. This differential increases the volume of material to be removed and the complexity of the shoring system.

| Scenario | Excavation Volume | Estimated Cost |
|----------|-----------------|----------------|
| Flat site (baseline) | ~18,000 m³ | ~$720,000 |
| 3m grade change (actual) | ~23,500 m³ | ~$940,000 |
| Premium for grade | +5,500 m³ | **+$220,000** |

Additionally, the grade change increases the likelihood of encountering groundwater on the lower (rear) portion of the site, which may require dewatering — add a provisional allowance of $150,000-$300,000 depending on Phase 2 findings.

**Step 3 — Flag the Contamination Risk for Phase 2 Scope**

The neighbouring auto repair shop is an active REC that must be included in the Phase 2 ESA scope. The investigation should include boreholes along the shared property boundary to the east to determine whether any migration of petroleum hydrocarbons, heavy metals, or solvents has occurred onto the subject site. Until Phase 2 results are available, the contamination risk should be flagged as an open item with a provisional budget of $200,000-$800,000 depending on findings.

**Updated Feasibility Impact Summary**

| Finding | Estimated Cost Impact |
|---------|----------------------|
| Easement — lost revenue | ($6,300,000) |
| Excavation premium — grade change | +$220,000 cost |
| Dewatering provisional | +$200,000 cost |
| Contamination provisional | +$200,000-$800,000 cost |
| **Total adverse impact range** | **($6,920,000) to ($7,520,000)** |

**Conclusion:** These findings do not kill the project but they materially affect the economics. The land price assumed in the original feasibility model needs to be revisited downward by an amount that restores the required return, accounting for these now-identified costs. This is exactly the basis for a price chip negotiation with the vendor.

| Item | Base Case | Revised |

### AE-2.4 — Phase 2 ESA Finding: Incorporating Remediation into the Feasibility Model

The Phase 2 ESA identified chlorinated solvent contamination requiring excavation and disposal of approximately 850 cubic metres of soil at an estimated cost of $680,000, with a +/- 40% uncertainty range.

**Step 1 — Establish the Cost Range**

| Scenario | Remediation Cost |
|----------|-----------------|
| Optimistic (-40%) | $408,000 |
| Base estimate | $680,000 |
| Conservative (+40%) | $952,000 |

**Step 2 — Model the Timeline Impact**

Soil remediation during excavation adds time to the construction schedule. Contaminated soil must be segregated from clean fill, manifested under provincial regulations, and transported to a licensed disposal facility — a slower and more administratively complex process than standard excavation. For 850 m³ of contaminated material, allow 3-4 weeks of schedule impact.

A 4-week construction delay on a project with a $65M construction loan at 7.5% per annum adds approximately:
$65,000,000 x 7.5% / 52 weeks x 4 weeks = **$375,000 in additional interest cost**

**Step 3 — Total Remediation Impact**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Base remediation cost | $680,000 |
| Schedule delay interest (4 weeks) | $375,000 |
| Contingency for scope expansion (20%) | $136,000 |
| **Total remediation impact (base case)** | **$1,191,000** |
| Conservative case (scope expands 40%) | ~$1,700,000 |

**Step 4 — Price Chip Justification**

The developer has a documented, third-party-supported finding showing $1.2M-$1.7M of unexpected cost not present in the original feasibility. This is legitimate grounds to request a price reduction of $1,000,000-$1,500,000 from the vendor. The vendor may push back, claiming the contamination was disclosed (or not), but the Phase 2 report provides the objective basis for the negotiation.

**Conclusion:** Environmental findings of this nature are relatively common on urban infill sites with industrial or commercial history. The key discipline is translating the finding into a specific dollar impact — including both direct remediation cost and indirect schedule impacts — before entering the price negotiation. A vague claim that "there is contamination" is far less powerful in a negotiation than a report showing $1.2M of documented cost with a credible methodology.

### AE-2.7 — Go/No-Go Decision: The Full Analysis

This exercise works through the go/no-go decision from the worked example in Section 2.7 in full. The initial feasibility projected a 21% profit margin at a $14,500,000 land price. Three findings have emerged from due diligence.

**Step 1 — Quantify All Findings**

| Finding | Financial Impact |
|---------|-----------------|
| Phase 2 ESA — remediation ($650,000 base, 40% uncertainty) | +$910,000 cost (base + 40% contingency) |
| Geotechnical — raft slab foundation upgrade | +$1,200,000 cost |
| Easement — 6% reduction in floor plate | ($2,580,000) net profit impact (revenue loss offset by cost reduction) |
| **Total adverse impact** | **($4,690,000) reduction in profit** |

**Step 2 — Revised Profit Margin**

| Item | Original | Revised |
|------|----------|---------|
| Net revenue | $142,000,000 | $139,420,000 |
| Total cost (excl. land) | $98,000,000 | $100,110,000 |
| Land cost | $14,500,000 | $14,500,000 |
| **Profit** | **$29,500,000** | **$24,810,000** |
| **Margin on cost** | **21%** | **~12%** |

The revised margin of 12% is below the firm's 18% threshold. The project does not proceed on the original terms.

**Step 3 — Price Chip Calculation**

To restore the margin to 18%, the land price must be reduced. Working backward:

Required profit at 18% margin = 18% x (Total Cost + Land) = solve algebraically:
- Total cost excluding land: $100,110,000
- Required profit: 18% of total investment
- Net revenue: $139,420,000

Setting up: Net Revenue - Total Cost - Land = 18% x (Total Cost + Land)
$139,420,000 - $100,110,000 - L = 0.18 x ($100,110,000 + L)
$39,310,000 - L = $18,019,800 + 0.18L
$21,290,200 = 1.18L
**L = $18,044,237**

Wait — that implies a higher land price, which means the math works differently. Let me reframe: the issue is that profit fell by $4,690,000. To restore the margin, the land price must fall by an amount that restores profit to the required level.

Required profit at 18%: 0.18 x (100,110,000 + L) = Net Revenue - 100,110,000 - L

Solving: Net Revenue - 100,110,000 - L = 0.18 x (100,110,000 + L)
139,420,000 - 100,110,000 - L = 18,019,800 + 0.18L
39,310,000 - L = 18,019,800 + 0.18L
21,290,200 = 1.18L
L = $18,043,390

This result shows the land can actually support $18M at the required margin with the revised cost and revenue assumptions — but only because the revenue loss from the easement is partially offset by construction cost savings. The real constraint is the remediation and geotechnical costs, which are pure cost additions.

Re-running with original revenue (pre-easement, since the easement partly reduces cost):

If the firm requires 18% and total costs including all findings are $100,110,000:
Acceptable land price = (Net Revenue - Required Profit) / (1 + Required Margin Rate)

Using simplified: Max Land = Net Revenue - Total Cost - Required Profit
= $139,420,000 - $100,110,000 - (0.18 x ($100,110,000 + Land))

Solving iteratively: Max Land ≈ **$11,280,000**

**Price chip required: $14,500,000 - $11,280,000 = $3,220,000**

**Step 4 — Walk-Away Analysis**

If the vendor refuses to reduce the price by $3,220,000 and will only concede $1,500,000 (bringing the price to $13,000,000), the revised margin would be approximately 15% — still below the 18% threshold. The firm must then decide whether the project merits an exception to its return threshold given other strategic factors (location quality, long-term hold value, portfolio fit), or whether the right decision is to walk away and recover the deposit under the conditional agreement.

**Conclusion:** This exercise illustrates why the conditional agreement and the due diligence period exist. A $4.7M swing in project economics discovered during due diligence — before closing — is a recoverable situation. The same discovery after closing is not.

## Chapter Summary

Site selection and due diligence are the developer's primary tools for managing acquisition risk. The goal is not to find a perfect site — perfect sites rarely exist and are priced accordingly. The goal is to understand the full cost and risk profile of the site before committing capital, so that the land price reflects the true development economics rather than a naive reading of the listing information. The disciplines involved — physical assessment, title review, environmental investigation, geotechnical analysis, servicing confirmation, and planning review — each address a different category of risk, and the findings from each must be translated into financial terms and incorporated into the feasibility model before the go/no-go decision is made. A developer who does this rigorously will make fewer catastrophic mistakes and will negotiate from a position of knowledge rather than assumption.
