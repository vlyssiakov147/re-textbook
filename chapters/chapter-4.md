# Chapter 4: Entitlements, Zoning & Permits

Before a developer can build anything, they must obtain permission from the government to build it. This permission — the legal right to develop land in a particular way — is called an entitlement. Obtaining entitlements is one of the most time-consuming, expensive, and uncertain phases of the entire development process. A project that takes 24 months to construct may have taken 36 months to entitle. A developer who masters construction but underestimates the entitlement process will consistently produce projects that are late, over budget on carrying costs, and financially underperforming relative to the original pro forma.

In Ontario, the entitlement process is governed by a layered planning framework that involves provincial policy, municipal official plans, zoning bylaws, and site-specific approvals. Understanding how these layers interact — and where the real risks and leverage points are — is what separates developers who navigate the system efficiently from those who fight it indefinitely.

---

## 4.1 What is the Entitlement Process?

The entitlement process is the sequence of approvals a developer must obtain from planning authorities before a building permit can be issued and construction can begin. The specific approvals required depend on what the developer wants to build relative to what the current land use permissions allow.

If the proposed development conforms exactly to current zoning — the right use, the right density, the right height, all within the permitted standards — the developer may be able to proceed directly to site plan approval and then building permit. This is called an as-of-right development and it is the fastest path to construction.

If the proposed development requires uses, density, or height beyond what current zoning permits, the developer must first apply to change the zoning — either through a Zoning Bylaw Amendment (ZBA), an Official Plan Amendment (OPA), or both. These are discretionary approvals, meaning the municipality has the power to approve, refuse, or impose conditions. There is no guarantee of success, and the timeline is largely outside the developer's control.

The entitlement process matters financially because it is a period of pure cost accumulation with no offsetting revenue. During entitlements the developer is paying carrying costs on the land, consultant fees for the planning application, development management overhead, and financing costs — all without generating any income. Every month of entitlement timeline is a month of cost that must be recovered from the eventual project revenue. This is why entitlement timeline assumptions are one of the most consequential inputs in a development feasibility model.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Entitlements sit outside your current responsibilities. The planning process is managed by senior development staff — typically a VP or Director of Development — working closely with the planning consultant, architect, and legal counsel. Your exposure to this phase is indirect: you may be asked to update the project pro forma when an entitlement milestone is hit or delayed, to track planning application status in the project schedule, or to incorporate a timeline change into the carrying cost model. Understanding the full process well enough to ask intelligent questions and follow team discussions is the right goal at this stage.

**The Three Phases of Entitlement**

Most Ontario development projects move through three sequential approval phases before a building permit can be issued:

1. **OPA and/or ZBA** — Establishing what can be built on the site in terms of use, density, and height. This is the most uncertain and time-consuming phase. Required when the proposed development exceeds current zoning permissions.
2. **Site Plan Approval** — Confirming how it will be built in terms of design, materials, landscaping, servicing, and interface with the public realm. Required for almost all significant development even on as-of-right projects.
3. **Building Permit** — Authorization to begin construction. Issued by the municipal building department once all planning approvals are in place and the drawings comply with the Ontario Building Code.

## 4.2 The Ontario Planning Framework

Ontario's land use planning system is hierarchical. Each level sets parameters within which the level below must operate. Understanding this hierarchy explains why certain approvals take longer than others and why some applications are more defensible than others.

**Provincial Policy Statement (PPS)**

The Provincial Policy Statement is issued by the Province of Ontario under the Planning Act and sets out province-wide land use planning policies. All planning decisions in Ontario must be consistent with the PPS. It directs growth to existing settlement areas, promotes intensification and efficient use of land and infrastructure, and establishes policies on housing supply, environmental protection, and infrastructure provision.

The PPS was significantly updated in 2023 with a strong emphasis on increasing housing supply. Municipalities are required to support residential intensification and are restricted in their ability to refuse density that is consistent with the PPS housing supply objectives.

**Provincial Plans**

In the Greater Golden Horseshoe — which includes Toronto and all 905 municipalities — provincial plans layer additional policies on top of the PPS. The most relevant are the Growth Plan for the Greater Golden Horseshoe (now consolidated into A Place to Grow), which directs specific intensification targets and defines where and how growth should occur, and the Greenbelt Plan, which restricts development on designated lands.

**Municipal Official Plans**

Each municipality prepares its own Official Plan — a long-term strategic document that translates provincial policy into local land use designations, policies, and urban structure decisions. The Official Plan divides the municipality into land use designations such as residential, employment, mixed-use, and open space, and establishes policies for each designation including permitted uses, density ranges, and development criteria.

An Official Plan Amendment (OPA) is required when a proposed development is not consistent with the current Official Plan designation for a site — either because the proposed use is not permitted in the designation, or because the proposed density exceeds the range the designation permits.

**Zoning Bylaws**

The zoning bylaw is the municipal instrument that implements the Official Plan at the parcel level. It assigns every property in the municipality a specific zoning designation that sets out permitted uses, maximum density (FSR/FAR), maximum height, required setbacks, minimum parking, and other development standards. Unlike the Official Plan — which is a policy document — the zoning bylaw is a legal instrument. Either a proposal conforms to it or it does not.

> **Key Concept — The Hierarchy Matters for Application Strategy**
> A ZBA application that is consistent with the Official Plan is significantly more defensible than one that also requires an OPA. If the Official Plan already designates the site for mixed-use intensification at the density proposed, the developer is essentially asking the municipality to update the zoning to reflect what the Official Plan already contemplates. If the application also requires an OPA, the developer is asking the municipality to change its long-term land use vision for the site — a higher bar, a longer process, and a more uncertain outcome. Developers who buy sites already designated for their intended use in the Official Plan have a materially lower entitlement risk profile.

**The Planning Act**

The Planning Act is the Ontario statute that governs the entire municipal planning process. It sets out the rules for how Official Plans and zoning bylaws are created and amended, what rights applicants have in the process, what timelines municipalities must follow, and what recourse is available when municipalities fail to act or refuse applications.

## 4.3 Official Plan Amendments (OPA)

An Official Plan Amendment is required when the proposed development is inconsistent with the land use designation or policies in the municipal Official Plan. It is the highest-order site-specific approval in the municipal planning system and the most difficult to obtain.

**When an OPA is Required**

Common situations requiring an OPA include: proposing residential development on land designated Employment Area (a politically contentious issue in Toronto); proposing density significantly above the range contemplated in the current designation; proposing a building height that conflicts with Official Plan policies (such as view corridor protections or midrise policies on certain streets); or proposing a use that the current designation simply does not contemplate.

Not all projects require an OPA. Many intensification projects in transit-served areas fall within the existing Official Plan designation and require only a ZBA to update the specific zoning standards.

**The OPA Process**

An OPA application is submitted to the municipal planning department along with the full application package — planning justification report, urban design guidelines, technical studies, and supporting drawings. The municipality reviews the application for completeness, circulates it to internal departments and external agencies for comment, holds statutory public meetings, and ultimately brings a recommendation to planning committee and then city council for a decision.

In Toronto, OPA decisions at council require a majority vote. In 905 municipalities, the process is similar but the political dynamics and timelines differ by municipality.

> **Common Problem — Misjudging Official Plan Conformity**
> Developers sometimes assume their application is OPA-compliant based on a superficial reading of the Official Plan designations, only to discover partway through the process that a specific policy — a height limit tied to a streetscape study, a shadow policy near a park, a view corridor protection — conflicts with the proposal. A thorough Official Plan review by an experienced planning consultant before the application is submitted — not after — is the only reliable way to identify these conflicts in time to adjust the development program or price them into the risk model.

**OPA Timeline**

In Toronto, OPA processes have historically taken 3-5 years from application to final approval, though recent provincial interventions have accelerated timelines on some applications. In 905 municipalities, processes are generally faster — 12-24 months is more typical for straightforward applications, though complex or controversial ones can exceed this significantly.

## 4.4 Zoning Bylaw Amendments (ZBA)

A Zoning Bylaw Amendment is required when the proposed development exceeds the standards in the current zoning bylaw — most commonly because the proposed density (FSR) or height exceeds the bylaw maximum, or because the proposed use is not permitted in the current zone. A ZBA is a site-specific change to the zoning bylaw for the subject property.

**What a ZBA Changes**

A ZBA typically seeks to increase one or more of: the maximum floor space ratio, the maximum building height, the permitted uses (e.g., adding residential to a commercially-zoned site), and to reduce or modify standards such as setbacks, parking minimums, or angular plane requirements. The resulting bylaw is site-specific — it creates a new set of permissions that apply only to the subject property.

**The ZBA Process**

The ZBA process in Ontario is initiated by submitting a complete planning application to the municipal planning department. The application is reviewed for completeness — a formal completeness determination under the Planning Act triggers the statutory clock for the municipality's decision timeline. The municipality has a defined period (see Section 4.7) to make a decision after a complete application is received before the applicant can appeal to the Ontario Land Tribunal.

Following acceptance, the application is circulated to internal departments (transportation, engineering, parks, heritage, urban design) and external agencies (conservation authorities, school boards, utilities) for technical review. A planning report is prepared by municipal staff summarizing the application, analyzing it against applicable policy, and making a recommendation. Statutory public meetings are held, typically a Community Council or Planning Committee meeting followed by a full Council vote.

> **Key Concept — The Difference Between Approval and Passing**
> A ZBA that receives a positive staff recommendation is more likely to succeed at committee and council — but is not guaranteed. Councillors can and do override staff recommendations, particularly on applications that generate significant community opposition. Conversely, a negative staff recommendation can sometimes be overridden if the developer has made a compelling case to councillors. Understanding the political dimension of the planning process — who the ward councillor is, what their development philosophy is, what the community organization landscape looks like — is part of experienced entitlement management that experienced planning consultants provide.

**Minor Variances**

Not all deviations from the zoning bylaw require a full ZBA. Minor variances — small deviations from specific zoning standards that do not change the fundamental character of the permitted use — can be approved by the Committee of Adjustment, a quasi-judicial body that meets more frequently and operates faster than a full council process. Minor variances are typically used for things like a small setback reduction or a modest height exceedance on an otherwise compliant project, not for the fundamental density and use changes that are the subject of major development applications.

**Worked Example — Identifying ZBA Requirements:** Your firm is evaluating a site at 9700 Yonge Street in Richmond Hill currently zoned for mixed commercial use with a maximum FSR of 2.0 and a maximum height of 6 storeys. The proposed development is two residential rental towers of 19 and 28 storeys over a shared below-grade parking structure, with a total density of approximately 4.5 FSR. The proposal also includes ground-floor retail. The required approvals are: (1) a ZBA to increase the permitted FSR from 2.0 to approximately 4.5 and to increase the maximum height from 6 storeys to 28 storeys; and (2) an OPA if the current Official Plan designation does not contemplate residential use or the proposed density range at this location. The project statistics confirm this is in SPA Resubmission stage as of November 2025 — meaning the OPA and ZBA have already been obtained and the project is now in the detailed design review phase.

## 4.5 Site Plan Approval (SPA)

Site Plan Approval is the approval stage that bridges the gap between the planning permissions established through OPA and ZBA, and the construction permit. Where OPA and ZBA establish what can be built, Site Plan Approval determines how it will be built — the detailed design, materials, landscaping, grading, drainage, loading, access, and interface with adjacent properties and the public realm.

**What SPA Reviews**

The Site Plan process reviews and approves:

- **Building massing and design** — Exterior materials, cladding systems, fenestration, architectural expression, and how the building meets the street at grade.
- **Site layout** — Location of the building on the lot, setbacks, pedestrian access routes, vehicle access and loading, surface parking if any, and landscaping.
- **Grading and drainage** — How stormwater is managed on the site, grades around the building perimeter, and connections to municipal storm infrastructure.
- **Servicing** — Water, sanitary, and storm connections, transformer vault locations, utility access, and garbage collection infrastructure.
- **Landscape plan** — Species selection, hardscaping, tree retention and replacement, amenity areas, and CPTED (Crime Prevention Through Environmental Design) considerations.
- **Sustainability measures** — Energy performance compliance with the applicable Toronto Green Standard tier or equivalent municipal requirement.

**SPA in Toronto vs. 905 Municipalities**

In Toronto, Site Plan Approval is processed through the City's Development Review division. Complex applications involve multiple rounds of comment from urban design, transportation, engineering, parks, and heritage staff, as well as external agencies. The process typically takes 12-24 months for a major residential project, with multiple resubmissions of revised drawings in response to comments.

In 905 municipalities, the SPA process is generally faster — 6-18 months for comparable projects — though requirements vary and municipalities like Vaughan, Richmond Hill, and Markham each have their own design guidelines and review processes.

The 9700 Yonge Street project in Richmond Hill provides a real example: the project statistics reference SPA Resubmission drawings dated November 13, 2025. This means the initial SPA submission received comments from the City of Richmond Hill, the design team revised the drawings in response, and the resubmission represents the revised package seeking approval. This back-and-forth is normal and expected — most significant projects go through at least two rounds of SPA submission before approval.

> **Common Problem — Underestimating SPA Complexity**
> Developers who have successfully obtained ZBA approval sometimes underestimate the SPA process, treating it as a formality rather than a substantive review. This is a mistake. SPA comments can require meaningful changes to the building design — reconfiguration of the lobby, relocation of the loading area, changes to exterior cladding specifications, additional landscape requirements — that have real cost implications. If the SPA process forces significant redesign, it can add months to the timeline and hundreds of thousands of dollars in redesign and consultant costs. Engaging an experienced architect who understands the municipality's design priorities from the outset — rather than designing for SPA as an afterthought — is the right approach.

**Site Plan Agreement**

Once the SPA drawings are approved, the developer and municipality enter into a Site Plan Agreement — a legal document that binds the developer to construct the project in accordance with the approved plans, to complete the public realm improvements required as a condition of approval, to provide securities (typically letters of credit) to guarantee those obligations, and to maintain the site in accordance with the approved landscape plan. The Site Plan Agreement is a registered instrument on title and must be executed before the building permit is issued.

## 4.6 The Application Package — What Gets Submitted

A major development application is not simply a set of drawings. It is a comprehensive package of planning, design, and technical documents assembled by a team of consultants under the direction of the lead planning consultant and architect. Understanding what goes into the package — and why each component exists — helps explain both the time and cost of the entitlement process.

**Core Planning Documents**

**Planning Justification Report (PJR)**
The PJR is the central planning document in any OPA or ZBA application. It describes the proposed development, analyzes it against applicable provincial, regional, and municipal policy, and makes the case for why the application should be approved. A well-prepared PJR does not simply assert that the project is good — it works through the applicable policy framework systematically, addresses potential objections, and demonstrates policy support for the proposal. It is prepared by the planning consultant and is typically 40-80 pages for a major application.

**Urban Design Brief**
Prepared by the architect, the Urban Design Brief describes the design vision for the project — massing, character, relationship to context, public realm interface, and architectural expression. It typically includes renderings, section drawings showing relationships to adjacent properties, and a sun/shadow analysis.

**Architectural Drawings**
Conceptual site plan, floor plans, elevations, and sections showing the proposed building design at a level of detail appropriate for the planning stage. These are not construction drawings — they establish the massing, program, and design intent but will be further developed through the SPA process.

**Technical Studies**

The following studies are typically required for a major OPA/ZBA application in Toronto and most 905 municipalities:

| Study | Purpose |
|-------|---------|
| Traffic Impact Study | Assesses the transportation impacts of the development on the surrounding road network and identifies required mitigation measures. Prepared by a transportation engineer. |
| Pedestrian Level Wind Study | Analyzes wind conditions at pedestrian level around the proposed building, particularly relevant for tall buildings that can create significant wind acceleration at street level. |
| Sun/Shadow Study | Documents the shadow impact of the proposed building on adjacent properties, streets, and parks at specified times and dates. A key planning issue in dense urban areas. |
| Noise and Vibration Study | Assesses noise impacts on and from the development, typically required near major roads, rail lines, or industrial uses. |
| Functional Servicing Report | Prepared by a civil engineer, confirms that municipal water, sanitary, and storm infrastructure can support the proposed development. |
| Stormwater Management Report | Documents how stormwater will be managed on-site to comply with municipal and conservation authority requirements. |
| Landscape Plan | Conceptual landscape design showing tree retention, new plantings, hardscaping, and amenity areas. |
| Tree Inventory and Preservation Plan | Identifies trees on and adjacent to the site, their condition and species, and what can be retained vs. what must be removed and replaced. |
| Heritage Impact Assessment | Required when the site or adjacent properties have heritage designations or are within a heritage conservation district. |
| Phase 1 ESA | As discussed in Chapter 2 — required as part of the planning application package. |
| Geotechnical Investigation | Preliminary geotechnical findings are often included in the planning package, with a full investigation completed at SPA stage. |
| Energy Strategy / Green Standard Report | Documents how the project will achieve the applicable sustainable design standard — Toronto Green Standard Tier 2 or 3, or equivalent. |

**Community Consultation**

In many municipalities, particularly Toronto, community consultation is a required component of the planning process. This typically includes a formal community consultation meeting where the applicant presents the proposal to area residents and takes questions and comments. The feedback from community consultation is not binding — the planning committee and council make the final decision — but it shapes the political context and can influence both the staff recommendation and the council vote.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> The application package is assembled by a team of external consultants — planning consultant, architect, transportation engineer, civil engineer, heritage consultant, and others — coordinated by the development team. At your level, you are unlikely to be directly involved in preparing any of these documents. However, you may be asked to track their status as part of a project schedule, to organize invoices and payments to the consulting team, or to compile documents for submission. Understanding what each document is and why it is required helps you do that coordination work intelligently.

## 4.7 The Approval Timeline — What Actually Takes So Long

One of the most consistent frustrations in Ontario development is how long the planning approval process takes. Understanding the sources of delay — both systemic and project-specific — is essential for building realistic feasibility models and managing investor expectations.

**Statutory Timelines Under the Planning Act**

The Planning Act establishes maximum timelines within which municipalities must make decisions on complete planning applications. For OPA and ZBA applications, the current statutory timeline is 120 days from receipt of a complete application. If the municipality fails to make a decision within this period, the applicant can appeal to the Ontario Land Tribunal as if the application had been refused.

In practice, 120 days is rarely sufficient to process a major application. Applications are routinely appealed to the OLT on the basis of non-decision — not because the applicant is unhappy with the outcome, but as a tactical move to accelerate the process by creating a parallel OLT process that motivates the municipality to negotiate a resolution.

**Sources of Delay**

**Application completeness**
The statutory clock does not start until the municipality formally determines the application is complete — meaning all required materials have been submitted in accordance with the municipality's pre-consultation requirements. Municipalities can issue incompleteness notices that pause the clock, requiring the applicant to provide additional materials before the timeline restarts. A poorly prepared initial submission can result in multiple incompleteness notices, adding months before the clock even starts properly.

**Technical circulation**
Once accepted as complete, the application is circulated to internal departments and external agencies for comment. Each agency has its own review timelines and priorities. A complex application touching transportation, heritage, engineering, parks, and multiple external agencies will generate comments over a period of weeks to months, often in multiple rounds as questions are asked and responses are provided.

**Resubmissions**
Very few applications are approved on the basis of the initial submission. The planning process typically involves at least one — and often two or three — rounds of resubmission where the applicant revises the application in response to comments from the municipality and agencies. Each resubmission can take weeks to prepare and triggers a new round of review.

**Community and political dynamics**
Politically controversial applications — those opposed by organized community groups, those in wards with councillors skeptical of intensification, or those that touch heritage or park issues — take longer than straightforward ones. Community opposition can delay statutory public meetings, generate additional technical requirements, and influence the pace at which municipal staff prioritize the application.

**Staff capacity**
Municipal planning departments are chronically under-resourced relative to the volume of applications received, particularly in fast-growing municipalities like Vaughan and Markham. Applications are processed in the order received, subject to internal priority-setting. Being in a queue behind other applications is simply a function of timing and cannot be controlled.

> **Key Concept — Realistic Timeline Assumptions by Application Type**

| Application Type | Typical Timeline (GTA) | Notes |
|-----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Minor Variance (Committee of Adjustment) | 2-4 months | Fastest path for small deviations |
| ZBA only (OP-conforming) | 18-36 months | Faster in 905 than Toronto |
| OPA + ZBA (combined) | 30-60 months | Most complex scenario |
| Site Plan Approval | 12-24 months (Toronto); 6-18 months (905) | Follows OPA/ZBA |
| Building Permit | 4-12 months | Depends on project complexity |
| **Total: OPA + ZBA + SPA + BP** | **4-8+ years** | From application to shovel in ground |

**How Developers Manage Timeline Risk**

Experienced developers use several strategies to manage entitlement timeline risk:

1. **Buy sites already designated** — Purchasing sites where the Official Plan already supports the intended development eliminates the OPA risk entirely and reduces the ZBA risk.
2. **Pre-consultation** — Meeting with municipal planning staff before submitting the application to understand their requirements, address potential concerns proactively, and ensure the submission will be accepted as complete. Pre-consultation is a mandatory step in most Ontario municipalities.
3. **File early, iterate** — Submitting the application as early as possible, even before all design elements are finalized, to start the clock and begin receiving comments. The design can evolve through the process.
4. **Use the OLT strategically** — Filing an OLT appeal on non-decision grounds motivates municipalities to resolve the application rather than allow it to proceed to a full hearing.
5. **Build government and community relationships** — Engaging the ward councillor and community stakeholders early, before the application is filed, to understand concerns and address them in the design rather than fighting them at a public meeting.

## 4.8 The Ontario Land Tribunal (OLT)

The Ontario Land Tribunal is an independent adjudicative tribunal that hears appeals of planning decisions in Ontario. It was created in 2021 by merging the Local Planning Appeal Tribunal (LPAT) and several other tribunals. Understanding the OLT is important because it is both a risk to developers (community groups can appeal approvals) and a tool for developers (they can appeal refusals or non-decisions).

**Who Can Appeal to the OLT**

Under the Planning Act, the following parties can appeal planning decisions to the OLT:

- The applicant (developer), if the municipality refuses the application or fails to make a decision within the statutory timeline
- The municipality, in limited circumstances
- Persons or public bodies who made oral or written submissions at the statutory public meeting

Note that the 2023 Planning Act amendments significantly restricted third-party appeal rights in certain circumstances — particularly for applications that meet minimum density and housing supply policies. This was a deliberate provincial intervention to reduce the ability of community groups to obstruct housing supply through the OLT appeal process.

**The OLT Hearing Process**

An OLT hearing is a formal quasi-judicial proceeding. Parties are represented by planning consultants and lawyers. Evidence is presented through expert witness reports — planning justification, traffic, heritage, urban design, shadow — and witnesses are subject to cross-examination. Hearings can last days or weeks on complex applications.

The OLT can allow the appeal (approving the development), dismiss the appeal (upholding the refusal), or approve a modified version. Its decisions are binding on the municipality, though they can be appealed to the Divisional Court on questions of law.

**OLT as a Development Tool**

For developers, the most common use of the OLT is not a full contested hearing — it is a negotiated settlement. A developer who files an OLT appeal on non-decision grounds creates a parallel process that motivates the municipality to negotiate rather than allow the matter to proceed to a costly and time-consuming hearing. Most OLT appeals in this context are resolved through Minutes of Settlement — a negotiated agreement between the applicant and the municipality on the specific permissions to be granted — that is then brought back to council for approval.

> **Common Problem — Underestimating OLT Cost and Timeline**
> A full contested OLT hearing is expensive and slow. Developer legal and expert witness costs for a complex hearing can reach $500,000-$1,500,000. Hearing dates are often booked 12-24 months out. A developer who assumes an OLT appeal will resolve quickly and cheaply is taking a significant risk. The OLT is best used as a negotiating tool — the threat of a full hearing motivates settlement — not as a routine path to approval.

## 4.9 Conditions of Approval & Securing Entitlements

Obtaining OPA and ZBA approval does not mean the entitlement is unconditional. Most planning approvals come with conditions that must be satisfied before the approval becomes final and enforceable, and before the subsequent SPA and building permit process can be completed.

**Typical Conditions of Approval**

Conditions attached to OPA and ZBA approvals commonly include:

- **Holding (H) symbol** — A provision in the ZBA that prevents development until specific conditions are met, such as completion of a technical study, execution of an agreement, or provision of financial securities. Once conditions are satisfied, the holding symbol is removed by bylaw amendment.
- **Section 37 / Community Benefits Charge agreement** — An agreement between the developer and the municipality defining the community benefits to be provided in exchange for the additional density permitted by the ZBA. In Toronto, this has transitioned from Section 37 agreements to the Community Benefits Charge framework, though legacy Section 37 obligations remain on existing approvals.
- **Servicing agreements** — Agreements with the municipality or utility providers for the design and installation of required infrastructure upgrades.
- **Heritage conservation agreements** — For sites with heritage resources, conditions requiring specific conservation treatments or heritage easements.
- **Affordable housing commitments** — Conditions requiring a defined number of units to be provided at below-market rents or prices, either on-site or through a cash contribution to a municipal affordable housing fund.

**What Securing an Entitlement Means Financially**

A secured entitlement — one where the OPA and ZBA have been passed by council, the appeal period has expired without a challenge (or any appeals have been resolved), and all conditions have been satisfied — is a fundamentally different asset than a site that is still in the planning process. A secured entitlement eliminates the rezoning risk entirely and dramatically reduces the uncertainty in the project timeline and cost model.

This is why land values increase as entitlements are obtained. A site purchased as raw land with rezoning potential might trade at one value. The same site after a ZBA has been passed — with the entitlement risk removed — trades at a materially higher value, reflecting the probability-weighted present value of the development potential now confirmed rather than merely possible.

> **Key Concept — The Entitlement Premium**
> The increase in land value from raw land to secured entitlement represents the "entitlement premium" — the value created by the developer through the planning process. For a developer who purchases a site speculatively, navigates a successful rezoning, and then sells the entitled land to a builder, this premium is the entire business model. For a developer who holds and builds, the entitlement premium is captured in the overall project return but never crystallized as a discrete transaction.

## 4.10 Key Metrics & Terms Reference

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Entitlement | The legal right to develop land in a specific way, obtained through the planning approval process. |
| As-of-Right Development | A project that conforms to existing zoning without requiring any planning amendments. |
| Official Plan (OP) | The municipality's long-term strategic land use document. Sets designations and policies for all land in the municipality. |
| Official Plan Amendment (OPA) | Application to change a site's Official Plan designation or policies. Required when the proposal conflicts with the current OP. |
| Zoning Bylaw | Legal instrument that implements the Official Plan at the parcel level. Specifies permitted uses, density, height, and development standards for each property. |
| Zoning Bylaw Amendment (ZBA) | Application to change the zoning standards for a specific property to permit greater density, height, or different uses. |
| Floor Space Ratio (FSR) | Total gross floor area divided by site area. The primary density measure in Ontario zoning bylaws. |
| Site Plan Approval (SPA) | Municipal approval of the detailed building and site design, required before building permit issuance. |
| Site Plan Agreement | Legal agreement between developer and municipality binding the developer to construct in accordance with approved plans and fulfill public realm obligations. |
| Minor Variance | A small deviation from zoning standards approved by the Committee of Adjustment without requiring a full ZBA. |
| Planning Justification Report (PJR) | The core planning document in an OPA/ZBA application analyzing the proposal against applicable policy. |
| Pre-Consultation | Mandatory meeting with municipal planning staff before submitting an application, to establish requirements and identify potential issues. |
| Holding (H) Symbol | A provision in a ZBA that prevents development until specified conditions are satisfied. |
| Section 37 Agreement | Legacy mechanism under the Planning Act allowing municipalities to secure community benefits in exchange for additional density. Now largely replaced by the Community Benefits Charge. |
| Community Benefits Charge (CBC) | A levy (up to 4% of land value) charged by municipalities on development applications that require a ZBA, replacing Section 37 for new applications. |
| Ontario Land Tribunal (OLT) | Independent adjudicative tribunal that hears appeals of municipal planning decisions. |
| Minutes of Settlement | Negotiated agreement between applicant and municipality resolving an OLT appeal, typically defining the specific permissions to be granted. |
| Statutory Public Meeting | Formal public meeting required under the Planning Act before a planning decision is made, giving the public an opportunity to comment. |
| Completeness Determination | Formal municipal assessment of whether a planning application includes all required materials. The statutory timeline begins only after a complete application is accepted. |
| Provincial Policy Statement (PPS) | Province-wide land use policies under the Planning Act. All planning decisions in Ontario must be consistent with the PPS. |

## Applied Exercises

## AE-4.4 — Mapping the Approval Pathway for a Mixed-Use Intensification Project

This exercise maps the full approval pathway for a mixed-use intensification project similar to 9700 Yonge Street, working from current zoning through to building permit.

**Project Description**

A 1.5-acre site in Richmond Hill currently zoned for commercial use with a maximum FSR of 2.0 and maximum height of 6 storeys. The Official Plan designates the site as "Major Retail Commercial" but is located within a Primary Centre node where the OP contemplates higher-density mixed-use intensification. The proposed development is two residential rental towers of 19 and 28 storeys with ground-floor retail, total FSR of approximately 4.5.

**Step 1 — Assess Official Plan Conformity**

The site is in a Primary Centre node. The OP policies for Primary Centre nodes contemplate mixed-use intensification including residential, at densities up to 4.0-5.0 FSR in appropriate locations with transit access. The proposed FSR of 4.5 is within the contemplated range. The proposed uses (residential, retail) are consistent with the mixed-use policies.

**Conclusion:** An OPA is likely not required — the existing Official Plan designation and policies for this Primary Centre node can support the application through a ZBA alone. This significantly reduces entitlement risk.

## AE-4.6 — Pre-Consultation Preparation: What to Expect from the Municipality

This exercise simulates the pre-consultation process for a new application, showing how information gathered at pre-consultation shapes the submission strategy and budget.

**Project Description**

Your firm is preparing to submit a ZBA application for a 22-storey purpose-built rental tower on a 0.6-acre site in Vaughan. The site is designated "High Rise Mixed Use" in the Vaughan Official Plan — OPA is not anticipated to be required. The proposed density is 4.2 FSR. The current zoning permits 8 storeys and 2.5 FSR.

**Pre-Consultation Meeting — Key Items Discussed**

The development team meets with Vaughan planning staff and the following requirements are confirmed:

| Requirement | Detail | Cost Implication |
|------------|--------|-----------------|
| Mandatory Studies | Traffic, wind, shadow, FSR, servicing, stormwater, noise, Phase 1 ESA, energy | $250,000-$380,000 in consulting fees |
| Community Meeting | One pre-application community meeting required before submission | $15,000-$25,000 coordination cost |
| Heritage Review | Adjacent property has heritage designation — Heritage Impact Assessment required | $20,000-$40,000 additional |
| Holding Symbol | H symbol will be placed on the ZBA — triggered by requirement for a Transportation Demand Management plan | Additional study and agreement required |
| Community Benefits | CBC at 4% of land value expected — estimated $520,000 based on a $13M land value | Hard cost to budget |
| Section 37 (Legacy) | Not applicable — new application falls under CBC regime | N/A |
| Affordable Housing | City policy requires 5% of units at 80% of market rent | 11 units at reduced rent — revenue impact to model |

**Updated Feasibility Impact**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Mandatory technical studies | $315,000 (midpoint) |
| Community meeting | $20,000 |
| Heritage Impact Assessment | $30,000 |
| TDM plan and H symbol removal process | $25,000 |
| Community Benefits Charge | $520,000 |
| Affordable unit revenue reduction (11 units x $400/month discount x 12 months) | ($52,800/year) |
| **Total one-time entitlement cost additions** | **$910,000** |
| **Annual revenue reduction (affordable units)** | **($52,800)** |

**Conclusion:** Pre-consultation surfaced $910,000 in entitlement costs plus an ongoing annual revenue reduction that were not captured in the initial feasibility estimate. The Heritage Impact Assessment and the TDM plan were not anticipated at the desktop screening stage — they were only identified through direct engagement with the municipality. This is exactly why pre-consultation is mandatory in most Ontario municipalities, and why developers who skip or defer it are operating with an incomplete cost picture.

## AE-4.7 — Building a Realistic Entitlement Timeline with Contingency

This exercise builds a realistic project schedule from ZBA submission to building permit, with base case, optimistic, and conservative scenarios, and translates the timeline difference into carrying cost.

**Project Assumptions**

- ZBA application submitted (Month 0)
- Land acquisition cost: $18,000,000
- Land financing rate: 7.5% per annum
- Monthly carrying cost on land: $112,500
- Soft costs accruing during entitlement: $85,000/month (planning consultants, design team, overhead)
- Total monthly carrying cost during entitlement: $197,500

**Timeline Scenarios**

| Milestone | Optimistic | Base Case | Conservative |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| Completeness determination | Month 1 | Month 2 | Month 4 |
| First round agency comments | Month 4 | Month 6 | Month 9 |
| Resubmission | Month 7 | Month 10 | Month 15 |
| Second round comments | Month 9 | Month 14 | Month 21 |
| Staff report issued | Month 11 | Month 17 | Month 25 |
| Community Council / Planning Committee | Month 13 | Month 20 | Month 29 |
| Council approval | Month 14 | Month 22 | Month 32 |
| Appeal period expires (no appeal) | Month 16 | Month 24 | Month 34 |
| SPA submission | Month 18 | Month 26 | Month 37 |
| SPA approval | Month 26 | Month 38 | Month 52 |
| Building permit issued | Month 30 | Month 44 | Month 60 |

**Carrying Cost by Scenario**

| Scenario | Months to Permit | Total Carrying Cost |
|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| Optimistic | 30 months | $5,925,000 |
| Base Case | 44 months | $8,690,000 |
| Conservative | 60 months | $11,850,000 |
| **Difference: Base vs. Conservative** | **16 months** | **$3,160,000** |

**Conclusion:** The difference between a base case and a conservative entitlement scenario is $3,160,000 in additional carrying cost on this project — a meaningful impact on project returns. A feasibility model that assumes only the optimistic timeline is not a credible underwriting document. The base case should be used for primary analysis, the conservative scenario for stress-testing, and the optimistic scenario should be treated as an upside case only. Any project that only works under the optimistic entitlement timeline should be viewed with significant skepticism.

## Chapter Summary

Entitlements are the gating function of development — nothing else happens until the planning approvals are in place. The Ontario planning framework is complex, multi-layered, and highly variable in its outcomes, which is why experienced developers treat entitlement risk as one of the top three project risks alongside construction cost and market risk. The key disciplines are: understanding exactly what approvals are required before acquiring a site, building realistic timeline assumptions into the feasibility model with meaningful contingency, engaging experienced planning consultants early, pre-consulting with the municipality before submission, and managing the political dimension of the process alongside the technical dimension. A developer who masters the entitlement process — who consistently obtains approvals faster and with better outcomes than their peers — has a genuine competitive advantage that translates directly into superior project economics.
