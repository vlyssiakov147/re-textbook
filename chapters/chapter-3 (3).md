# Chapter 3: Financing & the Capital Stack

No development project is built entirely with the developer's own money. Real estate development is a capital-intensive business, and the way that capital is sourced, structured, and deployed is one of the most consequential decisions a developer makes on any project. Get the financing structure right and the project has the runway to succeed even when things go wrong. Get it wrong — too little equity, too expensive debt, the wrong lender for the project type — and even a well-located, well-designed project can fail at the financing stage before a shovel hits the ground.

This chapter covers the full financing lifecycle of a development project: how the capital stack is assembled before construction, how debt and equity interact during the construction period, how funds flow through the monthly draw process, and how developers report back to their capital partners throughout. Sections 3.7 and 3.8 are directly relevant to your day-to-day responsibilities and are written accordingly.

---

## 3.1 Overview of Development Financing

Development financing differs fundamentally from permanent mortgage financing in two ways: it is short-term, and it disburses incrementally rather than as a lump sum.

A permanent mortgage is borrowed once against a stabilized, income-producing property and repaid over an amortization period of 20-30 years. A construction loan is borrowed progressively over the construction period — typically 18-36 months for a mid-to-high-rise project — as costs are incurred and verified. Once the project is complete and stabilized, the construction loan is repaid and replaced by permanent financing or proceeds from a sale.

This distinction matters because it shapes everything about how development financing is structured, priced, and managed. A construction lender is not making a loan against a finished asset — they are making a loan against a project in progress, with all the uncertainty that entails. Their security is land, work in progress, and the developer's ability to complete what they started. This risk profile commands a higher interest rate than permanent financing and requires more intensive ongoing monitoring — which is why the monthly draw process, the project monitor, and the detailed reporting requirements all exist.

**The Development Financing Timeline**

A typical development financing timeline has three distinct phases:

1. **Land financing** — Bridge or land loan to fund the acquisition. Often shorter-term and more expensive than construction financing. Sometimes the same lender provides both, sometimes not.
2. **Construction financing** — The primary debt instrument funding the build. Drawn progressively as costs are incurred. Typically 18-36 months.
3. **Permanent financing or sale** — At stabilization, the construction loan is repaid either through a conventional mortgage (for a rental asset held long-term), a CMHC-insured mortgage (for purpose-built rental), or proceeds from a condominium sale program.

> **Note — Land Control: Outright Purchase vs. Option**
> How a developer controls land before construction financing is in place affects the equity position the project starts from. An outright acquisition with a bridge loan requires capital immediately and starts the interest clock. An option-to-purchase allows the developer to control the site for a fraction of the cost — paying a negotiated option fee to take the land off the market — while completing entitlements, design, and financing arrangements before committing to a full purchase. Options are a lower-risk method of controlling land in early-stage development because the developer's downside is limited to the option payment rather than the full land cost. Most large Canadian development projects involve some form of option or conditional purchase period before the land closes and construction financing is engaged.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Your direct involvement in financing begins at the construction phase — specifically the draw process and investor reporting covered in Sections 3.7 and 3.8. Understanding how the loan was structured, what the conditions precedent are, what the lender's reporting requirements look like, and how the capital stack is organized is essential context for doing that work well. You are the person preparing the documents that the lender and investors rely on to make monthly funding decisions — which means understanding the full financing picture is not academic for you, it is directly operational.

## 3.2 The Capital Stack — How Deals Are Structured

The capital stack is the layered structure of debt and equity that finances a development project. Each layer has a different risk profile, a different return expectation, and a different priority of repayment if things go wrong. Understanding the capital stack is fundamental to understanding development finance.

A typical development capital stack from bottom to top looks like this:

**Senior Debt (Construction Loan)**
The largest single component — typically 60-75% of total project cost on a conventional deal. First in line to be repaid, first security over the asset, lowest risk, lowest cost. If the project fails, the senior lender has the first right to the proceeds from any sale or enforcement action. Interest rates are typically at a spread over Bank Prime or CORRA — on mid-2024 to early 2025 deals, all-in rates were running approximately 7.0-8.5% depending on lender, project type, and borrower strength.

**Mezzanine Debt / Preferred Equity**
An intermediate layer that sits between senior debt and common equity. Higher risk than senior debt (repaid after the senior lender), higher return than common equity in normal scenarios (fixed rate or preferred return), lower risk than common equity (preferred return and often a priority on capital return). Not present on every deal — used when the senior loan does not provide enough proceeds to close the equity gap, or when the developer wants to minimize the amount of expensive common equity required.

**Common Equity**
The developer's own capital and that of their equity partners. Last to be repaid, highest risk, highest potential return. Common equity absorbs the first losses if costs overrun or revenues disappoint. On a typical LP/GP structure, the equity layer is split between the LP (limited partners, who provide most of the capital) and the GP (general partner, the developer, who manages the project and typically contributes a smaller share of capital but retains a meaningful share of profits through the waterfall).

> **Key Concept — Loan-to-Cost (LTC) Ratio**
> The LTC ratio is the senior loan amount expressed as a percentage of total project cost. A $75M construction loan on a $110M project represents an LTC of 68.2%. Lenders typically cap LTC at 65-75% for conventional construction loans, meaning the developer must fund the remaining 25-35% through equity (and mezzanine if applicable). The LTC ratio is one of the primary underwriting metrics a lender uses to assess how much skin the developer has in the game.

> **Key Concept — Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio**
> Distinct from LTC, LTV is the loan amount expressed as a percentage of the completed project value (the appraised value on completion). Lenders use both metrics — LTC ensures adequate equity is invested, LTV ensures the loan is secured against sufficient asset value. A project with a low LTC but a low projected completion value can still fail LTV underwriting.

> **Key Concept — Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)**
> The DSCR is the third primary underwriting metric and, for permanent financing, arguably the most important. It measures whether the stabilized property generates enough income to service its debt:
>
> **DSCR = Net Operating Income (NOI) ÷ Annual Debt Service**
>
> A DSCR of 1.25 means the property generates $1.25 of NOI for every $1.00 of debt service — a 25% cushion. Permanent lenders typically require a minimum DSCR of 1.20–1.40x. Construction lenders use DSCR to stress-test the project's exit into permanent financing — if stabilized NOI is insufficient to support the expected permanent loan amount, the construction loan may not be repaid in full at maturity.
>
> When underwriting a loan, lenders size the facility to the *lower* of the amount justified by LTC, LTV, and DSCR. A project can pass LTC and LTV tests but still receive a smaller permanent loan if stabilized NOI cannot support the debt service at the proposed loan amount. On the 1156 The Queensway project, the CMHC MLI Select permanent mortgage will be sized against stabilized NOI at lease-up — the DSCR test on that stabilized income may constrain the permanent loan size even within CMHC's high LTV ceiling.

**Worked Example — Reading a Capital Stack:** A 208-unit purpose-built rental project has a total project cost of $122,397,000 (the 1156 The Queensway project). The Bank of Montreal construction facility is $100,000,000, representing an LTC of 81.7%. The remaining $22,397,000 is funded through a combination of equity ($12,283,642) and deferred costs ($10,113,358 — primarily development charges deferred under the rental DC deferral program). This structure is more aggressive than a typical conventional deal because the CMHC insurance application provides additional lender comfort, and the DC deferral effectively acts as a form of subordinate financing that reduces the cash equity requirement.

## 3.3 Equity — Types, Sources, and Expectations

Equity is the risk capital in a development project. It is the last to be repaid and the first to absorb losses, which means equity investors demand a return that compensates for that risk. Understanding how equity is sourced, structured, and priced is essential for anyone working in development finance.

**The LP/GP Structure**

The most common equity structure in Canadian development is the Limited Partnership (LP) organized around a General Partner (GP) — the developer — and one or more Limited Partners who contribute capital.

The **General Partner** is the developer entity that manages the project. The GP typically contributes a smaller share of the total equity — often 10-20% — but retains significant economic upside through the waterfall structure. The GP earns a development management fee (typically 1-3% of total project cost) for managing the development, and participates in profits disproportionate to their capital contribution once investors receive their preferred return.

The **Limited Partners** are passive investors — they contribute capital but have no management role in the project. In exchange for taking on capital risk without operational control, they receive preferred treatment in the distribution waterfall: they get their capital back first, plus a preferred return (typically 8-12% per annum on unreturned capital), before the GP participates in profits.

> **Key Concept — The Waterfall**
> The waterfall is the agreed sequence in which profits are distributed between LP and GP. A typical structure looks like this:
> 1. Return of LP capital contributions (100 cents on the dollar)
> 2. LP preferred return (e.g., 8% per annum on unreturned capital from the date of contribution)
> 3. GP catch-up (the GP receives distributions until they have received a share proportionate to their promote — e.g., 20% of total profit)
> 4. Residual split (remaining profits split, e.g., 80% LP / 20% GP)
>
> The GP's disproportionate share of profits above the preferred return is called the **promote** or **carried interest**. It is the economic incentive that aligns the GP's interests with the LP's — the GP only earns the promote if the project performs well enough to clear the preferred return hurdle first.
>
> **Note — Tiered Hurdle Structures:** The four-step structure above is a common simplified baseline. Institutional joint ventures frequently use tiered or multi-hurdle waterfalls where the profit split adjusts as the project's IRR climbs. For example: 80/20 (LP/GP) up to a 15% IRR; 70/30 between 15% and 20% IRR; 50/50 above 20% IRR. Each threshold is called a "hurdle rate." Tiered structures are designed to more aggressively incentivize the GP as returns improve — the GP earns a larger share of profits only if they deliver strong performance above each hurdle. If you encounter an LP agreement on an institutional deal, expect a tiered structure rather than a single promote tier.

**LP Investor Expectations**

LP investors in development projects set return thresholds that move with the risk stage of the investment. As a general framework: fully entitled, income-producing assets with low execution risk may attract equity at 10-12% IRR; projects that are entitled but unbuilt typically need to offer 15%+ unleveraged or 20-25%+ leveraged IRR to attract capital; land plays with entitlement risk often require 20-30%+ leveraged IRR. On a stabilized, CMHC-exiting purpose-built rental project — where execution risk is lower than a speculative condo and the permanent financing path is well-defined — investors may accept targets toward the lower end of the development range, typically 12-18% IRR depending on deal structure and market conditions. These thresholds shift with interest rates, capital market conditions, and the availability of competing opportunities. The key principle is that equity investors demand a premium above both the risk-free rate and above what debt investors receive, proportional to the risk they are taking on.

LP investors also typically negotiate protective rights in the limited partnership agreement — rights to receive regular reporting, rights to approve major budget deviations, step-in rights if the GP fails to perform, and anti-dilution protections if additional capital is required.

**Common Problem — Equity Gap Discovery Mid-Project**

One of the most dangerous situations in development financing is discovering partway through construction that the project is running out of equity — that costs are running over budget, revenues are projected below expectations, or both. At that point, the developer must either contribute additional capital, find a new equity partner willing to invest at a distressed valuation, or face the lender exercising their remedies. This situation is almost always the result of insufficient equity cushion at the outset combined with inadequate contingency. The minimum equity cushion should be stress-tested against realistic downside scenarios before the project starts, not after construction begins.

## 3.4 Construction Debt — How Construction Loans Work

The construction loan is the primary funding instrument for most development projects. Understanding how it works mechanically — how it is sized, what conditions must be met to access it, how interest accrues, and when it gets repaid — is essential context for the draw process you manage.

**Sizing the Construction Loan**

The construction loan is sized based on the lender's underwriting of the project. The lender will review the feasibility study, the construction cost estimate (prepared by a quantity surveyor like Finnegan Marshall), the project schedule, the capital stack, and the developer's track record. The loan amount is the lower of: the lender's LTC limit applied to total project cost, or the lender's LTV limit applied to the appraised completion value.

**Conditions Precedent**

Before the lender advances the first dollar, a series of conditions precedent (CPs) must be satisfied. These are documented in the credit agreement and typically include:

- Evidence that the required equity has been invested (or committed)
- Executed construction management agreement
- Building permit issued (or conditional permit in some structures)
- Environmental reports acceptable to the lender
- Geotechnical report acceptable to the lender
- Insurance certificates meeting lender requirements
- Appraisal confirming completion value
- Project monitor engagement letter
- Partnership agreements and development agreements
- CMHC commitment letter (for CMHC-insured deals)

The conditions precedent checklist is a living document that the development team — including you at the senior analyst level — tracks and manages throughout the pre-construction period. Each CP has an owner, a deadline, and a status. The first draw cannot be advanced until all CPs are satisfied or formally waived by the lender.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Tracking the CP checklist is one of the most operationally important tasks in the pre-construction period. A delayed CP can delay the first draw, which delays the start of construction, which triggers carrying cost and schedule cascades. You will often be the person chasing consultants, lawyers, and internal team members to ensure CPs are resolved on time. Understanding what each CP requires — and why the lender needs it — makes you more effective at prioritizing and escalating.

**Interest Accrual and the Interest Reserve**

Construction loans accrue interest on the outstanding drawn balance from the date each draw is advanced. Unlike a conventional mortgage with scheduled monthly payments, construction loan interest is typically funded from the loan itself — meaning each month's accrued interest is added to the outstanding balance rather than paid in cash. This is called capitalized interest or interest reserve.

The total construction loan interest is estimated at the outset based on the projected draw schedule and the applicable interest rate. On the 1156 The Queensway project, the budgeted construction loan interest was $6,036,316 — a significant line item that represents the real cost of the time value of money during the construction period.

> **Key Concept — The Draw Schedule and Interest Sensitivity**
> Because interest accrues on the drawn balance, projects that draw funds faster — front-loaded draws — accumulate more interest than back-loaded ones. The draw schedule (the projected monthly funding requirements across the construction period) is a critical input into the interest reserve calculation. If the project draws faster than projected, the interest reserve may be insufficient. If the project is delayed and extends beyond the loan maturity date, the developer must either negotiate an extension (at additional cost) or arrange refinancing.

**Loan Maturity and Repayment**

Construction loans have a defined maturity date — typically 6-12 months after the projected construction completion date to allow time for lease-up (for rental projects) or unit closings (for condo projects). On the 1156 The Queensway project, the loan maturity was set as "the earlier of 36 months after the first advance of the Construction Facility or August 31, 2028."

At maturity, the construction loan must be repaid in full. For a purpose-built rental project, this typically happens through a permanent mortgage — either a conventional mortgage or a CMHC-insured mortgage. For a condominium project, it happens through the proceeds of unit closings. The permanent loan that retires the construction loan is called the **takeout** — construction lenders advance funds with the expectation that a permanent lender (or condo sale proceeds) will take them out at the end of the construction period. On CMHC-insured projects like 1156 The Queensway, the CMHC commitment letter is listed as a condition precedent specifically because it gives the construction lender confidence that a bankable takeout is in place before they advance the first dollar.

**Personal Recourse and Guarantees**

Construction loans on development projects are almost always full recourse to the developer (the GP entity) and frequently require a personal guarantee from the principal(s) of the GP. This means that if the project fails and the construction loan cannot be repaid from project proceeds, the lender can pursue the developer personally for any shortfall — not just the project assets.

Lenders typically require the developer to demonstrate net worth at least equal to the loan amount and liquid assets sufficient to meet potential obligations. In a rising market when lenders are competing for deals, recourse requirements may be relaxed for established developers. In stressed markets, requirements tighten: personal guarantees become near-universal and lenders may require additional collateral.

Most construction loans also contain **"bad-boy" carve-outs** — specific events that convert an otherwise non-recourse loan to full personal recourse. Standard carve-out triggers include fraud or misrepresentation, failure to pay property taxes, unauthorized transfers of the property, and intentional waste of the asset. Bad-boy carve-outs are not negotiable — they are standard lender protections against bad faith conduct.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> You will encounter recourse and guarantee provisions when reviewing credit agreements and preparing materials for LP investors. LP investors should understand that the GP has personal exposure on the construction loan — it is one of the reasons the GP is entitled to the promote. When reviewing loan documents, flag any recourse provisions, guarantee amounts, and carve-out triggers for the principal's attention. Errors or misstatements in draw packages or investor reports that a lender could characterize as misrepresentation can trigger bad-boy carve-outs — another reason accuracy in your work is not optional.

**Worked Example — Construction Loan Interest Calculation:** A project has a $100,000,000 construction facility with a 24-month construction period. The draw schedule is evenly distributed — roughly $4.17M drawn per month. The interest rate is 7.5% per annum. Average outstanding balance over the 24-month period is approximately $50,000,000 (half the total facility, since draws are gradual). Estimated interest = $50,000,000 x 7.5% x 2 years = **$7,500,000**. This is a simplified estimate — actual interest is calculated on the daily outstanding balance and will vary based on the actual draw timing. The lender's project monitor tracks and certifies the actual accrued interest with each draw.

## 3.5 CMHC Financing Programs

The Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) operates several financing programs that are relevant to purpose-built rental development. For developers building rental housing in Canada, CMHC programs can provide access to lower-cost, longer-term debt that meaningfully improves project economics — but they come with requirements and timelines that affect the development process.

**MLI Select**

MLI Select (Multi-Unit Mortgage Loan Insurance Select) is CMHC's flagship insurance program for purpose-built rental housing. It provides mortgage loan insurance on permanent financing for rental projects, which allows lenders to offer more favourable terms — lower interest rates, higher LTV ratios (up to 95% in some cases), and longer amortization periods (up to 50 years).

The program uses a points-based system that rewards developers for achieving outcomes in three categories: affordability (below-market rents), energy efficiency, and accessibility. Higher point scores unlock better financing terms. The most favourable tier requires a combination of deeply affordable units, high energy efficiency standards, and full accessibility compliance.

**Why MLI Select Matters for Project Economics**

The difference between CMHC-insured and conventional permanent financing on a large purpose-built rental project can be substantial. A conventional mortgage might carry a rate of 5.5-6.5% with a 25-year amortization and an LTV of 65-70%. An MLI Select mortgage might carry a rate of 4.5-5.5% with a 40-50 year amortization (up to 50 years for the highest-scoring projects) and an LTV of 85-95%. The combination of a lower rate, longer amortization, and higher proceeds significantly reduces annual debt service and increases the net cash flow available to equity — which improves both project viability and long-term hold returns.

One cost that partially offsets these benefits is the CMHC mortgage loan insurance premium, which is paid on the permanent mortgage and ranges from approximately 0.6% to 4.0% of the insured loan amount depending on LTV ratio and amortization period. On a $100M insured mortgage, the premium could be $2-4M — a real project cost that must be incorporated into the permanent financing model. The premium is typically added to the mortgage balance (i.e., the borrower does not pay it in cash at closing), but it increases the total debt outstanding and should be reflected in the project's return analysis.

**CMHC Co-Lending (Formerly Rental Construction Financing Initiative)**

CMHC's Co-Lending program provides direct loans to eligible purpose-built rental projects alongside a private lender. It is designed for projects that would not otherwise be financially viable, targeting affordability outcomes in exchange for below-market financing rates. The program has specific eligibility criteria and is subject to annual funding allocations.

**The CMHC Process and Timeline**

Obtaining CMHC financing requires a formal application and approval process that must be initiated early in the development timeline. The lender submits the application on behalf of the borrower, but the developer must provide extensive project documentation including the feasibility study, architectural drawings, construction cost estimate, market study, and evidence of the development team's experience. CMHC approval typically takes 3-6 months and must be in place before construction financing is advanced on a CMHC-insured deal.

On the 1156 The Queensway project, the CMHC MLI application was listed as a condition precedent — the lender required documentation from the borrower confirming that the permanent CMHC-insured mortgage had been committed before the construction loan was advanced.

> **Common Problem — CMHC Timeline Miscalculation**
> Developers new to CMHC programs sometimes underestimate how early the application process needs to begin. A CMHC approval that takes 5 months to obtain, when the developer only allowed 2 months, can delay the construction loan closing and push back the construction start — with cascading effects on the entire project timeline. CMHC requirements must be incorporated into the project schedule from the outset, not treated as a parallel process that will sort itself out.

## 3.6 Mezzanine Financing & Preferred Equity

Mezzanine debt and preferred equity occupy the middle of the capital stack between senior debt and common equity. They are used when the senior loan does not provide sufficient proceeds to close the equity gap, or when the developer wants to reduce the amount of expensive common equity required.

**Mezzanine Debt**

Mezzanine debt is a subordinate loan that sits behind the senior construction loan in priority. The mezzanine lender is repaid after the senior lender but before equity investors. Because it carries higher risk than senior debt, it commands a significantly higher interest rate — typically 10-15%+ per annum on development deals — and often includes equity participation features such as warrants or conversion rights.

Mezzanine lenders typically require an intercreditor agreement with the senior lender — a legal document that defines the relative rights of each lender, restricts the mezzanine lender's ability to enforce against the property while the senior loan is outstanding, and establishes cure rights that allow the mezzanine lender to remedy a senior loan default to protect their position. A key feature of most intercreditor agreements is the **standstill provision**: following a default, the mezzanine lender is prohibited from enforcing its remedies against the property for a defined period (often 90-180 days), giving the senior lender priority to act first. The standstill, combined with the subordinated repayment position, is a significant part of why mezzanine debt commands the premium rate it does — the mezzanine lender has real credit exposure with constrained enforcement rights.

**Preferred Equity**

Preferred equity is technically an equity investment rather than a debt instrument, but it behaves similarly to mezzanine debt in the capital stack. A preferred equity investor contributes capital in exchange for a preferred return (fixed rate, often 10-14%) and priority over common equity in distributions and capital return. Unlike mezzanine debt, preferred equity is not secured by a charge on the property — it is a contractual right within the partnership agreement.

**When Mezzanine and Preferred Equity Are Used**

These instruments are more common in markets or periods where senior debt is constrained — either because the lender's LTC limit leaves a significant equity gap, or because the developer wants to preserve common equity capacity for other projects. They add cost and complexity to the capital structure and should only be used when the incremental cost is justified by the project economics.

> **Common Problem — Over-Leveraging the Capital Stack**
> A project that is financed with senior debt at 70% LTC, mezzanine at 10%, and only 20% common equity has very little cushion to absorb cost overruns or revenue shortfalls. Every dollar of cost overrun must be funded from that 20% equity layer, and the combined interest cost of senior and mezzanine debt is substantial. Highly leveraged deals work in scenarios where everything goes to plan — but they are fragile. The 2022-2024 period saw a number of Canadian development projects stall precisely because over-leveraged capital stacks could not absorb the combination of cost escalation and revenue compression that occurred simultaneously.

## 3.7 The Draw Process — How Funds Flow During Construction

The monthly construction draw is the mechanism through which the construction loan is advanced to fund project costs as they are incurred. It is the operational heartbeat of a development project in construction — and it is one of your core responsibilities.

**The Draw Cycle**

The draw cycle repeats every month throughout the construction period. The mechanics are as follows:

**Step 1 — Cost incurrence and invoicing**
The construction manager, general contractor, and subcontractors perform work and submit invoices for payment. Each invoice relates to a specific line item in the project budget — a Division from the construction cost estimate, a soft cost category, or a deferred cost item. Invoices are collected, verified against contracts and purchase orders, and organized by budget line item.

**Step 2 — Draw package assembly**
The draw package is assembled by the development team — typically led at the senior analyst level. It is a formal document package submitted to the construction lender and the project monitor requesting the advance of funds. A standard draw package contains:

- A draw request summary showing the amount requested by budget category
- Updated budget-to-actual schedule (the cost report) showing costs incurred to date, current period costs, and cost to complete for every line item
- Copies of all invoices supporting the current period draw
- Statutory declarations from the contractor confirming no liens
- Lien waivers from subcontractors and suppliers
- HST summary (net HST on costs is typically funded by the lender and recovered from CRA)
- Updated cash flow projection
- Construction progress report from the construction manager
- Certificates from the architect, structural engineer, mechanical engineer, and electrical engineer confirming progress to date

**Step 3 — Project monitor review**
The lender's project monitor — an independent quantity surveyor like Finnegan Marshall — reviews the draw package. Their role is to verify that the work claimed has actually been completed, that the invoices are consistent with the budget and contract, that the cost to complete is adequately funded by the remaining loan availability and contingency, and that the project is progressing according to schedule. The project monitor issues a report to the lender recommending approval, partial approval, or requesting additional information.

**Step 4 — Lender review and approval**
The lender's internal team reviews the project monitor report and the draw package. They confirm that the conditions for the current advance are met — no outstanding defaults, insurance certificates current, no registered liens, equity invested as required. The lender issues a funding authorization.

**Step 5 — Fund disbursement**
Funds are advanced to the project operating account. The development team then processes payments to contractors and suppliers from the project account. In some structures, the lender advances directly to a trust account managed by the developer's lawyer, from which payments are disbursed. This entire cycle from draw submission to fund receipt typically takes 7-14 business days, though complex draws or those requiring additional documentation can take longer.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Assembling and coordinating the monthly draw package is one of your primary responsibilities. In practice this means: collecting invoices from the construction manager and all soft cost vendors, reconciling them against the approved budget, updating the cost report, preparing the draw request summary, chasing lien waivers and statutory declarations from the contractor, coordinating the consultant certificates, and packaging everything for submission to the project monitor. The quality of your draw package directly affects how quickly the lender advances funds — a well-organized, complete package moves through the approval process quickly; an incomplete or disorganized one generates questions, requires resubmission, and delays payment to contractors. Contractors who are not paid on time stop work, which delays construction.

**The Holdback**

Under Ontario's Construction Act, the project owner is required to withhold 10% of the value of all work performed as a holdback, until the lien period expires and the project achieves substantial completion. The holdback is not advanced to contractors — it is retained in the project account (or a trust account) as security for lien claims. On the 1156 The Queensway project, the gross holdback retained was $797,546 at February 28, 2026. The holdback is released to contractors in stages following substantial completion once the statutory lien period has expired. Under the Construction Act, the lien period is **45 days from the date of publication of the Certificate of Substantial Performance** in a construction trade newspaper. No holdback can be released while a registered lien claim is outstanding. As an analyst tracking holdback release on active projects, you will need to confirm both that substantial completion has been certified and that the 45-day period has elapsed without a registered lien before holdback funds can be disbursed.

**HST Management**

HST (Harmonized Sales Tax) on construction costs is a significant cash flow item on development projects. The developer pays HST on all taxable costs — construction, consulting fees, soft costs — and is entitled to recover it from the Canada Revenue Agency through the Input Tax Credit (ITC) mechanism. However, there is a timing gap between paying HST and recovering it. On most construction loans, the lender funds the HST on monthly payables as part of the draw, and the ITC recovery flows back to reduce the loan balance. On the 1156 The Queensway project, HST on monthly payables was budgeted at $9,820,544 with a corresponding ITC recovery of the same amount — a net neutral position, but a significant cash flow item that must be tracked and managed carefully.

**Worked Example — Monthly Draw Package:** It is month 6 of construction on a 180-unit rental building. The draw package for the current period includes: $1,847,000 in base construction costs certified by the construction manager, $82,000 in architect and engineering fees, $43,000 in project management fees, $125,000 in development management fee accrual, $18,000 in project monitor fees, and $268,450 in HST on the above costs. The total gross draw request is $2,383,450. Less the 10% construction holdback on the base construction amount ($184,700), the net draw request to the lender is $2,198,750. The project monitor reviews the certified construction costs against the schedule of values and confirms that the work claimed aligns with site observations. The lender advances $2,198,750 to the project account within 10 business days.

## 3.8 Investor Reporting & Capital Account Management

Alongside the draw process, regular investor reporting is the other primary financial management responsibility at the senior analyst level. LP investors have contributed capital to the project and have a contractual right to be informed about how that capital is being used and how the project is performing.

**What Investor Reports Contain**

Investor reports are typically issued monthly or quarterly depending on the LP agreement terms. A standard report for a construction-period project includes:

- **Project status summary** — Overall construction progress, key milestones achieved in the period, upcoming milestones, and any schedule changes.
- **Budget and cost report** — Updated budget-to-actual showing costs incurred to date vs. budget for every major category, current period spend, and cost to complete. Variance analysis explaining any significant deviations from budget.
- **Source and use of funds** — Summary of total capital invested to date (equity by investor, construction loan drawn) vs. total costs incurred, showing the project's current cash position.
- **Construction loan status** — Outstanding loan balance, amount drawn in the period, remaining availability, and any covenant compliance items.
- **Capital account summary** — Each LP investor's capital account showing their total committed capital, amounts called to date, and their pro-rata share of project costs and returns to date.
- **Distribution waterfall update** — A projection of how distributions will flow under the waterfall when the project is complete and sold or refinanced, updated for current budget and market assumptions.
- **Key risks and mitigations** — Transparent disclosure of any material risks identified during the period — cost overruns, schedule delays, market changes — and the mitigation measures being taken.

> **Key Concept — Capital Accounts**
> Each LP investor's capital account is a running ledger of their economic interest in the project. It starts at zero, increases as capital is called and invested, is reduced as distributions are made, and reflects the investor's share of project income and expenses on an accrual basis. The capital account is the basis on which the waterfall is calculated at the end of the project — which makes its accuracy critical. An error in capital account tracking can result in incorrect distributions, which creates legal liability and damages investor relationships.

**Capital Calls**

When the project needs equity capital — to fund costs before the construction loan draws are available, or to cover costs that exceed the loan availability — the GP issues a capital call to the LP investors. A capital call notice specifies the amount required from each LP (in proportion to their committed share), the purpose of the call, and the date by which funds must be wired to the project account.

LP investors typically have 10-15 business days to fund a capital call under the LP agreement. Failure to fund a capital call triggers default provisions — which may include penalties, dilution of the LP's interest, or forfeiture of their position depending on the agreement terms. Managing capital calls — timing them appropriately, giving LPs adequate notice, and tracking responses — is a key operational responsibility.

**Budget Variance Reporting**

Budget variance reporting is the discipline of identifying, explaining, and managing deviations between the approved project budget and actual costs. Every development project experiences variances — some positive (costs come in below budget), most eventually negative (costs creep above budget in some categories).

The key discipline is not avoiding variances — it is identifying them early, understanding their cause, and managing them within the overall budget using contingency appropriately. An unexplained variance is a red flag to both lenders and investors. A variance with a clear explanation and a credible plan to manage it within overall budget is manageable.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Budget variance reporting is something you do directly. In practice this means maintaining a live version of the project budget that is updated with actuals from each draw period, calculating variances against the approved budget for each line item, writing variance explanations for any items above a defined threshold (typically 5-10% of line item budget or a dollar threshold), and presenting the report to both the lender's project monitor and the LP investors. The discipline of doing this rigorously every month — not just when something goes wrong — is what allows the project team to catch cost creep early, before it becomes a crisis that requires an equity top-up or a lender conversation about budget amendments.

**Worked Example — Investor Report Variance Analysis:** It is month 9 of construction on a 208-unit purpose-built rental project. The approved hard construction budget was $72,680,839. Costs incurred to date are $13,572,936 against a projected-to-date of $12,100,000 — a variance of +$1,472,936 or +12.2% ahead of the draw schedule projection. However, the construction contingency of $3,096,712 remains fully intact. The project monitor confirms that the variance is timing-related — the construction manager accelerated some concrete work in the period to take advantage of favourable weather, pulling forward costs that were budgeted for months 10-11. The cost to complete projection has not changed. In the investor report, the variance is explained as a timing acceleration with no impact on total project cost, supported by the project monitor's certification.

## 3.9 Key Metrics & Terms Reference

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Capital Stack | The layered structure of debt and equity financing a development project, from senior debt at the bottom to common equity at the top. |
| LTC (Loan-to-Cost) | Senior loan amount as a percentage of total project cost. Lenders typically cap at 65-75% for conventional construction loans. |
| LTV (Loan-to-Value) | Senior loan amount as a percentage of the completed appraised value. A separate underwriting test from LTC. |
| Senior Debt | The first-priority loan, secured by a first charge on the property. Lowest risk, lowest cost, repaid first. |
| Mezzanine Debt | Subordinate loan sitting behind senior debt. Higher rate (10-15%+), repaid after senior lender. |
| Preferred Equity | Equity with priority return and capital recovery over common equity. Contractual rather than security-based. |
| GP (General Partner) | The developer — manages the project, contributes a minority of equity, earns a promote on profits. |
| LP (Limited Partner) | Passive capital investor — contributes majority of equity, receives preferred return, no management role. |
| Promote / Carried Interest | The GP's disproportionate share of profits above the LP preferred return hurdle. The developer's primary economic incentive. |
| Preferred Return | The LP's priority return on invested capital (typically 8-12% per annum) before the GP participates in profits. |
| Waterfall | The agreed sequence of distributions between LP and GP — return of capital, preferred return, GP catch-up, residual split. |
| Capital Call | A request from the GP to LPs to contribute additional equity to fund project costs. |
| Capital Account | Running ledger of each LP's economic interest in the project — contributions, distributions, and share of income/expense. |
| Conditions Precedent (CPs) | Requirements that must be satisfied before the lender advances construction loan funds. |
| Draw Package | The monthly submission to the lender requesting an advance of construction loan funds, supported by invoices, certifications, and reports. |
| Project Monitor | Independent quantity surveyor appointed by the lender to review draw packages and certify project progress. |
| Holdback | The 10% statutory retention under Ontario's Construction Act withheld from contractor payments until the lien period expires. |
| Interest Reserve | The portion of the construction loan allocated to fund accrued interest during the construction period. |
| MLI Select | CMHC's mortgage loan insurance program for purpose-built rental. Enables lower rates, higher LTV, and longer amortization on permanent financing. |
| ITC (Input Tax Credit) | The mechanism by which HST paid on project costs is recovered from the Canada Revenue Agency. |
| Budget Variance | The difference between actual costs and the approved budget for a given line item or category. |
| Substantial Completion | The point at which a project is sufficiently complete for its intended use, triggering the start of the statutory lien period and the holdback release process. |

## Applied Exercises

## AE-3.2 — Reading the Capital Stack: 1156 The Queensway

This exercise uses the actual project data from the 1156 The Queensway draw report to illustrate how a real capital stack is structured and what it means for the developer's actual cash requirements.

**Project Facts (from Report No. 6, February 2026)**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Total Project Budget | $122,397,000 |
| Construction Facility (BMO) | $100,000,000 |
| Equity | $12,283,642 |
| Deferred Costs | $10,113,358 |

**Step 1 — Calculate the LTC Ratio**

LTC = Construction Facility / Total Project Budget
LTC = $100,000,000 / $122,397,000 = **81.7%**

This is above the typical 65-75% LTC range for conventional construction loans. This elevated LTC is enabled by the CMHC MLI application, which provides the lender with insurance comfort that reduces their risk exposure.

**Step 2 — Understand the Deferred Costs**

The $10,113,358 in deferred costs is not cash equity — it is a form of subordinate financing provided by government deferral programs. The breakdown from the draw report:

| Deferred Cost Item | Amount |
|-------------------|--------|
| City Development Charges | $5,967,078 |
| DC Interest on Frozen Rates | $667,125 |
| Development Management Fee | $368,826 |
| Residential Leasing Commissions | $575,241 |
| Retail Tenant Inducement | $110,460 |
| Rate Buydown Reserve | $739,910 |
| Operating Costs & Realty Taxes | $1,192,948 |
| Asset Management Fee | $491,769 |
| **Total Deferred Costs** | **$10,113,358** |

The largest component — City Development Charges of $5,967,078 — is deferred under Toronto's rental housing DC deferral program, payable in six annual instalments after occupancy with interest accruing at Prime + 3%. This is effectively a 6-year subordinate loan from the municipality at a floating rate.

**Step 3 — Calculate the Developer's Actual Cash Equity**

| Source | Amount | % of Total Cost |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| BMO Construction Facility | $100,000,000 | 81.7% |
| Deferred Costs (non-cash) | $10,113,358 | 8.3% |
| Cash Equity | $12,283,642 | 10.0% |
| **Total** | **$122,397,000** | **100%** |

The developer's actual cash equity requirement is $12,283,642 — only 10% of total project cost. This is a highly efficient capital structure that maximizes leverage and minimizes the cash the LP investors need to contribute, which increases the potential equity return if the project performs as projected.

**Step 4 — The Risk of High Leverage**

A 10% cash equity cushion means the project can absorb a maximum cost overrun of approximately $12.3M before equity is wiped out — assuming the construction loan and deferred costs cannot be increased. On a $122M project, this is a 10% buffer. Given that construction contingency alone is $5,082,000 (4.2% of project cost), and the remaining cash equity buffer above contingency is only about $7.2M, any meaningful combination of cost overruns and revenue shortfalls could create a situation where additional equity is required. This is not a criticism of the deal structure — it is appropriate for a purpose-built rental project with CMHC support — but it illustrates why monitoring costs and maintaining budget discipline throughout construction is so critical.

Either way, the lender and project monitor need to know.

**Scenario — Cost Overrun in Mechanical**

## AE-3.8 — Investor Report Variance Analysis

This exercise prepares a variance analysis narrative for inclusion in a monthly investor report, covering both a timing variance (benign) and a genuine cost increase (requiring disclosure).

**Project Status at Month 9**

Total costs incurred to date: $41,949,066
Total costs projected to date per original cash flow: $39,200,000
Variance: +$2,749,066 (+7.0% ahead of projected spend)

**Variance 1 — Construction Timing (Benign)**

*Category:* Base Construction Costs
*Budget:* $65,760,579
*Projected to date:* $10,500,000
*Actual to date:* $11,946,981
*Variance:* +$1,446,981

*Explanation for investor report:*
"Construction costs incurred to date are $1,446,981 ahead of the original cash flow projection, representing a timing acceleration rather than a cost increase. The construction manager accelerated structural concrete pours in months 7-9 to take advantage of favourable weather conditions and trade availability, pulling forward work originally scheduled for months 11-13. The total construction budget and cost-to-complete projection are unchanged. The project monitor has certified costs to date and confirmed no change to the total hard cost budget. The earlier spend will reduce draw requirements in months 11-13."

**Variance 2 — Mechanical Change Order (Genuine Cost Increase)**

*Category:* Mechanical (Division 15)
*Budget:* $24,099,575
*Amended budget:* $24,479,575
*Variance:* +$380,000

*Explanation for investor report:*
"The mechanical budget has been increased by $380,000 to reflect an approved change order for upgraded geothermal heat pump units in 54 suites. This upgrade was requested by the ownership group following a design review in month 8 to improve long-term operating efficiency and meet enhanced energy performance targets. The $380,000 has been absorbed from the construction contingency reserve, which now stands at $2,716,712 (4.1% of remaining construction cost). The project monitor has reviewed and approved the change order. Total project budget remains $122,397,000. Management considers the remaining contingency adequate for the current stage of construction."

**Key Principles Illustrated:**

1. Timing variances and genuine cost increases require different disclosures — conflating them misleads investors
2. Every variance should be explained with specificity — not "costs are running slightly over" but exactly which category, exactly why, and exactly what the impact on total budget is
3. Contingency drawdown must be disclosed and remaining coverage confirmed — investors need to know how much buffer remains
4. The investor report is prepared pursuant to reporting obligations in the LP agreement. Inaccuracies — particularly regarding budget variances, capital account balances, and distribution calculations — create legal and fiduciary liability for the GP. Where LP interests were sold pursuant to an offering memorandum, material misstatements in investor reports can also engage securities law obligations. Accuracy and completeness are not optional.

## Chapter Summary

Development financing is not a background consideration — it is the mechanism that determines whether a project gets built at all, and the framework within which every construction-period decision is made. The capital stack determines how much risk the developer carries and how returns are shared with investors. The construction loan terms determine how funds flow and what obligations the developer must meet every month. The draw process is the operational engine that keeps money moving to contractors and the project moving forward. And investor reporting is the communication discipline that maintains the trust of the capital partners whose money is funding the project.

For a Senior Analyst whose responsibilities include draw packages, investor reporting, and budget variance tracking, this chapter is not theoretical — it is the framework within which your daily work exists. Understanding the full picture — from how the capital stack was structured to why the holdback exists to what the waterfall means for your investors' returns — is what allows you to do your specific job with real understanding rather than mechanical execution.
