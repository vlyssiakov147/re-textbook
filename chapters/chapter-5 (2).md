# Chapter 5: Project Team Assembly

A development project is not built by a developer — it is built by a team. The developer's role is to originate the opportunity, assemble the capital, secure the approvals, and manage the process from start to finish. Everything in between — designing the building, managing the construction, obtaining the legal clearances, navigating the financing, and executing the entitlements — is done by specialized professionals who the developer selects, contracts, and coordinates.

The quality of the team is one of the most significant determinants of project outcomes. A mediocre site with an excellent team will consistently outperform an excellent site with a mediocre team. The right architect produces a design that wins approvals faster and leases up at better rents. The right construction manager keeps costs within budget and the schedule on track. The right planning consultant reads the political landscape and structures an application that builds rather than erodes municipal support. Team assembly is a strategic decision, not an administrative one.

This chapter covers who is on the development team, what each member does, how they are selected, and how their work is contracted. It is calibrated to a Construction Management delivery model, which is the approach your firm uses.

---

## 5.1 Why Team Assembly Matters

The consequences of a poor team selection decision compound over the life of a project. A construction manager who wins the job on a low fee but lacks the site supervision capacity to manage a complex high-rise will fall behind schedule in months 6 through 12, generating cost overruns that dwarf the initial fee savings. An architect who is talented but inexperienced in the specific municipality will produce a design that triggers avoidable planning objections and extends the entitlement timeline by 12 months. A planning consultant with weak relationships at a politically contentious municipality will fail to read the signals that an experienced consultant would catch early enough to adjust the strategy.

These are not hypothetical risks — they are the kinds of failures that experienced developers have lived through and that drive their emphasis on team quality over cost minimization at the selection stage.

**The Team Evolves Through the Project Lifecycle**

The project team is not assembled all at once. Different professionals are brought on at different stages depending on when their work is needed:

- **Pre-acquisition:** Planning consultant, architect (feasibility level), legal counsel, cost consultant
- **Post-acquisition, pre-application:** Full design team, traffic consultant, environmental consultant, geotechnical engineer, civil engineer
- **Entitlement period:** Planning consultant leads, full technical study team active
- **Pre-construction:** Construction manager, structural engineer deeper engagement, M&E engineers, interior designer, specifications consultant
- **Construction period:** Construction manager active, project monitor engaged, lender relationship active
- **Lease-up / closeout:** Leasing team, property manager, legal counsel for closing

Understanding this staging is important because it affects when consultant costs are incurred and how they flow through the project budget. A consultant brought on too early is paying fees before their work can be usefully applied. One brought on too late creates a bottleneck that holds up downstream work.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Your most direct team interactions are with the Construction Manager — from whom you collect monthly invoices, certified progress reports, and contractor declarations that form the core of the draw package — and with the Project Monitor, who reviews your draw submissions and whose report to the lender gates the advance of funds. Both relationships are operational and recurring, and the quality of those interactions has a direct effect on how smoothly monthly draws are processed. Understanding what each party needs from you — and why — is covered in depth in Section 5.3 and 5.6.

## 5.2 The Developer's Internal Team

Before any external consultant is hired, the developer must have the internal capacity to manage them. The internal development team is the hub through which all external consultants are coordinated, and whose judgment ultimately determines whether the project succeeds.

**Principal / Managing Partner**

The senior decision-maker on the project. Sets strategy, manages investor relationships, leads negotiations on land acquisition and financing, and makes the key go/no-go decisions throughout the project. On smaller development firms this person may also carry the VP of Development role.

**VP of Development / Director of Development**

Leads the day-to-day management of the entitlement and pre-construction process. Manages the planning consultant and architect relationship, attends planning meetings and community consultations, drives the design development process, and interfaces with the municipality. Responsible for the entitlement timeline and budget.

**Project Manager**

Manages the construction period execution — the construction schedule, budget, change orders, draw process, and interface with the construction manager. The project manager is the primary internal owner of the monthly draw cycle and is the construction manager's main point of contact within the developer organization.

**Senior Analyst / Analyst**

Supports the financial modelling, draw package preparation, investor reporting, budget variance tracking, and general financial administration of the project. The Senior Analyst role sits at the intersection of the project team and the financial team — close enough to the construction and procurement activity to understand what is driving costs, and responsible for communicating that information accurately to lenders and investors.

**Accounting / Finance**

Manages project banking, processes payments from the project account, tracks HST, manages capital calls, and maintains the LP capital accounts. On smaller development firms the Senior Analyst role overlaps significantly with project accounting.

> **Common Problem — Understaffed Internal Teams**
> Development firms that grow quickly sometimes expand their project pipeline faster than their internal team capacity. An internal project manager managing four simultaneous projects cannot give adequate attention to any of them. Draw packages get delayed. Change orders go unreviewed. Budget variances are not caught early. The cost of understaffing the internal team is always higher than the cost of hiring the right people — it just manifests more slowly and less visibly.

## 5.3 The Construction Manager — Your Most Important External Relationship During Construction

On a Construction Management (CM) delivery model, the Construction Manager is the developer's primary field partner throughout the construction period. Understanding what the CM does, how they are paid, and how their performance affects the draw process is directly relevant to your daily work.

**What is Construction Management?**

In a Construction Management model, the CM does not take a fixed-price lump sum risk on the project. Instead, they manage the construction process on behalf of the developer for a fee — typically expressed as a percentage of total construction cost (5-6% is common for high-rise residential in the GTA). The CM's fee covers their project management, site supervision, scheduling, procurement coordination, quality management, and reporting services.

Under this model, all trade contracts are entered into directly by the developer (or by the CM as agent for the developer), and all trade costs flow through to the developer at actual cost — hence "open book." The CM's financial exposure is limited to their fee; if trade costs exceed the budget, that exposure falls on the developer and their contingency, not the CM.

**Why Developers Use CM vs. Fixed Price**

The CM model is the dominant delivery approach for high-rise residential development in the GTA for several reasons. It gives the developer full transparency into actual costs — there is no markup hidden in a lump sum price. It allows the design to continue evolving during the construction period without the cost and friction of change order negotiations that would occur under a fixed-price contract. And it gives the developer direct control over trade selection and procurement, which allows them to leverage existing relationships and optimize for both cost and quality.

The tradeoff is that the developer carries the cost risk. A fixed-price GC takes on the risk of trade cost escalation; a CM does not. This is why cost monitoring — the draw package, budget variance reporting, and contingency management that you work on — is so critical under a CM model. The developer has no financial backstop other than their own contingency and budget discipline.

> **Key Concept — The CM Fee and What it Covers**
> The CM fee is typically calculated as a percentage of the total construction cost, excluding the CM fee itself. On the 9700 Yonge Street project, the construction cost estimate excludes the CM fee entirely — it is noted as "EXCLUDED" at Division 0 and carried separately in the proforma. On the 1156 The Queensway project, the CM fee is included as a line item in the project budget at approximately 5.5% of hard construction cost. Understanding whether the CM fee is inside or outside the hard cost estimate matters for LTC calculations, draw budget reconciliation, and investor reporting.

**The CM's Monthly Responsibilities — What You Receive from Them**

Each draw cycle, the CM provides the development team with the documentation that forms the backbone of the draw package:

- **Monthly progress certificate** — The CM's formal certification of the value of work completed to date across all trade contracts and the CM's own fee. This is the primary document that the project monitor reviews to verify the draw request.
- **Schedule of values** — A breakdown of the total construction contract by trade and line item, showing the original budget, work completed to date, current period work, and balance to complete. This is the construction-side equivalent of the overall project cost report.
- **Change order log** — A running record of all approved and pending change orders, showing their impact on the approved construction budget.
- **Construction schedule update** — Updated project schedule showing progress against the baseline, any schedule variances, and the projected substantial completion date.
- **Statutory declarations** — Formal legal declarations from the CM confirming that all subcontractors and suppliers have been paid for the previous period and that there are no outstanding liens. Required before each draw can be advanced under the Ontario Construction Act.
- **Lien waivers** — Signed documents from major subcontractors confirming they have been paid and waiving their lien rights for the amounts paid.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Collecting and organizing these documents from the CM is a core part of assembling the monthly draw package. In practice, this means following up with the CM's project coordinator in the days before the draw submission deadline, reviewing the progress certificate against the budget to identify any unexpected variances, flagging change orders that haven't been formally approved, and ensuring the statutory declarations cover the correct period and signatories. The quality and completeness of what the CM provides directly determines how much additional work you need to do to prepare the package — a well-organized CM makes your job substantially easier.

**Evaluating CM Performance**

The developer's internal team — primarily the Project Manager — monitors the CM's performance on an ongoing basis. The metrics that matter are: schedule adherence (is the project tracking to the baseline?), cost control (is the CM managing subcontractor costs within budget?), change order discipline (is the CM identifying and pricing change orders accurately and promptly?), and reporting quality (are the monthly documents accurate, complete, and on time?).

A CM who consistently submits late or incomplete draw documentation, who is slow to price change orders, or whose schedule reports consistently understate delays is creating downstream problems for the developer — delayed draws, lender friction, and investor reporting gaps that ultimately reflect on the development team.

## 5.4 The Architect and Design Consultants

The architect is the lead design professional on the project and the coordinator of the full design team. Their work begins at the feasibility stage — sometimes even before site acquisition — and continues through to construction completion and occupancy.

**The Architect's Scope**

The architect's scope of work is typically broken into stages that correspond to the project lifecycle:

1. **Feasibility / Concept Design** — Initial massing studies, program testing, and site layout to support the acquisition decision and early planning strategy.
2. **Schematic Design** — Development of the design concept to a level sufficient for the planning application package — floor plans, elevations, sections, and renderings.
3. **Design Development** — Refining the design in response to planning comments and advancing it toward construction-level detail.
4. **Construction Documents** — Full working drawings and specifications from which the building will be constructed. These are the documents that form the basis of the trade contracts.
5. **Construction Administration** — During construction, the architect reviews shop drawings, responds to requests for information (RFIs), conducts site reviews, and issues certificates of payment that align with the CM's progress certifications.

The architect also coordinates the structural, mechanical, and electrical engineers — who are typically sub-consultants to the architect rather than directly contracted to the developer — and the interior designer, landscape architect, and other specialists.

**The Architect's Fee**

Architectural fees on large residential projects are typically negotiated as a percentage of construction cost or as a lump sum based on an estimated scope. On a 200-unit purpose-built rental project, total architectural fees — including the sub-consultants coordinated through the architect — might range from $1,500,000 to $2,500,000 depending on project complexity and the firm's market positioning.

> **Common Problem — Scope Creep in Design Contracts**
> Architectural contracts specify a defined number of design iterations, submission rounds, and site review visits. When the planning process requires more resubmissions than anticipated, or when the client requests significant design changes after construction documents are complete, the additional work is legitimately out of scope and subject to additional fees. Developers who do not track design contract scope carefully can find themselves with significant unbudgeted architectural fees on complex or contentious projects.

**Sub-Consultants on the Design Team**

The following sub-consultants are typically part of the design team on a major residential project:

| Consultant | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Structural Engineer | Foundation design, concrete structure, shoring and dewatering specifications |
| Mechanical Engineer | HVAC, plumbing, fire protection design and specifications |
| Electrical Engineer | Power distribution, lighting, fire alarm, communications design |
| Geotechnical Engineer | Soil investigation, foundation recommendations, dewatering design |
| Civil / Servicing Engineer | Site servicing, grading, stormwater management |
| Landscape Architect | Landscape design, public realm, amenity areas |
| Interior Designer | Suite finishes, common area design, material specifications |
| Code Consultant | Ontario Building Code compliance review |
| Specifications Consultant | Technical specification writing for trade contracts |

## 5.5 The Legal Team

The developer's legal counsel is engaged at virtually every stage of the development process. Legal work on a large development project covers a wide range of specialized practice areas, and developers typically work with law firms that have dedicated real estate development groups.

**Land Acquisition**

Real estate legal counsel manages the purchase agreement negotiation, title review, due diligence coordination, and closing of the land acquisition. They review the vendor's representations and warranties, advise on deal structure, and manage the transfer of title.

**Corporate and Partnership Structure**

The legal team structures the LP/GP partnership, drafts the limited partnership agreement, and prepares the subscription documents through which LP investors commit their capital. The LP agreement — which governs the waterfall, capital call rights, LP protective provisions, and GP obligations — is one of the most important legal documents in the project and must be carefully drafted to reflect the agreed commercial terms.

**Financing**

Legal counsel negotiates and reviews the construction loan agreement, intercreditor agreements, and any CMHC-related documentation. They manage the conditions precedent checklist, coordinate title insurance, and manage the closing of the financing.

**Entitlements**

Planning lawyers advise on the planning application strategy, draft section 37 or CBC agreements, review conditions of approval, and manage OLT proceedings if the application is appealed.

**Construction Contracts**

The CM agreement and major trade contracts are reviewed and negotiated by legal counsel with construction law expertise. The CM agreement in particular — which defines the CM's scope, fee, performance obligations, and termination rights — is a critical document that must be carefully structured to protect the developer's interests.

## 5.6 The Financial and Lending Team — Your Primary External Interface

From a Senior Analyst perspective, the financial and lending team members are the most directly relevant external relationships. Two in particular affect your day-to-day work: the construction lender and the project monitor.

**The Construction Lender**

The construction lender — typically a Canadian chartered bank like BMO, TD, RBC, or Scotia, or a CMHC-approved lender — provides the senior debt that funds the majority of construction costs. The lender's primary interface with the developer during the construction period is through the draw process. Each month, the lender reviews the draw package, the project monitor report, and confirms that all conditions for the advance are met before releasing funds.

The lender also monitors the project through covenant compliance — the developer must maintain certain financial ratios and project metrics (LTC, cost to complete coverage, insurance) throughout the construction period. Breaches of loan covenants can trigger default provisions, so tracking covenant compliance is an ongoing obligation.

**The Project Monitor**

The project monitor is an independent quantity surveyor appointed by the lender — on your projects, Finnegan Marshall serves this role — to provide monthly independent certification of project progress and cost. The project monitor is the lender's eyes and ears on the project; their report is what gives the lender confidence to advance funds each month.

The project monitor's monthly report typically covers:

- Verification that work claimed in the draw package has actually been completed on site
- Review of the CM's progress certificate and schedule of values for reasonableness
- Assessment of the cost to complete against the remaining loan availability and contingency
- Comment on any budget variances, change orders, or schedule deviations
- A recommendation to the lender to approve, partially approve, or hold the draw pending additional information

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> Your relationship with the project monitor is one of the most operationally important external relationships you manage. In practice, it works like this: you submit the draw package to the project monitor, they review it and come back with questions or requests for additional documentation, you respond and provide the requested items, and they issue their report to the lender recommending approval. The faster and more completely you respond to project monitor queries, the faster the draw is approved and funds are advanced. Developing a working relationship with the project monitor team — understanding what they look for, anticipating their questions before they ask them, organizing the draw package so it is easy for them to review — is one of the most practical skills you can develop at this stage of your career. The Finnegan Marshall team you work with on 9700 Yonge and 1156 The Queensway are experienced at this process — they have a clear methodology and clear expectations, and meeting those expectations consistently is what keeps draws moving smoothly.

**The Cost Consultant / Quantity Surveyor (Developer-Side)**

Separate from the lender's project monitor, developers sometimes engage their own independent quantity surveyor to prepare and update the construction cost estimate throughout the project. This is the role Finnegan Marshall played in preparing the 9700 Yonge Street construction cost estimate — providing the developer with an independent, detailed cost picture that informs the budget, the financing structure, and the lender's underwriting.

**The CMHC Correspondent**

On CMHC-insured deals, the lender typically has a designated CMHC correspondent — a team member who manages the CMHC application process, tracks conditions, and coordinates between the developer, the lender, and CMHC. Understanding the CMHC timeline and requirements is the correspondent's responsibility, but the developer team needs to be responsive to information requests.

## 5.7 The Planning and Entitlement Team

The planning and entitlement team is responsible for navigating the approvals process described in Chapter 4. At the senior analyst level your exposure to this team is limited, but understanding who is on it and what they do provides important context for how the pre-construction period works.

**Planning Consultant**

The planning consultant — typically a registered professional planner (RPP) at a firm specializing in development approvals — leads the entitlement strategy and manages the municipal planning process. They prepare or supervise the preparation of the Planning Justification Report, manage the pre-consultation process, attend planning committee meetings, interface with municipal planning staff, and advise on community engagement strategy.

The planning consultant's value is a combination of technical planning knowledge and municipal relationship capital. An experienced consultant who has worked extensively in a specific municipality understands the planning department's priorities, knows the staff, and can read political signals that a less experienced firm would miss.

**Urban Designer**

Works closely with the architect to develop the Urban Design Brief and to ensure the project's massing, character, and public realm interface are positioned to win planning support. On complex or high-profile projects, the urban designer may be a separate engagement from the architect; on others, the architect performs this function.

**Transportation Consultant**

Prepares the Traffic Impact Study and, where required, the Transportation Demand Management plan. Interfaces with the municipal transportation engineering department during the review process. On projects near transit nodes or on arterial roads, the transportation study is typically a major item of scrutiny.

**Environmental and Geotechnical Consultants**

As covered in Chapter 2, these consultants are engaged during due diligence and carry their work forward into the planning and construction periods. The geotechnical consultant's dewatering and shoring designs are key inputs into the construction documentation.

## 5.8 Procurement — How Consultants and Contractors are Selected

Selecting the right team members through a rigorous procurement process is how developers manage team quality risk. The approach varies depending on the type of engagement, the relationship history between the developer and the firm, and the strategic importance of the role.

**Sole Source Selection**

For relationships where the developer has an established track record with a specific firm — a trusted planning consultant, an architect who has successfully designed multiple projects for the firm, a construction manager who has built their last three projects — the developer may engage that firm directly without a competitive process. This is called sole source selection.

Sole source selection is appropriate when the relationship value — established trust, shared project history, proven performance — outweighs the potential cost savings of a competitive process. It is most defensible for highly relationship-dependent roles like planning consultants and architects, and less defensible for commodity-like services where fee variation is significant and performance is easier to benchmark.

**Request for Proposal (RFP)**

For new relationships or for roles where the developer wants to test the market, a formal Request for Proposal process is used. The RFP is issued to a shortlist of qualified firms — typically 3-5 — and requests a written proposal covering the firm's understanding of the project, their proposed approach and team, their relevant experience, their proposed fee, and their availability.

A well-structured RFP evaluation considers several dimensions beyond fee: relevant project experience, proposed team quality, approach and methodology, reference checks, and cultural fit with the developer organization. Selecting purely on the basis of lowest fee is a reliable way to assemble a mediocre team.

> **Key Concept — The CM RFP Process**
> The Construction Manager RFP is one of the most consequential procurement decisions on a development project. The CM will manage hundreds of millions of dollars of construction work and will be the developer's primary field partner for 2-4 years. A typical CM RFP for a major high-rise project asks for: the proposed project team (superintendent, project manager, site coordinator), the CM's experience on comparable projects, their proposed preconstruction services scope, their fee proposal (percentage of construction cost), their general conditions budget proposal, their proposed insurance coverage, and their approach to schedule management, cost control, and reporting.

**Prequalification**

For construction trade contracts — the subtrades managed by the CM — a prequalification process is often used to establish a qualified bidder list before the formal tender. Prequalification screens trades on the basis of financial capacity, bonding capacity, relevant experience, safety record, and reference checks. Only prequalified firms are invited to bid.

**Competitive Tender**

Trade contracts under the CM model are typically procured through competitive tender — multiple qualified firms submit priced bids on a defined scope of work, and the contract is awarded to the lowest compliant bidder. The CM manages this process, reviews the bids, and makes a recommendation to the developer. On significant trade packages (concrete, mechanical, electrical), the developer may want to review the bid results and the CM's recommendation before the contract is awarded.

**Worked Example — Evaluating a CM Proposal:** Your firm is reviewing two CM proposals for a 350-unit rental tower. Firm A proposes a CM fee of 5.0% on a projected construction cost of $130,000,000 — a fee of $6,500,000 — with a general conditions budget of $11,200,000 and a proposed superintendent with 18 years of high-rise residential experience. Firm B proposes a CM fee of 4.5% — a fee of $5,850,000, saving $650,000 — with a general conditions budget of $10,400,000, but their proposed superintendent has 7 years of experience, primarily on mid-rise wood frame construction. The $650,000 fee saving on Firm B represents 0.5% of the construction cost. A single schedule delay of 3 months caused by inadequate site supervision would cost approximately $1,875,000 in additional construction loan interest alone — nearly three times the fee saving. Experience and team quality, not fee minimization, should drive the selection.

## 5.9 Contracts and Scope of Work

Every team member relationship is governed by a contract. Understanding the key elements of the contracts that frame your working relationships — particularly the CM agreement — helps you understand what obligations each party has and what recourse exists when performance falls short.

**The CM Agreement**

The Construction Management Agreement defines the legal relationship between the developer and the CM. Key provisions include:

- **Scope of services** — What the CM is responsible for, including preconstruction services (estimating, scheduling, procurement planning, subcontractor prequalification) and construction services (site management, trade coordination, quality management, reporting).
- **Fee** — The CM's compensation, typically expressed as a percentage of total construction cost. Whether the fee applies to the CM's own general conditions costs is an important point of negotiation — some agreements include general conditions in the fee basis, others exclude them.
- **General conditions** — The costs of running the site office — superintendent salaries, site trailer, temporary utilities, safety equipment, hoisting — which are funded separately from the CM fee as direct project costs.
- **Performance obligations** — Schedule adherence, reporting requirements, change order management procedures, and quality management obligations.
- **Change order authority** — The dollar threshold below which the CM can approve changes without developer authorization, and the process for approvals above that threshold.
- **Termination** — Grounds on which the developer can terminate the CM (for cause and for convenience) and the process and consequences of termination.

**CCDC 5A and CCDC 5B — The Two CM Contract Forms**

The Canadian Construction Documents Committee (CCDC) publishes two standard Construction Management contract forms that differ fundamentally in how risk is allocated between the developer and the CM.

**CCDC 5A — CM as Agent**

CCDC 5A is the standard form for Construction Management where the CM acts as the developer's agent. Under this form, all trade contracts are between the developer and the trade contractors directly — the CM manages the trades on the developer's behalf but does not hold the contracts. The developer has full contractual privity with every trade and bears all trade cost risk. This is the most common CM contract form used on GTA high-rise residential projects, and the open-book cost transparency it provides is one of its primary advantages. You will see CCDC 5A referenced in construction documentation, draw reports, and lender conditions on most projects your firm works on.

**CCDC 5B — CM as Constructor**

CCDC 5B is used when the CM takes on a more contractor-like role — holding the trade contracts directly rather than as the developer's agent. Under this form, the trade contracts are between the CM and the subtrades, not between the developer and the subtrades. The developer contracts only with the CM, who in turn contracts with and manages the trades.

The key differences from 5A are:

| Item | CCDC 5A (CM as Agent) | CCDC 5B (CM as Constructor) |
|------|----------------------|----------------------------|
| Trade contracts held by | Developer | CM |
| Developer privity with trades | Direct | Indirect — through CM |
| CM cost risk | None — fees only | Limited — CM may carry some cost risk on trade packages |
| Lien exposure | Developer directly exposed | CM is the contracting party — developer has one step of insulation |
| Transparency | Full open-book | Open-book but CM has more control over trade pricing |
| Common use case | Large complex projects where developer wants full control | Projects where developer wants a single point of responsibility |

**When CCDC 5B is Used**

CCDC 5B is chosen when the developer wants a single point of contractual responsibility for the construction work — one party to hold accountable for trade performance rather than managing direct relationships with dozens of trade contractors. It is also sometimes used when the developer has less internal construction management capacity and wants the CM to carry more of the day-to-day trade contract administration burden.

The lien exposure difference is particularly relevant in Ontario. Under CCDC 5A, trade contractors and suppliers have direct lien rights against the owner's interest in the property. Under CCDC 5B, the CM is the contracting party with the trades, which provides the developer one additional layer of separation from direct trade lien claims — though this does not eliminate lien risk entirely since the Construction Act still provides mechanisms for trades to reach the owner's interest.

> **Common Problem — Assuming 5A and 5B are Interchangeable**
> The choice between CCDC 5A and 5B is a strategic decision that affects the entire risk and accountability structure of the construction period. Switching from 5A to 5B mid-project, or selecting the wrong form for the project context, creates ambiguity about who holds the trade contracts and who is responsible for trade performance. This matters acutely in a dispute scenario — if a trade contractor stops work or submits a lien claim, who they have a contract with determines who they can pursue and through what mechanism. The CM agreement form should be selected deliberately, with legal counsel involved, before the project reaches the procurement stage.

> **Key Concept — Why the Contract Scope Matters for Draw Administration**
> The CM's reporting obligations under the CM agreement — what documents they must produce, how frequently, and to what standard — are what you rely on every draw cycle. If the CM agreement does not specifically require monthly statutory declarations, or does not define the format and timing of the progress certificate, the CM has no contractual obligation to provide them on your timeline. In practice, experienced development teams negotiate detailed reporting requirements into the CM agreement precisely because the draw cycle depends on them. When you review the CM agreement early in a project, pay attention to Section covering reporting — those provisions define what you can ask for and when.

**Consultant Agreements**

Consultant agreements — with the architect, engineers, and planning consultants — define scope, fee, schedule, and deliverables. Key provisions relevant to project administration include:

- **Deliverables and milestones** — What the consultant must produce and when. Tied to fee payment schedules.
- **Reimbursable expenses** — Travel, printing, permit fees, and other out-of-pocket costs that are reimbursed on top of the base fee.
- **Additional services** — Scope changes that are legitimately outside the original agreement and subject to additional fees. Understanding what is in scope vs. additional services prevents disputes.
- **Professional liability (E&O) insurance** — The consultant's errors and omissions insurance coverage, which the developer relies on in the event of a design error that causes loss.

## 5.11 Regulatory Licensing — HCRA and Tarion

Before a developer can legally build and sell new homes in Ontario, the firm and its principals must hold the appropriate licences issued by the Home Construction Regulatory Authority (HCRA). This is not a planning approval or a construction permit — it is a business licence that governs who is legally permitted to operate as a builder or vendor of new homes in the province. It belongs in a chapter on team assembly because it is a foundational credential that the development firm must have in place before any project involving new home sales can proceed.

**The Home Construction Regulatory Authority (HCRA)**

The HCRA is the regulatory body established under the New Home Construction Licensing Act, 2017 (NHCLA) to license and regulate builders and vendors of new homes in Ontario. It replaced the previous self-regulatory structure and gave the province direct oversight over the new home construction industry.

Two distinct licences are required to develop and sell new condominiums or freehold homes in Ontario:

**Builder Licence** — Required to construct new homes. The builder licence is held by the entity that physically constructs the building — in most cases the development company or a related entity. The licence requires the applicant to demonstrate financial solvency, provide details of the proposed officers and directors, and confirm that key personnel meet the HCRA's experience requirements.

**Vendor Licence** — Required to sell new homes to purchasers. The vendor licence is held by the entity that enters into agreements of purchase and sale with condo buyers. On many development projects, the builder and vendor are the same entity or related entities, but both licences must be held.

> **Key Concept — Licence Conditions and Principal Disclosure**
> HCRA licences are not issued to anonymous companies — they require disclosure of the principals (officers and directors) of the applicant entity. Each principal is individually assessed for suitability, including their history with prior development projects, any prior regulatory findings, and their financial background. A principal with a history of project failures, unresolved Tarion claims, or regulatory violations at a prior development firm may affect the new firm's ability to obtain or renew its licence. This is why the composition of the development firm's principals is not just a governance matter — it has direct regulatory implications.

**Licence Renewal and Ongoing Compliance**

HCRA licences must be renewed annually. Renewal requires submission of updated financial information, disclosure of any changes in principals, and confirmation of ongoing compliance with the NHCLA. The HCRA also conducts audits and investigations, and has the power to impose conditions on licences, suspend licences, or revoke them for non-compliance.

> **Common Problem — Letting Licences Lapse**
> A lapsed builder or vendor licence is not a minor administrative issue — it means the development firm is legally prohibited from constructing or selling new homes until the licence is reinstated. On an active project with purchase agreements outstanding, a lapsed vendor licence creates significant legal exposure with purchasers and potential Tarion complications. Licence renewal dates should be tracked as a standing item in the firm's compliance calendar.

**Tarion — New Home Warranty Enrolment**

Tarion administers the statutory new home warranty program in Ontario under the Ontario New Home Warranties Plan Act. Every new home sold in Ontario — including every condominium unit — must be enrolled with Tarion before an agreement of purchase and sale can be executed with a purchaser.

Tarion enrolment involves registering the project with Tarion, paying per-unit enrolment fees, and ensuring the development meets Tarion's construction standards throughout the build. In exchange, purchasers receive statutory warranty coverage: one year on defects in work and materials, two years on electrical, plumbing, heating, and building envelope systems, and seven years on major structural defects.

Tarion also administers deposit protection for condo purchasers — deposits paid on new condo purchases are protected up to defined limits in the event the builder fails to complete the project or return deposits.

> **In Practice — Senior Analyst Perspective**
> HCRA licensing and Tarion enrolment sit outside the financial administration work you do day-to-day — they are managed by the firm's principal and legal counsel. However, understanding that these licences exist and what they cover helps you understand the firm's regulatory context. If you are ever asked to compile financial information for an HCRA renewal submission or to track Tarion enrolment fees in the project budget, knowing what those items are and why they exist makes you more effective in that task. Tarion enrolment fees are a real project cost that should appear as a line item in the soft cost budget — on a 200-unit condo project, they can amount to $200,000-$400,000 depending on unit sizes and the applicable fee schedule.

The full treatment of the Tarion warranty process, condominium registration under the Condominium Act, the HCRA's role at occupancy, and the interim occupancy period is covered in Chapter 12 — Certificate of Occupancy & Closeout.

**Proposal Comparison**

| Item | Firm A | Firm B |
|------|--------|--------|
| CM Fee | 5.0% | 4.5% |
| CM Fee ($) | $6,500,000 | $5,850,000 |
| General Conditions Budget | $11,200,000 | $10,400,000 |
| Total CM Cost (fee + gen conditions) | $17,700,000 | $16,250,000 |
| Proposed Superintendent | 18 yrs high-rise residential | 7 yrs mid-rise wood frame |

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|

| Total cost saving — Firm B | $1,450,000 |
| As % of total construction cost | 1.1% |

**Risk-Adjusted Cost of Choosing Firm B**

The $1,450,000 saving must be weighed against the risk of underperformance from a less experienced team. Consider three scenarios:

| Risk Scenario | Probability | Cost Impact | Expected Value |
|---------------|------------|------------|----------------|
| No performance issue | 40% | $0 | $0 |

Select Firm A. The 18-year superintendent and proven track record on comparable high-rise projects reduces the probability of the schedule and cost scenarios that create the most significant financial exposure. The fee premium of $1,450,000 over Firm B represents 1.1% of construction cost — a reasonable price for experienced, proven site leadership on a $130M project with a $110M construction loan where a 3-month delay costs nearly $2M in interest alone.

## AE-5.9 — Reading a CM Contract Scope: Identifying Gaps

This exercise presents a simplified CM agreement scope of services and asks you to identify what is missing or ambiguous that could create problems during the draw cycle.

**Scope Extract — CM Agreement, Article 3: Services During Construction**

*"The Construction Manager shall provide general management and supervision of the Work, coordinate the activities of trade contractors, maintain a competent site superintendent on site during normal working hours, and provide monthly progress reports to the Owner. The CM shall assist the Owner in preparing draw requests to the construction lender and shall provide such documentation as the lender may reasonably require."*

**Identified Gaps**

| Gap | Why it Matters |
|-----|---------------|
| "Monthly progress reports" is undefined | Does not specify format, content, or submission deadline. The CM can satisfy this obligation with a one-page narrative — insufficient for a draw package. |
| "Normal working hours" for superintendent | Does not define what normal working hours means, whether the superintendent must be exclusively assigned to this project, or what happens when the superintendent is absent. |
| "Assist in preparing draw requests" is vague | Does not specify the CM's obligation to provide progress certificates, schedule of values, statutory declarations, lien waivers, or change order logs on a defined timeline. |
| No mention of statutory declarations | A draw cannot be advanced without the CM's statutory declaration under the Ontario Construction Act. Its absence from the scope means the CM has no explicit obligation to provide it. |
| No change order management procedure | Does not specify the CM's obligation to identify, price, and present change orders within a defined timeframe, or the developer's approval threshold. |
| No schedule baseline requirement | Does not require the CM to produce a CPM schedule as a baseline, making it impossible to measure schedule variance objectively. |
| No reporting deadline specified | "Monthly" could mean anytime within the calendar month — the draw cycle requires documentation by a specific date to meet lender submission deadlines. |

**Recommended Additional Language**

The CM agreement should be amended to include:

- A monthly reporting package specification defining exact document types, formats, and submission deadline (e.g., the 15th of each month)
- An explicit obligation to provide progress certificates in a defined format, statutory declarations, lien waivers from trades over a defined threshold, and change order logs
- A CPM schedule requirement with monthly update obligation
- A change order management procedure with defined pricing timelines and developer approval thresholds
- A superintendent exclusivity provision confirming the proposed superintendent is dedicated to this project

## Chapter Summary

The development team is the developer's primary tool for converting a site and a capital stack into a completed building. Getting team selection right — choosing the construction manager with the right experience, the planning consultant with the right municipal relationships, the architect with the right design sensibility for the product type — has more impact on project outcomes than almost any other decision in the development process. For a Senior Analyst whose daily work involves the construction manager and the project monitor, understanding what those parties are obligated to provide, why they provide it, and what the consequences are when they fall short gives you the context to do your own work more effectively and to identify problems before they escalate into project issues. Team assembly is not glamorous work — it does not involve creative design decisions or bold financial structuring — but it is foundational to everything that follows. And before any of it begins on a condo project, the regulatory foundation must be in place: the HCRA licences that authorize the firm to build and sell, and the Tarion enrolments that protect the buyers who will eventually purchase what the team builds.
